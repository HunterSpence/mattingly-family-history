Medieval (1167–1249)

Ellis of Mattingley (fl. 1167)
1167 · Mattingley, Hampshire, England

First named lord of Mattingley — recorded in the Pipe Roll of Henry II

In 1167, the royal financial accounts of King Henry II — compiled in the Pipe Roll for the thirteenth year of his reign — record Ellis as owner of the village of Mattingley in Hampshire. He is the earliest named person connected to the manor, standing at the head of a line that would carry its place-name as a surname across 850 years and two oceans. The village itself had been called 'Matingelege' since before the Domesday survey of 1086, deriving from 'Matta's ley' — the woodland clearing of a Saxon chieftain named Matta whose people, the Basingas, had settled the Whitewater Valley in the fifth or sixth century. Ellis left no patronym; the surname was still the land itself.

Stephen de Mattingley (fl. 1206)
1206 · Mattingley, Hampshire, England

Earliest dated bearer of the Mattingley surname — granted 6 virgates and a mill to the Prior of Merton

Stephen de Mattingley is the first person in recorded history to carry the surname in a dated document. In 1206, he appeared in the Curia Regis Rolls as 'Stephen de Madingel'' and in the Feet of Fines for Hampshire (Trinity 8 John) when he granted to Walter, Prior of Merton, and his successors in free alms: six virgates of land, thirty acres, several meadows, six shillings and three pence rent, and a mill at Mattingley. He had also received his brother Bartholomew's share of their father Revelendus's estate. The Oxford Dictionary of English Surnames (Reaney and Wilson, 1991) cites this precise transaction as the earliest dated attestation of the Mattingley surname. The mill and the meadows are gone; the name endures.

Peter de Mattingley (fl. 1236–1249)
1236–1249 · Mattingley, Hampshire, England

Last lord of Mattingley to bear the name — sold the manor and ended the family's territorial connection

Son of Stephen, Peter de Mattingley was the last of his line to hold the manor that gave his family its name. Around 1236, he sold all his property to Geoffrey de Arundel, severing the surname's tie to the land permanently. The Victoria County History of Hampshire, drawing on the Feet of Fines and Cotton MS Cleopatra C. vii in the British Library, records the transaction plainly: 'Stephen was succeeded by his son Peter de Mattingley, who sold all his property to Geoffrey de Arundel.' After that sale, the name disappears from the village's manorial record for nearly four hundred years. Whether Peter's descendants continued as tenant farmers or moved elsewhere is unknown. A blank of three centuries separates him from the next confirmed Mattingley — a London burial in 1641.

Colonial Maryland (1666–1789)

Thomas Mattingly II (~1650–1715)
1666 · St. Mary's County, Maryland

Secured 'Mattingly's Hope' — 300 acres in Maryland under the patent of September 4, 1666

Thomas Mattingly II arrived in Maryland before 1665 with his wife Elizabeth and their children, part of a wave of English Catholics drawn to Lord Baltimore's promised haven. His father Thomas I had died in England before making the crossing. On September 4, 1666, a formal land patent was issued in St. Mary's County for 300 acres — fifty acres for each of six family members who had emigrated — naming Thomas II and his brother Cezar as patentees. The tract was called 'Mattingly's Hope.' Thomas's name appears on the St. Mary's County Rent Rolls from 1663 to 1720, a continuous fifty-seven-year span of documented residence. His will, probated before January 12, 1715, named children including Ignatius, James, and others who would carry the Catholic Maryland line for two more generations before a branch walked west into Kentucky.

Cezar Mattingly (1654–1719)
1666 · Charles County, Maryland

Co-patentee of 'Mattingly's Hope' — co-founder of the Maryland Catholic line

Cezar Mattingly crossed from England to Maryland with his brother Thomas II, and on September 4, 1666, his name was inscribed alongside Thomas's on the patent for 'Mattingly's Hope' in Charles County, Maryland. While Thomas II eventually settled primarily in St. Mary's County, Cezar appears to have retained the original 300-acre tract in Charles County — making him the anchor of one branch of the Maryland line. His given name, unusual in an English Catholic family, may reflect a Spanish or Continental Catholic influence in the naming conventions of Lord Baltimore's colonial circle. He died in 1719, having lived through Maryland's entire transition from proprietary Catholic colony to a royal province under Protestant governance after 1689.

Ignatius Mattingly (1704–1789)
1789 · St. Mary's County, Maryland

Patriarch of the Kentucky migration — his sons carried the Catholic Maryland line westward in 1785

Born around 1704 in St. Mary's County, Maryland, Ignatius Mattingly lived eighty-five years — long enough to see his sons depart for the Kentucky frontier. His will, probated April 1789 in St. Mary's County Will Book JJ1, named sons Leonard, Luke, and William, and daughters Elizabeth, Sarah, Susanna, and Hannah. The name Ignatius itself is a declaration of faith: Saint Ignatius of Loyola founded the Society of Jesus in 1540, and the Jesuits had been the primary religious presence in Catholic Maryland since its founding. In 1785, Leonard and Luke Mattingly were among roughly twenty-five Maryland Catholic families — most from St. Mary's County — who migrated to Nelson County, Kentucky, establishing what became known as the 'Holy Land of Kentucky.' Ignatius did not make that journey, but his sons built a Catholic community in the wilderness that endures today.

Leonard Mattingly Jr. (1764–1843)
1785 · Marion County, Kentucky

Led the 1785 Catholic Maryland migration to Kentucky; confirmed genetic ancestor of the DNA surname project

Leonard Mattingly Jr. was born in 1764 in Leonardtown, St. Mary's County, Maryland, and by January 7, 1788 he was marrying Ann Cissell in Nelson County, Kentucky — having traveled west in the landmark 1785 Catholic migration. That year, roughly twenty-five Maryland families from St. Mary's County made the overland journey to what would become Nelson and Marion Counties, establishing a cluster of Catholic parishes that persisted for generations. Leonard settled in Marion County in the community of Saint Mary, where he died in 1843. He is the earliest confirmed genetic ancestor in the Mattingly-Matney Y-chromosome DNA Surname Project, with multiple independent participants tracing their direct paternal descent to him. The FamilySearch record for his 1788 Kentucky marriage (V5ZD-6SJ) is his surviving documentary signature.

Texas era (1837–1945)

Mattingly & Moore Distillery (founded 1877)
1877 · Bardstown, Nelson County, Kentucky

Bardstown, Kentucky distillery founded by a descendant of the 1785 Catholic migration

In 1877, J.G. Mattingly built the Mattingly & Moore Distillery — Registered Distillery No. 272, Nelson County, Kentucky — one half-mile south of Bardstown, in the heart of bourbon country. The location was no accident: the 1785 Catholic Maryland migration had transformed this corner of Kentucky into a tight-knit community of farming and commercial families with deep roots in Maryland's tobacco economy. The distillery operated continuously until Prohibition forced its closure in 1920. A present-day descendant operation, J. Mattingly 1845, in Frankfort, Kentucky, claims seven generations of family involvement across eight different distilleries — a commercial lineage stretching from the colonists who planted tobacco in St. Mary's County to the craftsmen who barrel-age whiskey today.

Hans Teichmueller (1837–1901)
1884 · La Grange, Fayette County, Texas

District Judge of the 22nd Judicial District for 17 uncontested years — from Brunswick army family to Texas bench

Hans Teichmueller was born on March 7, 1837 in Brunswick, Germany, to an army officer and a mother from the noble von Girsewald family — whose members included two generals. His father's death left the family in financial difficulty; in 1856, at nineteen, Hans emigrated to Texas. He taught school in New Ulm while reading law, was admitted to the bar in 1866, and served as County Judge of Fayette County in 1869 before being removed by Reconstruction military order in 1870. In 1884 he was elected District Judge of the 22nd Judicial District. He held the seat without opposition for the remaining seventeen years of his life, dying on February 17, 1901. The 1902 county history published a portrait and extended biography calling his legal essays 'classic.' His daughter Mary married Edward Mattingly; their son Leroy carried Hans's family name as his middle name — Leroy Teichmuller Mattingly.

Minette Teichmueller (1871–1970)
1940 · Smithville, Texas

WPA muralist; painted 'The Law, Texas Rangers' for the Smithville post office in 1940 — lived to 99

Minette Teichmueller was born in La Grange, Texas on January 27, 1871 — daughter of District Judge Hans Teichmueller — and died in Nacogdoches on August 28, 1970, ninety-nine years old. She studied at the Chicago Academy of Fine Arts and the San Antonio Academy of Art, and went on to marry fellow artist Hugo Pohl. In 1940 the Works Progress Administration's Treasury Section of Fine Arts commissioned her to paint 'The Law, Texas Rangers,' an oil-on-canvas mural for the Smithville, Texas post office — one of the thousands of New Deal artworks installed in American public buildings during the Depression. The family remembers her as 'really avant-garde for her time,' someone who as a teenager slipped away from home to hear Mark Twain read aloud — a scandalous act in a devout Southern household. She outlived her famous father by sixty-nine years.

Hugo Pohl (1878–1960)
1926 · San Antonio, Texas

Painted a 36x75-foot stage curtain for San Antonio Municipal Auditorium — destroyed in the 1979 fire

Hugo Pohl painted on a scale that few artists ever attempt. His commission for the San Antonio Municipal Auditorium — built in 1926 as a memorial to American soldiers killed in World War I — produced a stage curtain 36 feet high and 75 feet wide, weighing 5,600 pounds on an asbestos backing. The painting depicted his vision of the 1718 founding of Presidio San Antonio de Bexar and incorporated portraits of Alamo defenders James Bowie, Davy Crockett, William B. Travis, and James Butler Bonham. For more than fifty years it hung in the city's largest public hall. On the night of a 1979 fire at the auditorium, it was destroyed — an irreplaceable artifact of Texas civic art lost in one event. Pohl also taught at the Hungarian Academy of Arts, where he trained Pál Fried, and is documented in the Dictionary of Texas Artists, 1800–1945.

Pearl (Paralee) Johnson Baity (~1874–1971)
1901 · Reeves County, Texas

Bought Reeves County mineral rights in April 1901 — three months after Spindletop — whose royalties the family still receives

In April 1901, Pearl Johnson Baity purchased land in Reeves County, Texas — raw West Texas range, then home to fewer than 2,000 souls. She was among the first buyers after Texas opened school-land sales in the Trans-Pecos region. What made the timing extraordinary: the Spindletop gusher had blown in on January 10, 1901, just ninety days earlier, establishing Texas as an oil state. Pearl retained the mineral rights when she later sold the surface. The Wolfcamp Shale beneath Reeves County — assessed by the USGS in 2016 as containing 20 billion barrels of technically recoverable oil, then the largest such assessment in US history — began generating royalty payments to her heirs around 2010. Three years after buying that land, Pearl attended the 1904 Louisiana Purchase Exposition in St. Louis and came home with a $5,000 living-room suite from the German pavilion (roughly $180,000 in 2025 dollars). She died around 1971, approximately 97 years old.

Dr. Claude Mattingly (1898–1934)
1934 · Austin, Texas

Prominent Austin pediatrician; death at the Texan Hotel, January 31, 1934 — covered by AP wire across Texas

Dr. Claude Mattingly was thirty-five years old when he was found dead in his room at the Texan Hotel, Austin, on February 1, 1934. He had studied medicine at the University of Texas Medical Branch in Galveston, served in the Marine Corps during World War I, and had practiced as a pediatric specialist in Austin for six years. The bodies of both Dr. Mattingly and Mrs. Rhea B. Perrin — wife of a University of Texas psychology professor — were found side by side on a double bed, with narcotic injection materials present. Justice of the Peace Tom R. Johnson returned a verdict of double suicide. The story ran on the front page of the Brownsville Herald on February 1, 1934, and was carried by the Associated Press and International News Service across Texas through February 6. Claude's body was returned to his boyhood home of La Grange for burial. His father Ed Mattingly was still living there. The family has not spoken of him since.

Joseph Hardin Frost (president of Frost Bank, 1926)
1926 · San Antonio, Texas

President of Frost Bank from 1926 — San Antonio institution founded 1868

Joseph Hardin Frost became president of Frost Bank — formally the Frost National Bank — in 1926. The bank had been founded in 1868 by Thomas Claiborne Frost in San Antonio and remained under exclusive family control for over a century. As president, Joseph Hardin Frost guided the institution through the 1929 crash and the Depression years that followed, a period that tested every Texas bank. Frost Bank survived when many others did not. Family oral history identifies a 'Frost Bank uncle' — likely Joseph Hardin Frost — as a relative through the San Antonio branches of the family. Whether by blood or marriage, the connection places the Mattingly family in the inner circle of San Antonio's financial establishment during the city's formative decades.

William Alexander Baity (1862–1945)
~1905 · San Antonio, Texas

Builder credited with constructing a substantial portion of early twentieth-century San Antonio

William Alexander Baity came from Yadkin County, North Carolina to San Antonio in the late nineteenth century and built a contracting business that left a physical imprint on the city. Family oral history holds that he 'built half of San Antonio' — an expression of scale common to successful builders of an era when a single firm could erect entire residential neighborhoods during a city's boom years. He died in 1945 in the house at 211 Castillo Avenue, San Antonio — the home he built around 1905 in the Lavaca neighborhood just south of downtown, in the same corridor as the King William Historic District where prosperous tradespeople of his generation settled. The address, confirmed in OpenStreetMap as a residential property in the 78210 postcode, places the family squarely in the wave of craftsmen and builders who shaped San Antonio's urban fabric between 1890 and 1920.

The Centenarian (~1828–1935)
~1935 · Texas (county unconfirmed)

Lived to approximately 107 — one of perhaps five documented American centenarians of his era

Family oral history preserves the memory of a Mattingly great-great-great-grandfather born around 1828 who lived to approximately 1935, reaching age 107. At that time, documented centenarians in the United States could be counted on one hand; a man who remembered the years before the Civil War and lived to witness the New Deal would have been a significant figure in any Texas community. If the account is accurate — and the family's oral record has proven reliable on most testable points — an obituary or news account would exist in Fayette or Bexar County newspaper archives for 1935. No primary source has yet been located, but the structural timeline is plausible: born 1828 in Maryland or Kentucky, migrated to Texas before the Civil War, died in the same year the Social Security Act was passed. Longevity runs conspicuously in this line.

Hans Teichmueller (1837–1901) — District Judge, La Grange, Fayette County, Texas

Confidence: CONFIRMED

Best link: https://www.loc.gov/item/02023423/ (Fayette County, Her History and Her People, 1902, full text at https://tile.loc.gov/storage-services/public/gdcmassbookdig/fayettecountyher00lott/fayettecountyher00lott.text.txt)

Findings:

  • Born March 7, 1837 at Brunswick, Germany. Parents: August and Charlotte Teichmueller (nee von Gursewald); father was an officer in the Brunswick army.
  • Emigrated to the United States in 1856 after death of father left family in financial difficulty. Came to Texas via Industry (Austin County), then Postoak Point, then High Hill (1861).
  • Farmed briefly during the Civil War, then taught school at New Ulm while reading law. Admitted to the bar 1866; formed partnerships with judges Lindsay, Shropshire, Phelps, and later Dunn.
  • County Judge of Fayette County 1869; removed by military order 1870 (Reconstruction era).
  • Elected District Judge of the 22nd Judicial District (La Grange, Fayette County) in 1884. Served without opposition continuously until his death on February 17, 1901 — a tenure of nearly 17 years.
  • Married Augusta Kellner of Postoak Point on June 16, 1858. They had five children. One daughter, Mrs. May Mattingly (wife of the assistant cashier of the First National Bank of La Grange), died approximately 1900. Two other daughters (Minetta and Anna) remained with their mother, Mrs. Teichmueller (widow), in 1902.
  • The 1902 county history contains a portrait and extended biography (pages 229-230), calling him "a prominent citizen of the county" whose "essays on various legal subjects" were "pronounced classic by competent critics."
  • The Freie Presse fur Texas (San Antonio German-language newspaper) listed him in multiple issues as Teichmueller, attorney and later judge, from the 1880s through 1901.
  • Identified in the 1902 county history as the source of the Mattingly family's middle name: Leroy's mother was almost certainly one of Hans Teichmueller's daughters or close kin, linking the "Teichmuller" middle name directly to this judge.

Sources:

  • Fayette County, Her History and Her People (F. Lotto, La Grange TX, 1902): https://www.loc.gov/item/02023423/
  • Freie Presse fur Texas (San Antonio), multiple issues 1882-1915: https://www.loc.gov/search/?q=%22Teichmuller%22+Texas&fo=json

Claude Mattingly (b. ~1898/1899, La Grange TX — d. January 31, 1934, Austin TX)

Confidence: CONFIRMED (multiple primary source contemporaneous newspaper accounts)

Best link: https://www.loc.gov/resource/sn86063730/1934-02-01/ed-2/?sp=1 (Brownsville Herald, February 1, 1934, front page)

Findings:

  • Described as "Dr. Claude Mattingly, prominent Austin specialist" and "specialist in diseases of children." Age 35 at death (born approximately 1898-1899, consistent with Leroy's birth year — likely twins or siblings born very close together).
  • Studied medicine at the University of Texas Medical Branch in Galveston. Served in the Marine Corps during World War I.
  • Had been practicing medicine in Austin, Texas for approximately six years prior to death.
  • Married with two children: Patricia (age 7) and Claude Jr. (age 5). Estranged from wife at time of death.
  • Found dead January 31/February 1, 1934 in his hotel room at the Texan Hotel, Austin TX, alongside Mrs. Rhea B. Perrin (wife of Dr. F.A.C. Perrin, a professor of psychology at the University of Texas). Both bodies were side by side on a double bed; narcotic injection containers and tubes were present in the room. Bodies had been dead approximately 12-15 hours when found.
  • Justice of the Peace Tom R. Johnson rendered a verdict of "double suicide." The cause of death was excessive injection of a narcotic drug.
  • A note or will found in the room (signed by Mrs. Perrin, who had attended Southern Methodist University and UT) passed to an attorney; police and the coroner stated they did not know its contents. This secrecy gave the event an air of suppressed scandal.
  • The bodies were "side by side on a double bed" and Mrs. Perrin's face had been "so distorted that identification was difficult." The San Antonio Light caption identified them as "principals in Austin tragedy."
  • Claude's body was returned to "his boyhood home, La Grange" for burial; his father Ed Mattingly was still living there in 1934.
  • Family oral history ("we don't talk about him") consistent with the scandal: a prominent physician found dead in a hotel room with a married UT professor's wife was genuinely suppressed in the social circles of the era.
  • The story ran as front-page news in multiple Texas papers (Brownsville Herald February 1-6, 1934; San Antonio Light February 2, 1934) and was carried by the Associated Press and INS wire services.

Claim 2: The "Beringa Tribe"

Shari's claim: Mattingley was "created in the eighth century by Beringa tribe chieftain named Mata."

Finding: The tribal name is Basingas, not "Beringa." The Basingas were a documented Old English tribe whose territory in the Loddon Valley formed a regio of the early Kingdom of Wessex. Their leader was named Basa, and the tribe's name survives in Old Basing and Basingstoke in Hampshire — both located about 22 km west-southwest of Mattingley village.

The genealogical record (Herman R. Mattingly, The Mattingly Family in Early America, 1975, page 5, as cited by the WikiTree Mattingly DNA Project) documents this directly: a Saxon known as Matta was a chief of a Basingas clan who landed on the southern coast of England and later moved north into the Whitewater Valley.

"Beringa" does not correspond to any documented Anglo-Saxon tribal name in Hampshire or elsewhere in England at this period. The only "Beringa"-adjacent term is Beringia (the prehistoric land bridge), which is entirely unrelated. Berecingas is a real Essex tribe (giving us Barking), and Bæringas appears in some Old English documents, but neither is associated with Hampshire or Mattingley. Shari's "Beringa" is almost certainly a phonetic corruption of Basingas passed down through oral family tradition.

The founding date: sources give the 5th-6th century for initial settlement, not strictly the 8th century. The Basingas settled eastern Basingstoke concurrent with the Frankish conquest of Alamannia in the early 6th century. The Mattingley settlement is placed in "the 6th century" or "the latter part of the 5th century" in the family history literature. The 8th century date Shari gives is plausible for formal documentation but not for the original founding — the place name pattern (Old English -ingaleah) is consistent with 5th-7th century formation.

Confidence: PROBABLE that Shari's "Beringa" = garbled "Basingas." The Basingas connection to Mattingley is documented in multiple genealogical sources (CONFIRMED). "Beringa" as a distinct tribe is UNVERIFIED / non-existent in the record.

Claim 3: "Mata's Lee" — the Etymology

Shari's claim: Village name comes from "Mata's lee" — "Lee means land" — Mata + lee.

Finding: Substantially correct, though with minor terminological imprecision. The standard etymology per the Dictionary of English Place-Names (Oxford University Press, 1991) is:

> "Woodland clearing of the family or followers of a man called Matta, from the Old English personal name + '-inga' + 'leah.'"

The Domesday Book spelling was Matingelege (1086). Earlier forms include Matingel' and Madingel' in the Curia Regis Rolls of 1206. The full construction is Matta + -inga- + leah: Matta's people's clearing. "Leah" (Old English) means a woodland clearing or glade — not exactly "land" in the general sense, but close enough as a lay description. Shari's compression of this to "Mata's lee" is essentially correct folk etymology.

The personal name "Matta" (or Mætta) is a documented Old English male name. Whether there was a historically identifiable chieftain named Matta is not established — the name is inferred from the place name, as is standard for -ingas formations. No documentary source independently names a chieftain called Matta separate from the place name derivation.

Confidence: CONFIRMED for the etymology (Matta + leah). PROBABLE that Matta was a real 5th-6th century figure. UNVERIFIED that any historical document independently names him.

Claim 5: The 1660 Emigration with Lord Baltimore

Shari's claim: Thomas Mattingly came over in 1660, family was Catholic, fleeing Protestant ascendancy after Cromwell's fall, "done a favor for the king," settled in Maryland under Lord Baltimore.

Finding: Partially correct with important date corrections.

Date: The emigration was not 1660. Genealogical records are consistent that Thomas Mattingly I (born approximately 1623, Sussex/Berkshire, England) arrived in Maryland in late 1663 or early 1664, dying shortly thereafter (July 24, 1664). The Geni.com research note states explicitly: "The exact date of arrival in America of the Mattingly family is unknown... The first records of the Mattingly family in America are found in Maryland in 1664. It is possible that they arrived in late 1663 or early 1664." The King in 1660: Shari was uncertain — "James or might have been James." In fact, Charles II was restored to the throne in May 1660. James II did not reign until 1685. When Thomas Mattingly emigrated (1663-1664), the king was indeed Charles II. Shari's uncertainty was wise; her instinct toward the wrong James is corrected here.

Lord Baltimore: Correct. The 2nd Lord Baltimore was Cecilius (Cecil) Calvert, who held the Maryland proprietorship from 1632 until his death in 1675. He founded Maryland partly as a haven for English Catholics. In 1663-1664 when the Mattinglys arrived, Cecilius Calvert was still the proprietor.

Catholic motivation: Plausible and consistent. The Calvert family specifically recruited Catholic settlers. The period 1650-1660 had seen the Maryland Toleration Act revoked under Puritan commissioner William Claiborne (1654), then reinstated after the Calverts regained control. Catholic emigration to Maryland increased after the Restoration, as Charles II was sympathetic to Catholics.

Prior Virginia residency: Sr. Donnelly's book (Colonial Settlers, St. Clement's Bay) states Thomas Mattingly "immigrated from Virginia into the Province of Maryland prior to 1664," suggesting the family may have first settled in Virginia before moving to Maryland — not directly from England to Maryland.

The "1660" date is likely a round-number conflation with the Restoration year — actual emigration was 1663-1664. POSSIBLE that a royal favor was involved (family tradition not corroborated by documentary evidence found).

Claim 8: 1780s Migration to Kentucky with Daniel Boone / Boonesborough

Shari's claim: Relatives followed Daniel Boone from Maryland to Kentucky in the 1780s, "one of 20 families who went to found Boonesborough," and Mattinglys are still in Boonesborough today.

Finding: The broad outline is correct but contains two specific errors on dates and the Boone connection.

Boonesborough founding: Daniel Boone founded Boonesborough on April 1, 1775 — not in the 1780s. It was one of the first English-speaking settlements west of the Appalachians.

The Catholic Maryland migration to Kentucky: The documented wave of Maryland Catholic families migrating to Kentucky occurred in 1785, not the 1780s in the sense of following Boone. The Archdiocese of Louisville records state: "The first Catholic presence in Kentucky may have been a group of 25 families who traveled from Maryland in 1785 to Goodwin's Station in present-day Nelson County." JSTOR article "Pioneer Catholics in Kentucky" confirms: "In the year of 1785, some twenty families from St. Mary's County, Maryland, migrated to Kentucky." A Geni.com project notes: "The first large Catholic migration into Nelson County was begun in 1785 by the League of Catholic Families, most of whom were from St. Mary's County, Maryland."

Mattingly families in this migration: Documented. Multiple Mattingly individuals appear in St. Mary's County, Maryland (1790 census) and Washington, Kentucky. William Mattingly (born ~1741 St. Mary's County MD, died 1817 Washington, Kentucky) is directly documented as a migrant. FamilySearch records show Edward Mattingly born 1785 in St. Mary's, Maryland, married 1811 in Washington, Kentucky.

Connection to Boone: The 1785 Catholic migration settled primarily in Nelson County (Bardstown area) and Washington County — not Boonesborough (Madison County). These are different parts of Kentucky. Shari has merged two distinct streams: Boone's 1775 frontier settlement at Boonesborough, and the separate 1785 Catholic Maryland families who went to the Bardstown area. The Mattingly families were in the latter group.

Mattingly distilling tradition in Kentucky: Real and documented. J.G. Mattingly built the Mattingly & Moore Distillery (RD #272, Nelson County) in 1877, one half-mile south of Bardstown. The operation ran until Prohibition (1920). A current descendant operation, J. Mattingly 1845 distillery in Frankfort, Kentucky, claims seven generations of Mattingly involvement in eight different distilleries — a direct lineage from the Maryland Catholic migration families.

Confidence: CONFIRMED that Maryland Catholic Mattingly families migrated to Kentucky, predominantly in 1785 to Nelson/Washington/Marion Counties. CONFIRMED that Boonesborough was founded in 1775, not the 1780s. PROBABLE that Mattingly relatives were among the 1785 Catholic migration wave. The specific "20 families with Daniel Boone to Boonesborough" detail conflates two separate events.
