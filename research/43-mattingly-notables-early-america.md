# Mattingly Colonial / Early-America Notables and Collateral Lines

**Compiled:** 2026-04-26
**Scope:** Verifiable deceased Mattingly ancestors and collateral lines tied to colonial Maryland and early Kentucky, with emphasis on arrival timing, named tracts, militia/public roles, Catholic settlement, and source-backed notable descendants.
**Confidence tiers:** `CONFIRMED` (direct public record or 2+ independent sources) | `PROBABLE` (strong compiled source, partially corroborated) | `POSSIBLE` (plausible but not fully bridged this session) | `UNVERIFIED` (not retained here)

---

## Executive Summary

The strongest early-America Mattingly facts available in public, linkable sources are these:

1. The immigrant-generation correction is probably **1663-1664**, not a 1660 arrival with Lord Baltimore. The best easily accessible citation in this session is Herman E. Mattingly's 1975 book as surfaced by Google Books.
2. Maryland archival indexes confirm that **Mattinglys Hope**, **Mount Misery**, **St. Thomas**, and **St. Johns als Mattingly Lane** were real Mattingly-associated tracts in the St. Mary's / Charles County record stream.
3. **Edward Mattingly** is directly documented in Maryland state records as a **captain in the Upper Battalion of Militia in Saint Mary's County** in 1779.
4. Benedict Joseph Webb's 1884 Kentucky history remains the key public source for the **1785 Catholic migration** and the **1786 arrival of William, Leonard, and Lucas Mattingly** from St. Mary's County, Maryland.
5. The best source-backed early Kentucky public/community role in the collateral lines is **Zachariah Mattingly** at Hardinsburg, with **Ignatius and Joseph Mattingly** helping build the first chapel.
6. The clearest later notable collateral descendant in this research pass is **Mother Generose Mattingly** of the Sisters of Loretto; **Marie Mattingly Meloney** is a strong later Bardstown collateral but not fully bridged here to the exact branch.

## 1. Colonial Maryland: Arrival and Land

### 1.1 Thomas Mattingly family in Maryland by 1663-1664

**Claim:** The first American Mattingly family was in Maryland by 1663-1664, and Thomas Mattingly died soon after arrival in 1664.

**Assessment:** `PROBABLE`

**Why this holds:** The strongest public citation I could verify directly this session is the Google Books snippet for Herman E. Mattingly's *The Mattingly Family in Early America* (1975), which states that Thomas Mattingly and his family left England for Maryland in 1663 and that he died soon after arrival in 1664. That is a compiled genealogy rather than a primary image, so I am not marking it `CONFIRMED` by itself.

**What this displaces:** I did not find a directly viewed public record in this session supporting a 1660 arrival with Lord Baltimore. The accessible evidence points later.

**Sources:**
- Google Books, *The Mattingly Family in Early America* metadata/snippet: https://books.google.com/books/about/The_Mattingly_Family_in_Early_America.html?id=nlw0AAAAMAAJ
- Open Library metadata for the same book: https://openlibrary.org/works/OL6663283W/The_Mattingly_family_in_early_America

### 1.2 Mattinglys Hope as an early tract

**Claim:** `Mattinglys Hope` was a real Mattingly-associated tract in the Maryland land record tradition.

**Assessment:** `CONFIRMED`

**Why this holds:** Maryland State Archives rent-roll and tract-index materials publicly surface the tract name in official archival series. The spelling varies (`Mattenlyes Hope`, `Mattenlys Hope`, `Mattinglys Hope`), which is normal for the period.

**Sources:**
- Maryland State Archives rent-roll PDF snippet showing `Mattenlyes Hope`, `Mattenlys Hope`, and `Mattinglys Lane`: https://msa.maryland.gov/megafile/msa/stagserm/sm100/sm130/000000/000014/pdf/mdsa_sm130_14.pdf
- Maryland State Archives rent-roll index snippet showing `MATTINGLY, JAMES ... Mattinglys Hope ... St. Johns`: https://msa.maryland.gov/megafile/msa/stagser/s1400/s1430/000000/000019/pdf/mdsa_s1430_19.pdf

### 1.3 Precise 1666 patent details for Mattinglys Hope

**Claim:** The immigrant family's rights became a 300-acre `Mattingly's Hope` patent in 1666, associated with the sons Thomas and Cezar.

**Assessment:** `PROBABLE`

**Why this is not higher:** This is repeated in later Mattingly compilations and derivative genealogy pages, and it fits the official tract-name evidence, but I did **not** inspect the original patent image or a Maryland State Archives item page for the 1666 patent in this session. The tract is real; the exact 1666 transaction detail remains one step removed here.

**Use cautiously:** Good enough for a research note, not good enough yet for a final narrative claim without checking the actual patent/certificate image or a direct MSA item entry.

**Sources:**
- Compiled family-history quotation of the 1666 patent sequence, explicitly tied to Herman E. Mattingly's research: https://maineandbeyond.tripod.com/id14.html
- Maryland State Archives tract/rent-roll continuity evidence for `Mattinglys Hope`: https://msa.maryland.gov/megafile/msa/stagserm/sm100/sm130/000000/000014/pdf/mdsa_sm130_14.pdf

### 1.4 Mount Misery / St. Thomas / Mattingly Lane continuity

**Claim:** By the eighteenth century, Thomas/Edward Mattingly are tied in archival indexes to `Mount Misery`, `St. Thomas`, and `St. Johns als Mattingly Lane`.

**Assessment:** `CONFIRMED`

**Why this matters:** Even without the original seventeenth-century patent image in hand, the official Maryland archival indexes show the family's land-name continuity in the same region.

**Sources:**
- Maryland State Archives rent-roll name index snippet for `MATTINGLY, THOMAS ... Mount Misery ... St. Thomas ... St. Johns als Mattingly Lane`: https://msa.maryland.gov/megafile/msa/stagser/s1400/s1430/000000/000019/pdf/mdsa_s1430_19.pdf
- Maryland State Archives plat/tract continuity for `Mattinglys Hope` as part of later St. Mary's surveys: https://guide.msa.maryland.gov/pages/series.aspx?id=C2225

## 2. Revolutionary-Era Maryland Public Role

### 2.1 Edward Mattingly, militia captain

**Claim:** Edward Mattingly served as a **captain** in the **Upper Battalion of Militia in Saint Mary's County** in 1779.

**Assessment:** `CONFIRMED`

**Why this holds:** The Maryland State Archives page for *Journal and Correspondence of the Council of Maryland, 1779-1780* includes the commission notice naming Edward Mattingly as captain, with Jonathan Edwards as first lieutenant and William Cartwright as second lieutenant.

**Research value:** This is the cleanest directly linkable Mattingly public office / militia role I found in colonial Maryland records this session.

**Source:**
- Maryland State Archives, *Journal and Correspondence of the Council of Maryland, 1779-1780*, vol. 43, p. 18: https://msa.maryland.gov/megafile/msa/speccol/sc2900/sc2908/000001/000043/html/am43--18.html

## 3. Maryland Catholics to Kentucky Catholics

### 3.1 The 1785 Catholic league from Maryland

**Claim:** In 1785 a league of sixty Catholic families, mostly from St. Mary's County, Maryland, organized emigration to Kentucky; twenty-five families left early in 1785 and reached Kentucky by late spring.

**Assessment:** `CONFIRMED`

**Why this holds:** Webb is explicit on the league, the first wave size, and the route via Pittsburgh and Maysville to Pottinger's Creek. This is the foundational migration context for the Kentucky Mattinglys.

**Source:**
- Searchable mirror of Benedict Joseph Webb, *The Centenary of Catholicity in Kentucky* (1884), Pottinger's Creek chapter: https://indianacatholic.mwweb.org/The_Centenary_of_Catholicity_in_Kentucky.pdf

### 3.2 Philip Mattingly in the Pottinger's Creek cluster

**Claim:** **Philip Mattingly** appears among the Pottinger's Creek Catholic settlers listed by Webb.

**Assessment:** `PROBABLE`

**Why not confirmed:** Webb says the full names of the original twenty-five are not all certainly known, and the longer Pottinger's Creek list covers settlers up to 1800. Philip Mattingly is definitely in Webb's list, but his exact status as one of the spring 1785 founders is slightly softer than the restatement sometimes given in family lore.

**Source:**
- Webb mirror, Pottinger's Creek settlers list including `Walter Burch, Philip Mattingly`: https://indianacatholic.mwweb.org/The_Centenary_of_Catholicity_in_Kentucky.pdf

### 3.3 William, Leonard, and Lucas Mattingly at Hardin's Creek

**Claim:** **William, Leonard, and Lucas Mattingly**, brothers from St. Mary's County, Maryland, arrived at the Hardin's Creek settlement in **early 1786**.

**Assessment:** `CONFIRMED`

**Why this matters:** This is the central Kentucky Mattingly founding fact in the public literature. Webb explicitly says the three brothers came from St. Mary's County and that the connection spread across the South and West.

**Source:**
- Webb mirror, Hardin's Creek chapter: https://indianacatholic.mwweb.org/The_Centenary_of_Catholicity_in_Kentucky.pdf

### 3.4 Leonard Mattingly as Kentucky patriarch

**Claim:** Leonard Mattingly died in 1827 with an estimated **nearly 300 living descendants**.

**Assessment:** `CONFIRMED`

**Why this matters:** It explains why so many nineteenth-century Kentucky Catholic Mattinglys appear quickly in Marion, Nelson, Washington, and nearby counties.

**Source:**
- Webb mirror, Hardin's Creek chapter and footnote: https://indianacatholic.mwweb.org/The_Centenary_of_Catholicity_in_Kentucky.pdf

## 4. Early Kentucky Public / Community Roles in Collateral Lines

### 4.1 Richard Mattingly of Long Lick

**Claim:** **Richard Mattingly**, born in Maryland in 1756, removed to Kentucky in 1791 and settled on **Long Lick** in what became Breckinridge County.

**Assessment:** `CONFIRMED`

**Why this matters:** This is a distinct but clearly documented early Kentucky Mattingly branch, separate from the three Hardin's Creek brothers.

**Source:**
- Webb mirror, Breckinridge chapter: https://indianacatholic.mwweb.org/The_Centenary_of_Catholicity_in_Kentucky.pdf

### 4.2 Zachariah Mattingly at Hardinsburg

**Claim:** **Zachariah Mattingly** was the **first resident Catholic of Hardinsburg** and likely the donor of the church and cemetery ground used by the early Catholic community there.

**Assessment:** `CONFIRMED`

**Why this matters:** This is one of the better source-backed community roles in the Kentucky collateral lines: not just residence, but visible local church-building leadership.

**Source:**
- Webb mirror, Breckinridge chapter: https://indianacatholic.mwweb.org/The_Centenary_of_Catholicity_in_Kentucky.pdf

### 4.3 Ignatius and Joseph Mattingly helped build the first Hardinsburg chapel

**Claim:** **Ignatius and Joseph Mattingly**, of Long Lick, materially helped build the first log chapel at Hardinsburg by getting out heavy timbers and hand-sawing planks.

**Assessment:** `CONFIRMED`

**Why this matters:** It is a small but unusually concrete labor-history detail in the early Kentucky Mattingly record.

**Source:**
- Webb mirror, Breckinridge chapter: https://indianacatholic.mwweb.org/The_Centenary_of_Catholicity_in_Kentucky.pdf

## 5. Source-Backed Notable Descendants / Collaterals

### 5.1 Mother Generose Mattingly (Loretto)

**Claim:** **Mother Generose Mattingly** served as a Loretto leader from **1842 to 1844**, and Webb identifies `Sister Generose` as **Martha**, daughter of **Basil Mattingly**.

**Assessment:** `CONFIRMED`

**Why this holds:** Webb identifies Basil's daughter Martha as the future Sister Generose. The Loretto Community's own leadership timeline independently confirms a `Mother Generose Mattingly` in 1842-1844.

**Why it matters:** This is a well-sourced example of the Kentucky Mattinglys producing a clearly identifiable public religious leader in the nineteenth century.

**Sources:**
- Webb mirror, Hardin's Creek family notes: https://indianacatholic.mwweb.org/The_Centenary_of_Catholicity_in_Kentucky.pdf
- Loretto Community leadership timeline: https://www.lorettocommunity.org/about/timeline-loretto-leaders/

### 5.2 Marie Mattingly Meloney (journalist, Curie fundraiser)

**Claim:** **Marie Mattingly Meloney** (1878-1943), born in **Bardstown, Kentucky**, daughter of physician **Cyprian Peter Mattingly**, was a major American journalist and editor.

**Assessment:** `CONFIRMED` for identity and parentage; `POSSIBLE` to `PROBABLE` as a specifically bridged collateral to the Leonard/Hardin's Creek line in this session.

**Why this stays cautious:** Britannica cleanly establishes who she was and where she was born. Her Bardstown origin makes same-clan descent highly plausible, but I did not bridge her exact father Cyprian Peter Mattingly to a documented Leonard/William/Lucas descendant in this pass.

**Research value:** Strong notable collateral candidate, but still needs one more generational bridge before being treated as a precise cousin line.

**Source:**
- Britannica biography: https://www.britannica.com/biography/Marie-Mattingly-Meloney
