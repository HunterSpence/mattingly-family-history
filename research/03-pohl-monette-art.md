# Research: Pohl-Monette Branch — Hugo Pohl, Minette Teichmueller, and Related Entities

## 1. Hugo Pohl — Texas Painter (1878–1960)

**Confidence: CONFIRMED (3 sources)**

Hugo D. Pohl was a real, documented San Antonio artist. Dates confirmed: born 1878, died 1960. The Minette Teichmueller Wikipedia article (citing primary sources) places him as "Hugo Pohl (1878 - 1960)."

**Major Work — "Founding of San Antonio"**
Pohl's largest known commission was the stage curtain painting for the San Antonio Municipal Auditorium (now site of the Tobin Center for the Performing Arts). The curtain was 36 x 75 ft, weighed 5,600 lb on an asbestos backing, and depicted his vision of the 1718 founding of Presidio San Antonio de Bexar. It also incorporated likenesses of Alamo defenders James Bowie, Davy Crockett, William B. Travis, and James Butler Bonham. The auditorium was built in 1926; the painting was destroyed in a 1979 fire. Source: Wikipedia, "San Antonio Municipal Auditorium."

**Texas A&M Connection**
Family oral history places large Pohl paintings at a Texas A&M student union. The Wikimedia Commons archive (Vol11No8.pdf) separately references a "Hugo B. Pohl" who exhibited California Mission drawings at the Museum in Santa Fe. Whether "Hugo B." and "Hugo D." are the same person needs further archival verification, but the San Antonio context strongly implies one primary artist.

**Teaching**
The Pál Fried Wikipedia article notes that Hugo Pohl taught at the Hungarian Academy of Arts, where Fried was one of his pupils. This indicates Pohl had a formal academic training and teaching career, likely prior to or concurrent with his Texas work.

**Reference books confirmed to contain a Hugo Pohl entry:**
- *Dictionary of Texas Artists, 1800–1945* by Paula L. Grauer and Michael R. Grauer — lists him alphabetically with color reproductions.
- *Art for History's Sake* by Cecilia Steinfeldt / Witte Memorial Museum (encyclopedia of Texas artists 1840–1940).

**Where paintings may exist today:**
The San Antonio Municipal Auditorium curtain is confirmed destroyed (1979 fire). Other works likely survive in the Witte Museum collection (San Antonio) and possibly the Texas A&M Memorial Student Center, but online collection searches were inconclusive due to paywalls. The San Antonio Museum of Art website search returned no results accessible via the public API.

**Photo of his work:** No confirmed publicly accessible image of a Pohl painting was located in Wikimedia Commons or LOC. The *Dictionary of Texas Artists* book is the most likely source for a color reproduction.

## 2. Monette Pohl (born Minette Teichmueller, 1871–1970)

**Confidence: CONFIRMED (2 sources — Wikipedia + infobox citing primary sources)**

The family called her "Monette" but her given name was **Minette Teichmueller**. This resolves both the maiden name question and the spelling. Wikipedia entry: "Minette Teichmueller (1871–1970), American muralist."

**Vital facts:**
- Born: January 27, 1871, La Grange, Texas (NOT Louisiana — she was a Texas native)
- Died: August 28, 1970, Nacogdoches, Texas (age 99 confirmed)
- Married: Hugo Pohl (dates of marriage not found in accessible sources)
- Studied at: Chicago Academy of Fine Arts and the San Antonio Academy of Art

**Her own artistic career:**
Minette was herself a professional artist — a muralist, not just a painter's wife. In 1940 she completed "The Law, Texas Rangers," an oil-on-canvas mural for the Smithville, Texas post office, commissioned by the Works Progress Administration / Treasury Section of Fine Arts. Wikipedia article at https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minette_Teichmueller

**On maiden name:** The name "Teichmueller" is a German-origin surname common in central Texas German immigrant communities (the Hill Country / La Grange area had large German settlement in the 19th century). The family photo reportedly searchable under "Hans Teichmueller" would be a relative with this same surname.

**On the Louisiana story:** Minette was born in La Grange, Texas, not Louisiana. However, the family may have had Louisiana connections — the Teichmueller family history would need archival research to confirm any Louisiana residence. The story of a 16-year-old sneaking out to see Mark Twain (~1887) could still be accurate if the family visited Louisiana or if Shari's recollection slightly conflated the location.

## 3. Date Conflict — 1903 vs. 1904 World's Fair

**Confidence: CONFIRMED (definitively resolved)**

The Louisiana Purchase Exposition (the St. Louis World's Fair that the 1944 film "Meet Me in St. Louis" depicts) ran **April 30 to December 1, 1904.** Source: Wikipedia, "Louisiana Purchase Exposition."

The key explanatory fact: "While initially conceived as a centennial celebration to be held in 1903, the actual opening of the St. Louis exposition was delayed until April 30, 1904, to allow for full-scale participation by more states and foreign countries." This is why the film is titled and set in 1903–04 — it dramatizes the period of anticipation leading up to the fair's opening. Shari's recollection of "1903" reflects the film's framing, not the actual fair date.

Pearl Baity almost certainly attended in **1904**, not 1903.

**German Pavilion documentation:**
The Library of Congress holds period stereograph photographs of the German Building at the 1904 World's Fair:
- "East Restaurant Pavilion and German Building, World's Fair, St. Louis, Mo." (LOC item 2013649886)
  ![German Building at 1904 World's Fair](https://tile.loc.gov/storage-services/service/pnp/stereo/1s00000/1s03000/1s03200/1s03296r.jpg)
- "Festival Hill from western pavilion, German Bldg., and Palace of Mines and Metallurgy in the background, World's Fair, St. Louis, 1904" (LOC item 2013649772)
  ![German Building festival view 1904](https://tile.loc.gov/storage-services/service/pnp/stereo/1s00000/1s03000/1s03200/1s03230r.jpg)

Kaiser Wilhelm II did not attend the fair personally. Germany's exhibit was mounted by the German government and commercial interests. The Wikipedia article on the exposition does not specifically describe a "Kaiser's living room set" for sale, but high-value German decorative arts exhibits at such fairs were standard — furniture and applied arts were major categories. The $5,000 purchase price (~$180,000 in 2025 dollars) is plausible for a premium reproduction of an imperial interior display piece.

## 4. Mark Twain's Lecture Tour and Monette at 16 (~1887)

**Confidence: PROBABLE for Louisiana stop, POSSIBLE for exact 1887 date**

**Confirmed tour:** Mark Twain and George Washington Cable conducted the "Twins of Genius" reading tour in 1884–85. Cable was a New Orleans novelist who wrote extensively about Creole life, and the tour did include Southern cities. Twain's own account in *Life on the Mississippi* describes touring New Orleans with Cable. Wikipedia on George Washington Cable confirms the tour ran in "1884 and 1885."

**Date issue:** If Minette (born January 27, 1871) was 16, that places the Twain sighting in 1887 or early 1888 — two to three years after the Cable-Twain tour ended. Possible explanations:
1. Shari's recollection of "age 16" is approximate — if she was 13–14, it fits the 1884–85 tour.
2. Twain made other Southern appearances in the late 1880s not captured in Wikipedia's brief tour summary.
3. The location may have been a Texas (not Louisiana) city on a different Twain tour.

**"Atheist" characterization:** Twain was publicly skeptical of organized religion and wrote sharply anti-clerical pieces, especially later in life. In the 1880s this reputation was forming. A Southern religious family in that era would likely view Twain as dangerously irreverent, making the "avant-garde" framing credible.

**Recommended verification:** The Mark Twain Project at UC Berkeley (marktwainproject.org) has annotated lecture schedules and correspondence. Search their archives for Louisiana / Texas appearances circa 1884–1888.

## 5. Kaiser's Living Room Set — Supporting Context

**Confidence: PROBABLE (context confirmed, specific purchase unverifiable)**

The 1904 St. Louis World's Fair featured major German commercial and cultural exhibits. Germany's government spent heavily on its pavilion. The German Building (documented in LOC photographs above) was a significant structure in Forest Park. High-end decorative arts — including furniture reproductions — were sold or auctioned at world's fairs of this era.

A $5,000 purchase at the 1904 fair (~$180,000 in 2025 dollars) for a mahogany furniture suite marketed as a reproduction of an imperial interior is entirely plausible. Kaiser Wilhelm II maintained lavish palace interiors that were widely publicized and photographed in the early 1900s, and reproduction "imperial" furniture was a prestige item at such exhibitions.

No specific catalog record for this purchase has been located — fair vendor records from 1904 would require archival research at the Missouri History Museum (St. Louis) or the National Archives.
