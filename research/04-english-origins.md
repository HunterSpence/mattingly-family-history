## Claim 1: Village Location — "near Cambridge" with "Mattingly Row"

**Shari's claim:** Mattingley is "a little village right outside of Cambridge" with a "Mattingly Row" street into Cambridge.

**Finding:** Definitively incorrect on the location. Mattingley is a village and civil parish in **Hampshire**, not Cambridgeshire. It lies on the Reading road between Hook and Reading, in the Hart District of northeast Hampshire (near the Surrey border). The River Whitewater runs through the parish. The nearest railway station is Hook, on the London and South Western line. Cambridge is approximately 70-80 miles northeast of Mattingley — an entirely different county.

No street named "Mattingly Row" exists in Cambridge or its environs. Zero results returned for this street name in any English gazetteer or street search.

The likely confusion: Mattingley is moderately close to **Reading** (about 12 miles), and Reading is sometimes conflated colloquially with being "near" London. Shari may have merged Mattingley's proximity to Reading with a different memory, or received a corrupted version of the location over generations.

**Confidence:** CONFIRMED (Hampshire) — 4+ sources all consistent. CONFIRMED absent near Cambridge — zero evidence of any Mattingley in Cambridgeshire.

## Claim 2: The "Beringa Tribe"

**Shari's claim:** Mattingley was "created in the eighth century by Beringa tribe chieftain named Mata."

**Finding:** The tribal name is **Basingas**, not "Beringa." The Basingas were a documented Old English tribe whose territory in the Loddon Valley formed a regio of the early Kingdom of Wessex. Their leader was named Basa, and the tribe's name survives in **Old Basing** and **Basingstoke** in Hampshire — both located about 22 km west-southwest of Mattingley village.

The genealogical record (Herman R. Mattingly, *The Mattingly Family in Early America*, 1975, page 5, as cited by the WikiTree Mattingly DNA Project) documents this directly: a Saxon known as Matta was a chief of a Basingas clan who landed on the southern coast of England and later moved north into the Whitewater Valley.

"Beringa" does not correspond to any documented Anglo-Saxon tribal name in Hampshire or elsewhere in England at this period. The only "Beringa"-adjacent term is Beringia (the prehistoric land bridge), which is entirely unrelated. Berecingas is a real Essex tribe (giving us Barking), and Bæringas appears in some Old English documents, but neither is associated with Hampshire or Mattingley. Shari's "Beringa" is almost certainly a phonetic corruption of **Basingas** passed down through oral family tradition.

The founding date: sources give the 5th-6th century for initial settlement, not strictly the 8th century. The Basingas settled eastern Basingstoke concurrent with the Frankish conquest of Alamannia in the early 6th century. The Mattingley settlement is placed in "the 6th century" or "the latter part of the 5th century" in the family history literature. The 8th century date Shari gives is plausible for formal documentation but not for the original founding — the place name pattern (Old English -ingaleah) is consistent with 5th-7th century formation.

**Confidence:** PROBABLE that Shari's "Beringa" = garbled "Basingas." The Basingas connection to Mattingley is documented in multiple genealogical sources (CONFIRMED). "Beringa" as a distinct tribe is UNVERIFIED / non-existent in the record.

## Claim 3: "Mata's Lee" — the Etymology

**Shari's claim:** Village name comes from "Mata's lee" — "Lee means land" — Mata + lee.

**Finding:** Substantially correct, though with minor terminological imprecision. The standard etymology per the *Dictionary of English Place-Names* (Oxford University Press, 1991) is:

> "Woodland clearing of the family or followers of a man called Matta, from the Old English personal name + '-inga' + 'leah.'"

The Domesday Book spelling was **Matingelege** (1086). Earlier forms include Matingel' and Madingel' in the Curia Regis Rolls of 1206. The full construction is **Matta + -inga- + leah**: Matta's people's clearing. "Leah" (Old English) means a woodland clearing or glade — not exactly "land" in the general sense, but close enough as a lay description. Shari's compression of this to "Mata's lee" is essentially correct folk etymology.

The personal name "Matta" (or Mætta) is a documented Old English male name. Whether there was a historically identifiable chieftain named Matta is not established — the name is inferred from the place name, as is standard for -ingas formations. No documentary source independently names a chieftain called Matta separate from the place name derivation.

**Confidence:** CONFIRMED for the etymology (Matta + leah). PROBABLE that Matta was a real 5th-6th century figure. UNVERIFIED that any historical document independently names him.

**Sources:**
- houseofnames.com (citing *Dictionary of English Place-Names*, OUP 1991): https://www.houseofnames.com/matingley-family-crest
- Wikipedia (Mattingley): https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mattingley — "The name comes from 'Matta's ley' (ley means place in Old English), referring to the Matta family."

## Claim 4: Domesday Book Entry for Mattingley (1086)

**Shari's claim:** "The name is in there" — spelled "Mattingley maybe."

**Finding:** Correct that Mattingley appears in the Domesday Book, but the actual 1086 spelling was **Matingelege** (not Mattingley). The village was recorded as a settlement in the hundred of Holdshott, part of the manor of Heckfield. The entry records:

> 8 villagers, 3 smallholders, 3 ploughlands, 3 men's plough teams, 4 acres of meadow, 1 mill valued at 5 shillings.

Tenants listed were Hugh de Port and Alfsi son of Brictsi, holding from the King. Mattingley remained a chapelry of Heckfield parish until 1894, when it became its own civil parish.

Shari's claim that it is "in there" is correct. Her hedging on the spelling ("maybe") is appropriate — the actual spelling is quite different from modern "Mattingley." This is a case where Shari's instinct was right even if the specifics were uncertain.

**Confidence:** CONFIRMED — multiple sources cite the Domesday entry as "Matingelege."

## Claim 5: The 1660 Emigration with Lord Baltimore

**Shari's claim:** Thomas Mattingly came over in 1660, family was Catholic, fleeing Protestant ascendancy after Cromwell's fall, "done a favor for the king," settled in Maryland under Lord Baltimore.

**Finding:** Partially correct with important date corrections.

**Date:** The emigration was **not 1660**. Genealogical records are consistent that Thomas Mattingly I (born approximately 1623, Sussex/Berkshire, England) arrived in Maryland in **late 1663 or early 1664**, dying shortly thereafter (July 24, 1664). The Geni.com research note states explicitly: "The exact date of arrival in America of the Mattingly family is unknown... The first records of the Mattingly family in America are found in Maryland in 1664. It is possible that they arrived in late 1663 or early 1664." One record (nielsenhayden.com) places his death in 1663.

**The King in 1660:** Shari was uncertain — "James or might have been James." In fact, **Charles II** was restored to the throne in May 1660. James II did not reign until 1685. When Thomas Mattingly emigrated (1663-1664), the king was indeed Charles II. Shari's uncertainty was wise; her instinct toward the wrong James is corrected here.

**Lord Baltimore:** Correct. The 2nd Lord Baltimore was **Cecilius (Cecil) Calvert**, who held the Maryland proprietorship from 1632 until his death in 1675. He founded Maryland partly as a haven for English Catholics. In 1663-1664 when the Mattinglys arrived, Cecilius Calvert was still the proprietor.

**Catholic motivation:** Plausible and consistent. The Calvert family specifically recruited Catholic settlers. The period 1650-1660 had seen the Maryland Toleration Act revoked under Puritan commissioner William Claiborne (1654), then reinstated after the Calverts regained control. Catholic emigration to Maryland increased after the Restoration, as Charles II was sympathetic to Catholics.

**Prior Virginia residency:** Sr. Donnelly's book (*Colonial Settlers, St. Clement's Bay*) states Thomas Mattingly "immigrated from Virginia into the Province of Maryland prior to 1664," suggesting the family may have first settled in Virginia before moving to Maryland — not directly from England to Maryland.

**Confidence:** PROBABLE on Catholic motivation and Lord Baltimore connection. CONFIRMED on Charles II as king. The "1660" date is likely a round-number conflation with the Restoration year — actual emigration was 1663-1664. POSSIBLE that a royal favor was involved (family tradition not corroborated by documentary evidence found).

## Claim 6: "Mattingly's Hope" Land Grant

**Shari's claim:** Land grant called "Mattingly's Hope," described as "a little village type thing."

**Finding:** Confirmed and well-documented. Thomas Mattingly died in 1664 before a formal patent was issued. His widow Elizabeth obtained letters of administration. A certificate was issued in **May 1666** for **300 acres** in **Charles County, Maryland**, to be known as "Mattingly's Hope." The formal patent was issued on **September 4, 1666**, made out to Thomas Mattingly I's two sons, **Thomas (II) and Cezar Mattingly** — representing 50 acres per head for six family members who immigrated.

Cezar Mattingly apparently retained the original Mattingly's Hope tract in Charles County. Thomas II later purchased additional land in St. Mary's County including a parcel called "Mount Misery" (100 acres). "Mattingly's Hope" as a current geographic feature or village does not appear in any modern gazetteer — it was a colonial land patent designation, not a formal settlement that persisted under that name.

The acreage (300 acres) is confirmed by three independent genealogical sources.

**Confidence:** CONFIRMED — three consistent independent sources (Geni, WikiTree, jhink.org all citing the same 1666 record). The "village" characterization is folk-memory imprecision; it was a land patent, not a village.

## Claim 7: 1525 Ancestor via LDS Records

**Shari's claim:** LDS Genealogical Center in Houston traced the line back to "1525," described as the "great great grandfather" of Thomas Mattingly who emigrated.

**Finding:** This is consistent with what the genealogical record shows, though exact 1525 documentation is hard to confirm from web sources. The freshford.com research notes that "The Hampshire Record Office at Winchester has some of the earliest documents relating to the Mattingly family," with forms including "Mattyngle" and "Mattenley." houseofnames.com documents the earliest named record as "Stephen de Madingel', de Matingel'" in the Curia Regis Rolls of **1206**, and "Peter Mattingeley" listed in **1249** in Hampshire — though these are pre-surname period.

For the family line of Thomas Mattingly I (born ~1623, Sussex), working back four generations at ~25-30 years per generation would reach approximately 1500-1525, making the 1525 claim structurally plausible. The FamilySearch catalog reference for "The Mattingly Family in Early America" (Herman R. Mattingly, 1975) is the likely source of this information. FamilySearch catalog entry: https://www.familysearch.org/en/search/catalog/570053.

No independently verified 1525 Hampshire Mattingly document was accessible in this research session. The claim is plausible given the depth of Hampshire parish records but should be treated as POSSIBLE until verified against a primary document.

**Confidence:** POSSIBLE — structurally plausible, not independently verified from this research session.

## Claim 8: 1780s Migration to Kentucky with Daniel Boone / Boonesborough

**Shari's claim:** Relatives followed Daniel Boone from Maryland to Kentucky in the 1780s, "one of 20 families who went to found Boonesborough," and Mattinglys are still in Boonesborough today.

**Finding:** The broad outline is correct but contains two specific errors on dates and the Boone connection.

**Boonesborough founding:** Daniel Boone founded Boonesborough on **April 1, 1775** — not in the 1780s. It was one of the first English-speaking settlements west of the Appalachians.

**The Catholic Maryland migration to Kentucky:** The documented wave of Maryland Catholic families migrating to Kentucky occurred in **1785**, not the 1780s in the sense of following Boone. The Archdiocese of Louisville records state: "The first Catholic presence in Kentucky may have been a group of 25 families who traveled from Maryland in 1785 to Goodwin's Station in present-day Nelson County." JSTOR article "Pioneer Catholics in Kentucky" confirms: "In the year of 1785, some twenty families from St. Mary's County, Maryland, migrated to Kentucky." A Geni.com project notes: "The first large Catholic migration into Nelson County was begun in 1785 by the League of Catholic Families, most of whom were from St. Mary's County, Maryland."

**Mattingly families in this migration:** Documented. Multiple Mattingly individuals appear in St. Mary's County, Maryland (1790 census) and Washington, Kentucky. William Mattingly (born ~1741 St. Mary's County MD, died 1817 Washington, Kentucky) is directly documented as a migrant. FamilySearch records show Edward Mattingly born 1785 in St. Mary's, Maryland, married 1811 in Washington, Kentucky.

**Connection to Boone:** The 1785 Catholic migration settled primarily in **Nelson County** (Bardstown area) and **Washington County** — not Boonesborough (Madison County). These are different parts of Kentucky. Shari has merged two distinct streams: Boone's 1775 frontier settlement at Boonesborough, and the separate 1785 Catholic Maryland families who went to the Bardstown area. The Mattingly families were in the latter group.

**Mattingly distilling tradition in Kentucky:** Real and documented. J.G. Mattingly built the **Mattingly & Moore Distillery** (RD #272, Nelson County) in **1877**, one half-mile south of Bardstown. The operation ran until Prohibition (1920). A current descendant operation, **J. Mattingly 1845** distillery in Frankfort, Kentucky, claims seven generations of Mattingly involvement in eight different distilleries — a direct lineage from the Maryland Catholic migration families.

**Confidence:** CONFIRMED that Maryland Catholic Mattingly families migrated to Kentucky, predominantly in **1785** to Nelson/Washington/Marion Counties. CONFIRMED that Boonesborough was founded in 1775, not the 1780s. PROBABLE that Mattingly relatives were among the 1785 Catholic migration wave. The specific "20 families with Daniel Boone to Boonesborough" detail conflates two separate events.
