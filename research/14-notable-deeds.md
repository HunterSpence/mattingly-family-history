## Medieval English Line

### Ellis of Mattingley (fl. 1167)
**The first named lord — recorded in the Pipe Roll of Henry II**

In 1167, the royal financial accounts of King Henry II — compiled in the Pipe Roll for the thirteenth year of his reign — record Ellis as owner of the village of Mattingley in Hampshire. He is the earliest named person connected to the manor, standing at the head of a line that would carry its place-name as a surname across 850 years and two oceans. The village itself had been called "Matingelege" since before the Domesday survey of 1086, deriving from "Matta's ley" — the woodland clearing of a Saxon chieftain named Matta whose people, the Basingas, had settled the Whitewater Valley in the fifth or sixth century. Ellis left no patronym; the surname was still the land itself.

**Sources:** British History Online, VCH Hampshire vol. 4 — https://www.british-history.ac.uk/vch/hants/vol4/pp44-51 | Wikipedia — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mattingley

### Stephen de Mattingley (fl. 1206)
**Earliest dated bearer of the Mattingley surname — granted 6 virgates and a mill to the Prior of Merton**

Stephen de Mattingley is the first person in recorded history to carry the surname in a dated document. In 1206, he appeared in the Curia Regis Rolls as "Stephen de Madingel'" and in the Feet of Fines for Hampshire (Trinity 8 John) when he granted to Walter, Prior of Merton, and his successors in free alms: six virgates of land, thirty acres, several meadows, six shillings and three pence rent, and a mill at Mattingley. The Oxford Dictionary of English Surnames (Reaney and Wilson, 1991) cites this precise transaction as the earliest dated attestation of the Mattingley surname. The mill and the meadows are gone; the name endures.

**Sources:** BHO VCH Hampshire vol. 4 — https://www.british-history.ac.uk/vch/hants/vol4/pp44-51 | Reaney & Wilson cited at https://www.houseofnames.com/matingley-family-crest

### Peter de Mattingley (fl. 1236–1249)
**Last lord of Mattingley to bear the name — sold the manor and ended the family's territorial connection**

Son of Stephen, Peter de Mattingley was the last of his line to hold the manor that gave his family its name. Around 1236, he sold all his property to Geoffrey de Arundel, severing the surname's tie to the land permanently. The Victoria County History of Hampshire records the transaction plainly: "Stephen was succeeded by his son Peter de Mattingley, who sold all his property to Geoffrey de Arundel." After that sale, the name disappears from the village's manorial record for nearly four hundred years. A blank of three centuries separates him from the next confirmed Mattingley — a London burial in 1641.

**Source:** BHO VCH Hampshire vol. 4 — https://www.british-history.ac.uk/vch/hants/vol4/pp44-51

## Colonial Maryland

### Thomas Mattingly II (~1650–1715)
**Secured 'Mattingly's Hope' — 300 acres in Maryland under the patent of September 4, 1666**

Thomas Mattingly II arrived in Maryland before 1665 with his wife Elizabeth and their children, part of a wave of English Catholics drawn to Lord Baltimore's promised haven. On September 4, 1666, a formal land patent was issued in St. Mary's County for 300 acres — fifty acres for each of six family members who had emigrated — naming Thomas II and his brother Cezar as patentees. The tract was called "Mattingly's Hope." Thomas's name appears on the St. Mary's County Rent Rolls from 1663 to 1720, a continuous fifty-seven-year span of documented residence. His will, probated before January 12, 1715, named children including Ignatius, James, and others who would carry the Catholic Maryland line for two more generations before a branch walked west into Kentucky.

### Cezar Mattingly (1654–1719)
**Co-patentee of 'Mattingly's Hope' — co-founder of the Maryland Catholic line**

Cezar Mattingly crossed from England to Maryland with his brother Thomas II, and on September 4, 1666, his name was inscribed alongside Thomas's on the patent for "Mattingly's Hope" in Charles County, Maryland. Cezar appears to have retained the original 300-acre Charles County tract while Thomas moved to St. Mary's County — making him the anchor of one branch of the line. His given name, unusual in an English Catholic family, may reflect Continental Catholic influence in the naming conventions of Lord Baltimore's colonial circle. He died in 1719, having lived through Maryland's entire transformation from proprietary Catholic colony to Protestant royal province.

### Ignatius Mattingly (1704–1789)
**Patriarch of the Kentucky migration — his sons carried the Catholic Maryland line westward in 1785**

Born around 1704 in St. Mary's County, Ignatius Mattingly lived eighty-five years — long enough to see his sons depart for the Kentucky frontier. His will, probated April 1789 in St. Mary's County Will Book JJ1, named sons Leonard, Luke, and William. The name Ignatius is a declaration of faith: Saint Ignatius of Loyola founded the Society of Jesus in 1540, and the Jesuits had been the primary religious presence in Catholic Maryland since its founding. In 1785, Leonard and Luke were among roughly twenty-five Maryland Catholic families — most from St. Mary's County — who migrated to Nelson County, Kentucky, establishing what became known as the "Holy Land of Kentucky." Ignatius did not make the journey, but his sons built a Catholic community in the wilderness that endures today.

## Kentucky Migration

### Leonard Mattingly Jr. (1764–1843)
**Led the 1785 Catholic Maryland migration to Kentucky; confirmed genetic ancestor of the DNA surname project**

Leonard Mattingly Jr. was born in 1764 in Leonardtown, Maryland, and by January 7, 1788 was marrying Ann Cissell in Nelson County, Kentucky — having traveled west in the landmark 1785 Catholic migration. That year, roughly twenty-five Maryland families made the overland journey to what would become the "Holy Land of Kentucky." Leonard settled in Marion County in the community of Saint Mary, where he died in 1843. He is the earliest confirmed genetic ancestor in the Mattingly-Matney Y-chromosome DNA Surname Project, with multiple independent participants tracing their direct paternal descent to him. The FamilySearch record for his 1788 Kentucky marriage (V5ZD-6SJ) is his surviving documentary signature.

**Sources:** WikiTree Mattingly-210 — https://www.wikitree.com/wiki/Mattingly-210 | JSTOR Pioneer Catholics in Kentucky — https://www.jstor.org/stable/23377364

### Mattingly & Moore Distillery (founded 1877)
**Bardstown, Kentucky — a descendant business of the 1785 Catholic migration line**

In 1877, J.G. Mattingly built the Mattingly & Moore Distillery — Registered Distillery No. 272, Nelson County, Kentucky — one half-mile south of Bardstown, in the heart of bourbon country. The 1785 Catholic Maryland migration had concentrated a community of farming and commercial families in exactly this region. The distillery operated until Prohibition forced its closure in 1920. A present-day descendant operation, J. Mattingly 1845 in Frankfort, Kentucky, claims seven generations of family involvement across eight different distilleries — a commercial lineage stretching from the colonists who planted tobacco in St. Mary's County to craftsmen who barrel-age whiskey today.

**Source:** pre-pro.com DST391 — https://www.pre-pro.com/midacore/view_distillery.php?did=DST391

## Texas Generations

### Hans Teichmueller (1837–1901)
**District Judge of the 22nd Judicial District for 17 uncontested years — from Brunswick army family to Texas bench**

Hans Teichmueller was born on March 7, 1837 in Brunswick, Germany, to an army officer whose wife came from the noble von Girsewald family — whose members included two Prussian generals. His father's death left the family in financial difficulty; in 1856, at nineteen, Hans emigrated to Texas. He taught school in New Ulm while reading law, was admitted to the bar in 1866, and served as County Judge of Fayette County before being removed by Reconstruction military order in 1870. In 1884 he was elected District Judge of the 22nd Judicial District and held the seat without opposition for the remaining seventeen years of his life, dying February 17, 1901. The 1902 county history published a portrait and biography calling his legal essays "classic." His daughter Mary married Edward Mattingly; their son Leroy carried Hans's family name as his middle name.

**Sources:** Lotto 1902 at Internet Archive — https://archive.org/details/fayettecountyher00lott | TSHA — https://www.tshaonline.org/handbook/entries/teichmueller-hans

### Minette Teichmueller (1871–1970)
**WPA muralist; painted 'The Law, Texas Rangers' for Smithville post office in 1940 — lived to 99**

Minette Teichmueller was born in La Grange, Texas on January 27, 1871 — daughter of District Judge Hans Teichmueller — and died in Nacogdoches on August 28, 1970, ninety-nine years old. She studied at the Chicago Academy of Fine Arts and the San Antonio Academy of Art, and married fellow artist Hugo Pohl. In 1940 the WPA's Treasury Section of Fine Arts commissioned her to paint "The Law, Texas Rangers," an oil-on-canvas mural for the Smithville, Texas post office. The family remembers her as "really avant-garde for her time," someone who as a teenager slipped away from home to hear Mark Twain read aloud — a scandalous act in a devout Southern household. She outlived her famous father by sixty-nine years.

**Source:** Wikipedia Minette Teichmueller — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minette_Teichmueller

### Hugo Pohl (1878–1960)
**Painted a 36x75-foot stage curtain for San Antonio Municipal Auditorium — destroyed in the 1979 fire**

Hugo Pohl painted on a scale that few artists ever attempt. His commission for the San Antonio Municipal Auditorium — built in 1926 as a WWI memorial — produced a stage curtain 36 feet high and 75 feet wide, weighing 5,600 pounds on an asbestos backing. The painting depicted the 1718 founding of Presidio San Antonio de Bexar and incorporated portraits of Alamo defenders James Bowie, Davy Crockett, William B. Travis, and James Butler Bonham. For more than fifty years it hung in the city's largest public hall. A 1979 fire at the auditorium destroyed it — an irreplaceable artifact of Texas civic art lost in a single night. Pohl also taught at the Hungarian Academy of Arts, where he trained painter Pal Fried, and appears in the Dictionary of Texas Artists, 1800-1945.

**Sources:** Wikipedia San Antonio Municipal Auditorium — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Antonio_Municipal_Auditorium | Wikipedia Minette Teichmueller — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minette_Teichmueller

### Pearl (Paralee) Johnson Baity (~1874–1971)
**Bought Reeves County mineral rights in April 1901 — three months after Spindletop — whose royalties the family still receives**

In April 1901, Pearl Johnson Baity purchased land in Reeves County, Texas — raw West Texas range with fewer than 2,000 residents. The Spindletop gusher had blown in on January 10, 1901, just ninety days earlier, establishing Texas as an oil state. Pearl retained the mineral rights when she later sold the surface. The Wolfcamp Shale beneath Reeves County — assessed by the USGS in 2016 as containing 20 billion barrels of technically recoverable oil, the largest such assessment in US history at the time — began generating royalty payments to her heirs around 2010. Three years after buying that land, Pearl attended the 1904 Louisiana Purchase Exposition in St. Louis and returned with a $5,000 living-room suite from the German pavilion (roughly $180,000 in 2025 dollars). She died around 1971, approximately 97 years old.

**Sources:** Wikipedia Spindletop — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spindletop | USGS Fact Sheet 2016-3092 — https://pubs.usgs.gov/fs/2016/3092/fs20163092.pdf | TSHA Reeves County — https://www.tshaonline.org/handbook/entries/reeves-county

### William Alexander Baity (1862–1945)
**Builder credited with constructing a substantial portion of early twentieth-century San Antonio**

William Alexander Baity came from Yadkin County, North Carolina to San Antonio and built a contracting business that left a physical imprint on the city. Family oral history holds that he "built half of San Antonio" — an expression common to successful builders of an era when a single firm could erect entire residential neighborhoods during a boom. He died in 1945 in the house at 211 Castillo Avenue, San Antonio — the home he built around 1905 in the Lavaca neighborhood just south of downtown, in the same corridor as the King William Historic District where prosperous tradespeople of his generation settled.

**Sources:** Family oral history (Shari Mattingly, 2024) | OSM address confirmation — https://www.google.com/maps/place/211+Castillo+Ave,+San+Antonio,+TX+78210

### Dr. Claude Mattingly (1898–1934)
**Prominent Austin pediatrician; death at the Texan Hotel on January 31, 1934 — covered by AP wire across Texas**

Dr. Claude Mattingly was thirty-five years old when he was found dead in his room at the Texan Hotel, Austin, on February 1, 1934. He had studied medicine at the University of Texas Medical Branch in Galveston, served in the Marine Corps during World War I, and practiced as a pediatric specialist in Austin for six years. The bodies of both Dr. Mattingly and Mrs. Rhea B. Perrin — wife of a University of Texas psychology professor — were found side by side, with narcotic injection materials present. Justice of the Peace Tom R. Johnson returned a verdict of double suicide. The story ran on the front page of the Brownsville Herald on February 1, 1934, and was carried by the Associated Press and International News Service across Texas through February 6. His body was returned to La Grange for burial. The family has not spoken of him since.

**Sources:** Brownsville Herald Feb 1 1934 at LOC — https://www.loc.gov/resource/sn86063730/1934-02-01/ed-2/?sp=1 | San Antonio Light Feb 2 1934 at LOC — https://www.loc.gov/resource/sn85060004/1934-02-02/ed-1/?sp=9

### The Centenarian (~1828–1935)
**Reportedly lived to 107 — one of perhaps five documented American centenarians of his era**

Family oral history preserves the memory of a Mattingly great-great-great-grandfather born around 1828 who lived to approximately 1935, reaching age 107. At that time, documented centenarians in the United States could be counted on one hand; a man who remembered the years before the Civil War and lived to witness the New Deal would have been a significant figure in any Texas community. If the account is accurate, an obituary would exist in Fayette or Bexar County newspaper archives for 1935. No primary source has yet been located, but the family's oral record has proven reliable on most testable points. Longevity runs conspicuously in this line.

**Source:** Family oral history (Shari Mattingly, 2024) — primary source verification pending

### Joseph Hardin Frost (president of Frost Bank, 1926)
**President of Frost Bank from 1926 — connection places the family inside San Antonio's financial establishment**

Joseph Hardin Frost became president of Frost Bank — formally the Frost National Bank — in 1926. Founded in 1868 by Thomas Claiborne Frost, the bank had remained under exclusive family control and survived the 1929 crash and Depression when many Texas banks did not. Family oral history identifies a "Frost Bank uncle" — likely Joseph Hardin Frost — as a relative through the San Antonio branches of the family. Whether by blood or marriage, the connection places the Mattingly family in the inner circle of San Antonio's financial establishment during the city's formative decades.

**Source:** Wikipedia Frost Bank — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frost_Bank

## Cross-Cutting Themes

**Longevity runs in the family**
Three near-centenarians or centenarians across five generations: Minette Teichmueller (1871-1970, age 99), Pearl Baity (~1874-1971, age ~97), and the unverified Centenarian ancestor (~1828-1935, age ~107). Judith (Mattingly) Turner (1645-1744) and Elizabeth Dorcas Mattingly (1656-1749) appear in family tradition with reported lifespans of ~99 and ~93.

**Catholic identity across eight generations**
From Lord Baltimore's Maryland in 1665 to the Holy Land of Kentucky in 1785, the family maintained its Catholic faith through colonial persecution, Reconstruction, and the American frontier. The name Ignatius appears twice across the line — a deliberate Jesuit echo.

**Art and law in one generation**
Hans Teichmueller (judge), Minette Teichmueller (WPA muralist), and Hugo Pohl (monumental painter) were all members of the same family circle in the 1880s-1940s.

**Mineral foresight**
Pearl Baity bought Reeves County land three months after Spindletop and retained the mineral rights. A decision made in 1901 still generates royalties in 2025 from the Wolfcamp Shale.

**Suppressed tragedy**
A prominent physician's death that made AP wire across Texas in 1934 was never discussed in the family again.
