## 2. Uncle Zay — Stauffer Chemicals

**Status: PROBABLE match to Stauffer family era; specific individual UNVERIFIED**

**What is confirmed about Stauffer Chemical (Wikipedia, confirmed):**
- Founded 1885 in San Francisco as a partnership between John Stauffer Sr. (German) and Christian de Guigné (French)
- Hans Stauffer, nephew of founder John Stauffer Sr., joined in 1920 and retired as president in **1967**, died 1986
- John Stauffer Jr., son of the founder and director emeritus, died in 1972. He was the donor behind the John Stauffer Chemistry Building at **Stanford University** and the John Stauffer Laboratory for Physical Chemistry
- The company's head office was in Westport, Connecticut by 1987
- Acquired by Chesebrough-Pond's, then sold to Imperial Chemical Industries in 1987

**Regarding "Uncle Zay":**
The Wikipedia article does not name who succeeded Hans Stauffer as president after 1967. "Zay" almost certainly reads as a nickname — most likely Zachary or Isaiah, possibly Ezra. Shari's uncle (her mother's or father's brother) with a PhD from MIT in chemistry or physics and a senior role at Stauffer would have been active in the 1960s–1980s window, consistent with post-Hans-Stauffer leadership.

The Stanford connection is striking: John Stauffer Jr. had a named building at Stanford. A Stauffer-family-adjacent executive with a chemistry/physics PhD could have come from either the Stauffer family itself or a closely connected senior hire. The de Guigné family also contributed heirs to the company.

**To resolve:** MIT alumni records (PhD chemistry/physics, graduation range 1945–1965) or the Stauffer Chemical corporate archive, if held by NARA or a university library. The SEC filings from 1982–1984 (earnings dispute) would list corporate officers and may name the then-president by name.

## 3. "We're From Ignatius" — Recording Opening

**Status: POSSIBLE — two competing explanations, one strong candidate**

**Candidate 1 — A named Mattingly ancestor (POSSIBLE):**
The Wikipedia disambiguation page for "Mattingly" lists **Ignatius G. Mattingly (1927–2004)**, an American linguist and speech scientist. He held a BA from Yale (1947), an MA in linguistics from Harvard (1959), and a PhD from Yale (1968). He was an NSA analyst 1955–1966, then a professor at the University of Connecticut and a researcher at Haskins Laboratories until his death. The name Ignatius is strongly Catholic in tradition — St. Ignatius of Loyola founded the Jesuits in 1540, and Maryland Catholic families (which the Mattinglys were — Catholic refugees from Protestant England, documented from the 1660s) routinely used Ignatian names across multiple generations.

It is plausible that an older or collateral Mattingly ancestor named Ignatius appears in the family's oral genealogy and that the recording began mid-sentence: "We're from [the line of] Ignatius" or "We're from [a branch that goes back through] Ignatius."

**Candidate 2 — Mishearing or recording artifact (POSSIBLE):**
The recording may have started mid-sentence. "We're from Ignatius" could be a fragment of a longer phrase that is inaudible or cut off. No Texas town named "Ignatius" (as opposed to San Ygnacio, a small community in Zapata County on the Rio Grande) is documented; San Ygnacio is a historically Mexican-American community and has no apparent Mattingly connection.

**Bottom line:** The Catholic-saint-name interpretation within the Mattingly genealogy is the more coherent reading. Recommend asking Shari directly: "Who was Ignatius in your family?"

## 4. Frost Bank President — Shari's Uncle

**Status: PROBABLE family identification; specific individual UNVERIFIED**

**What is confirmed (Wikipedia, confirmed):**
- Frost Bank (formally Cullen/Frost Bankers) was founded in 1868 by Thomas Claiborne Frost in San Antonio
- Named presidents in the historical record: T.C. Frost Sr. (founder), T.C. Frost Jr. (son), Joseph Hardin Frost (brother of T.C. Jr., took over as president in 1926)
- In 1977, the company merged with Cullen Bankers Inc. of Houston to form Cullen/Frost Bankers
- The third generation, Thomas Claiborne Frost III ("Tom Frost III"), was the key figure who led the company through the 1977 Cullen merger and into the 1980s–1990s
- As of the Wikipedia article, current leadership is Phil Green (Chairman/CEO) and Paul Bracher (President)

**The puzzle:** Frost Bank has been exclusively Frost-family controlled since 1868. For Shari's maternal uncle to have been "president of Frost Bank," one of two things must be true: (a) he was a Frost family member by birth on his mother's side — meaning Shari's grandmother was a Frost — or (b) he was a highly senior non-family executive, which would be unusual given the bank's history of family leadership. A lawyer who became bank president fits the Tom Frost III profile (lawyer background in San Antonio society) but that would imply a Frost family blood connection.

**To resolve:** San Antonio social registers from the 1960s–1990s, or Shari's recollection of her mother's maiden name, would immediately clarify whether the family carried Frost blood or married into the family.

## 6. The Texas Governor Connection

**Status: POSSIBLE — two strong candidates identified**

The claim: a great-aunt's family arrived through Galveston in 1880 and settled central Texas, where they had a farm neighboring the family that became the governor of Texas.

Galveston was the major port of entry for European immigrants (especially Germans and Czechs) into Texas from the 1840s through the 1920s. German settlers came heavily in 1845–1860; Czech settlers peaked in the 1870s–1900s. Both groups settled primarily in the central Texas corridor (Austin, Bastrop, Fayette, McLennan, Milam, and Williamson counties).

**Candidate 1 — Lawrence Sullivan Ross (PROBABLE):**
Governor 1887–1891. His family settled in Milam County in central Texas in 1839, near present-day Cameron. Ross grew up in Waco (McLennan County), served in the Civil War, and became governor. His family farmed in exactly the region where German and Czech immigrants settled in the 1870s–1880s. If the great-aunt's family came through Galveston in 1880 and took the common immigrant route to central Texas, they would have landed in McLennan or Milam county — the Ross heartland. Ross became governor in 1887, seven years after the 1880 arrival; a neighbor family recollecting "we used to farm with the Rosses" is entirely plausible.

**Candidate 2 — Pat Morris Neff (POSSIBLE):**
Governor 1921–1925. Born 1871 in McGregor, McLennan County, Texas. McGregor is a heavily Czech/German-settled community west of Waco. The Neff family was farming there through the 1880s, exactly the period when immigrant families were arriving. However, Neff was less prominent than Ross and the timing of his governorship (1920s) is more distant from the 1880 arrival story.

**Sul Ross is the stronger candidate** because: his family was physically present in central Texas before and during the 1880 immigration wave, he became governor within living memory of the 1880 arrivals, and his home base (Milam County / Waco area) aligns with the standard Galveston-to-central-Texas immigrant corridor.

## 7. Genetic Claims — Accuracy Check

**R1b haplogroup origin date**
Shari's claim: "originated about fifteen thousand years ago." Wikipedia (Rh blood group Wikipedia article, multiple sources): estimated at 12,500–25,700 BP, most probably around 18,500 years ago. The claim is approximately correct — within the range of estimates, though 15,000 is slightly toward the lower bound.
**Verdict: PROBABLE — slightly understated but within scientific range.**

**Out of Africa timing**
Shari's claim: "60–80,000 years ago." Wikipedia (Out of Africa article): present-day non-African humans descend mainly from a single expansion approximately 70,000–50,000 years ago. Shari's figure is slightly offset on the older end but not inaccurate.
**Verdict: CONFIRMED — consensus figure is 70,000–50,000 BP, her "60–80k" overlaps.**

**Basque Rh-negative blood frequency**
Shari's claim: Basque have the highest O-negative frequency at about 30%.
Wikipedia (Rh blood group system article): Basque country has 47.2% Rh-negative (lack of D antigen) — the highest globally. Wikipedia (blood type distribution table): Basques are 51% type O (ABO). If ~51% are type O and ~47.2% are Rh-negative, a rough estimate for O-negative in the Basque population is approximately 24–25%.
**Verdict: PARTIALLY CONFIRMED with a correction.** The Basque *Rh-negative* rate (~47%) is the world's highest, well confirmed. The *O-negative* specific figure Shari cited (~30%) is an overestimate; the actual O-negative frequency is closer to 24–25%. The underlying fact she's pointing to — Basque people having uniquely high rates of Rh-negativity — is correct.

**Irish/Celtic O-negative rate**
Wikipedia (blood type distribution table): Ireland O-negative = 8%, compared to a world average of approximately 7%. Ireland does have a slightly elevated O-negative rate, and O-positive is 47% (one of the highest nationally). This supports the broad claim that Celtic populations have elevated O-negative rates, though the difference from the global average is modest.
**Verdict: PROBABLE — Ireland's 8% O-negative is somewhat elevated but not dramatically so.**

**Mitochondrial Eve age**
Shari's claim: L haplogroup traces to "mitochondrial Eve 240,000 years ago."
Wikipedia (Mitochondrial Eve): as of 2013, estimates of the mt-MRCA age are around 155,000 years ago. The L haplogroup article gives the origin of L1'2'3'4'5'6 at approximately 170,000 ± 36,000 years ago (Soares et al. 2009). Haplogroup L0 arose between 200,000 and 130,000 years ago.
**Verdict: OVERCLAIMED.** The 240,000 figure is outside the consensus range. The corrected range is approximately 130,000–200,000 BP for Mitochondrial Eve, with the central estimate around 155,000 years ago.

**Neanderthal and Denisovan interbreeding**
**Verdict: CONFIRMED.** Neanderthal DNA in non-African modern humans (~1–4%) is firmly established. Denisovan DNA in Melanesian/Oceanian populations (~3–5%) is firmly established.

**Homo erectus interbreeding**
Shari's claim: modern humans also interbred with Homo erectus.
Wikipedia (Interbreeding between archaic and modern humans, Denisovan article): The Denisovan genome itself contains DNA from an unknown "ghost" archaic population whose divergence time is consistent with a H. erectus-like ancestor. There is also evidence of archaic introgression into African populations from an unidentified archaic source. However, direct H. erectus-to-modern-human gene flow has not been confirmed as a distinct event — the evidence is indirect and the source population is labeled "archaic/ghost" rather than specifically identified as H. erectus.
**Verdict: SPECULATIVE — the underlying phenomenon (archaic ghost introgression) is real science, but the specific attribution to H. erectus is not yet confirmed. A fair way to state it: "modern humans may have interbred with H. erectus-related populations, as evidenced by ghost DNA in Denisovan and some African genomes."**
