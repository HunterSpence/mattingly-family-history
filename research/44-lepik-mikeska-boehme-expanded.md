# Lepik / Lepick + Mikeska + Boehme Lines - Expanded Maternal Research

**Scope:** Jennive Imogene Lepick Mattingly's maternal Lepik/Lepick, Mikeska, and Boehme ancestry

## Bottom line

The best-supported line at present is:

**Jennive Imogene Lepick (1923-2008)**  
daughter of **Fred Charles Lepick Sr. (1894-1954)** and **Hilda Boehme Lepick (1900-1945)**  
granddaughter of **Frank Lepik/Lepick (Bohemia, immigrated 1881)** and **Mary Mikeska (Bohemia, immigrated by 1871 per 1900 census)**  
and of **Herman F. Boehme (1863-1900, Lavaca County/Shiner, Texas)**.

The strongest new finding in this pass is on the **Boehme side**: a cemetery aggregator with internally consistent relationship data identifies **Hilda's mother as Minna Marie Macker, later Luedecke**, which would explain why Hilda's younger half-siblings carried the Luedecke surname. I am treating that as **strong secondary evidence, not final proof**, until matched to a death certificate, church register, or census household.

The strongest immigration evidence in this pass is not a passenger manifest but the **1900 U.S. census**:

- **Frank Lepik** reported immigration in **1881**.
- **Mary Lepik** reported immigration in **1871**.

I did **not** find a ship manifest for either person in this pass.

Frank is the clearest immigrant ancestor in the Lepick surname line. Prior repo research, backed by Brown County marriage records, census citations, and Kansas newspaper citations, places him in **Brown County, Kansas** by the mid-1880s as a Bohemian-born shoemaker.

## Immigration and migration pattern

### Lepik / Mikeska

**Most likely route currently supported by records:**

- **Mary Mikeska** came from Bohemia by **1871** (per 1900 census report)
- **Frank Lepik** came from Bohemia in **1881** (per 1900 census report)
- they married in **Brown County, Kansas** in **1885**
- their family appears in Kansas, then later in **Prairie County, Arkansas** by **1910**
- Fred Sr.'s branch then moved to **Wilson County / San Antonio, Texas**

This is a classic **Bohemian/Czech migration chain into northeastern Kansas**, not a direct Texas Czech settlement from Europe.

Context sources:

- Brown County history: `https://www.kansashistory.gov/kansapedia/brown-county-kansas/15284`
- Everest Catholic-town background: `https://legendsofkansas.com/everest-kansas/`

### Boehme

**Most likely route currently supported by context, not by a found ship record:**

- Herman F. Boehme was born in **Texas in 1863**
- his family therefore belongs to an **earlier German-Texan immigrant generation**
- Shiner/Lavaca County and later Wilson County fit the **South Texas German belt**, not the original core Adelsverein Hill Country settlements

Context sources:

- TSHA on Adelsverein: `https://www.tshaonline.org/handbook/entries/adelsverein`
- TSHA on Shiner: `https://www.tshaonline.org/handbook/entries/shiner-tx`
- TSHA on Wilson County: `https://www.tshaonline.org/handbook/entries/wilson-county`
- TSHA on Sons of Hermann: `https://www.tshaonline.org/handbook/entries/sons-of-hermann`

Interpretation:

- The **Boehme** line looks like an established **Texas-born second-generation German-Texan** line by Herman's 1863 birth.
- The **Lepik/Mikeska** line looks like a later **Bohemian immigrant line** entering through Kansas.

## Early Texas / German / Czech context

### Boehme in South Texas

Useful context, not direct proof:

- TSHA notes that **Shiner** became a town where **Czech and German immigrants soon became the dominant ethnic groups** after the railroad era began.
- TSHA notes that **Wilson County** received **German immigrants from Guadalupe County** and **Polish immigrants from Karnes County** between **1850 and 1860**.
- The **Sons of Hermann** order was founded in **San Antonio on July 6, 1861**, and remained a major German fraternal institution in Texas. Herman's burial in a **Sons of Hermann** cemetery fits that ethnic setting, though it is not by itself proof of denomination.

### Lepik / Mikeska in northeastern Kansas

Useful context, not direct proof:

- Everest's Catholic church community predated the railroad boom and remained substantial after the town's 1882 expansion.
- That setting is consistent with the **1885 Lepik-Mikeska marriage** in the Brown County / Everest Catholic orbit.

## Working conclusion

This branch now resolves into two distinct immigrant stories:

- **Lepik/Mikeska:** late 19th-century **Bohemian/Czech immigration into Brown County, Kansas**, with Frank arriving in **1881** and Mary already in the United States by **1871**.
- **Boehme:** an earlier **German-Texan South Texas line** already rooted in Texas by **Herman F. Boehme's 1863 birth**, probably in the Shiner/Lavaca County German-Czech belt.

The most important unresolved document gap is the lack of a **primary record for Hilda's parents**. The best current working answer is:

**Hilda Boehme Lepick (1900-1945), daughter of Herman F. Boehme and probably Minna Marie Macker, later Luedecke.**

That is the strongest reading of the evidence now on hand, but it still needs one more primary record to become final.
