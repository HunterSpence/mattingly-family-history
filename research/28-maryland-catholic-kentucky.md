# The 1785 Maryland Catholic Migration to Kentucky

**Compiled:** 2026-04-25

## The 1785 League and Migration

### Structure of the Migration

In the year 1785, a formal compact—Webb calls it "a league"—of sixty families was organized in Maryland, all Catholics, "mostly residents of St. Mary's county." Each member pledged to emigrate to Kentucky within a specified time. The goal was mutual protection against Indians and swift establishment of a parish and church (Webb, pp. 28–29). Spalding corroborates: "The Catholic population of Kentucky emigrated almost entirely from Maryland; chiefly from St. Mary's, Charles', and Prince George's Counties" (Spalding, p. 24).

Of the sixty families in the league, twenty-five departed Maryland early in 1785, reaching Kentucky before the end of spring. Their route: overland to Pittsburgh, then flatboats down the Ohio River to Maysville (then called Limestone), chosen to avoid Indian-infested territory above the Falls of the Ohio. From Maysville they marched inland to **Goodwin's Station** near the present town of Boston, Nelson County, the nearest fortified post to their destination. The able-bodied men then located their preassigned land on **Pottinger's Creek**, 10–15 miles from Bardstown (Webb, pp. 28–29; corroborated by *United States Catholic Miscellany*, December 1, 1824, cited in Webb footnote).

The remaining 35 families of the league followed over subsequent years as circumstances permitted. A **larger second colony** traveled with Captain James Rapier in spring 1786. A third came in 1787 with Philip Miles and Thomas Hill. Further waves came in 1788 (Robert Abell), 1790 (Benedict Spalding from St. Mary's County), and 1791 (Leonard Hamilton). After the Treaty of Greenville in 1795 removed the Indian threat, emigration increased significantly (Spalding, pp. 26–27).

Confidence: **CONFIRMED** — two independent primary sources, one of which (Webb) cites a 19th-century newspaper corroboration.

### The "Holy Land of Kentucky" Designation

The term "Holy Land of Kentucky" — used colloquially for the Catholic-dense area of Nelson, Washington, and Marion counties — is not documented in Webb or Spalding under that phrase. Neither Webb (1884) nor Spalding (1844) uses this name. It appears to be a later popular designation. The communities themselves were organized around three geographic clusters: the **Pottinger's Creek settlement** (Washington County), the **Hardin's Creek settlement** (became Marion County), and the **Cartwright's Creek settlement** (Nelson County/Bardstown). These became the three principal Catholic congregations in early Kentucky.

## The Twenty-Five Founding Families: Pottinger's Creek (1785)

Webb states the names of all twenty-five original 1785 families "are not now all certainly known." However, he gives the following list as "reasonably certain" for Pottinger's Creek settlers up to 1800, noting that the first names on the list are "thought to be, in part, those borne by the twenty-five families of the Maryland league":

Basil Hayden (leader), Philip Lee, William Bald, Bernard Cissell, Charles Payne, William Brewer, Leonard Johnson, Henry McAtee, Joseph Clark, Stephen Elliott, James Mollihorne, Henry Norris, Ignatius Cissell, Ignatius Byrne, Randal Hagan, Ignatius Hagan, Jeremiah Brown, Robert Cissell, Ignatius Bowles, Hezekiah Luckett, Stanislaus Melton, Thomas Bowlin, John Baptist Dant, Philip Miles, Harry Hill, John Hutchins, Isaac Thawles, John Spalding, William Mahony, Henry Lucas, William Bowles, John Bowles, James Queen, Bernard Nally, James Stevens, Ignatius French, Washington Boone, Francis Bryan, Jeremiah Wathen, Thomas Mudd, Raphael R. Mudd, **Walter Burch, Philip Mattingly**, Joseph Spalding, James Dant, Joseph Dant, Urban Speaks, Joseph Edelin, Joseph Howe, Joseph Mills, Harry Miles, Monica Hagan, Rodolphus Norris, Francis Peak (Webb, pp. 29–30).

**Basil Hayden** was the acknowledged leader. His land bond for his Kentucky property was signed at Baltimore in 1785, the signed document being of record in the Nelson County clerk's office as of Webb's writing in 1884. Philip Lee was Hayden's security on the bond. The first board of Catholic church trustees in Kentucky—organized for Holy Cross Church, the first Catholic church built in the state (1792)—was William Bald, Bernard Cissell, Charles Payne, and William Brewer (Webb, p. 30).

**Philip Mattingly** appears in this list. He is one of two Mattinglys documented in the 1785 Pottinger's Creek cohort, the other being Walter Burch as a neighbor. Philip Mattingly is separately identified as a resident of Washington County (Webb, p. 47 footnote).

Confidence for Philip Mattingly in the 1785 cohort: **PROBABLE** — Webb includes him in the definitive Pottinger's Creek list but notes not all names on the list were strictly from the 1785 group.

## The Mattingly Families: Hardin's Creek Settlement (1786)

### The Three Brothers

"Early in 1786, the settlement [Hardin's Creek, about ten miles east of Pottinger's Creek] was much strengthened by the addition to its numbers of the families of **William, Leonard, and Lucas Mattingly, three brothers, whose previous homes had been in St. Mary's county, Maryland**. To these three is to be traced the descent of a family connection that is known in every State of the South and West, and is represented by hundreds in the single congregation of St. Charles, in Marion county." (Webb, p. 46)

Webb is explicit: the three brothers arrived **early in 1786**, not 1785. However, he notes that "several of the earliest emigrants to the [Hardin's Creek] district were members of the emigration league of sixty families formed in Maryland" (Webb, p. 46). This makes the three brothers part of the broader 1785 league, even if their personal arrival was 1786.

**William Mattingly** — arrived first of the three. First wife: a Miss Spalding (Maryland); with her he had three sons, James, Edward, and Richard Mattingly. After her death, he married Elizabeth Clark (sister of Joseph Clark, Pottinger's Creek). By his second wife he had ten children: William, Mary, Benjamin, Felix, Ignatius, Julia, George, Susan, Catherine, and John. As of 1879, Felix and John were living in the congregation of St. Charles with large families; Ignatius was a citizen of Bardstown; Julia was known as Sister Theresa of the Loretto Sisterhood. One of George Mattingly's daughters was Sister Mary Charles of Loretto. (Webb, p. 46 and footnote)

**Leonard Mattingly** — "the acknowledged patriarch of the family in Kentucky, must have been past middle life when he came to the State." His children were all born in Maryland: Leonard Jr., Basil, John, William, Joseph, Ignatius, Jane, Margaret, Susan, and one unnamed daughter. He died in 1827, at which time his living descendants were estimated at "nearly three hundred souls." (Webb, pp. 46–47 and footnote)

**Lucas Mattingly** — the third brother. Webb states he learned "absolutely nothing" about Lucas beyond that one of his descendants, **John G. Mattingly**, was living near the village of Manton, Washington County, as a member of Holy Rosary Church congregation. (Webb, p. 47)

Confidence: **CONFIRMED** for all three brothers' arrival via Webb (primary source, 1884).

### Leonard Mattingly Sr.'s Children — Fates Documented

Webb provides the most granular account of Leonard Sr.'s ten Maryland-born children:

- **Leonard Mattingly Jr.** — married a sister of Mathew Cissell. This is the direct ancestor in our lineage (b. 1764 per prior research documents, though Webb gives no birth year for him here).
- **Basil Mattingly** — first wife Monica Miles (sister of Harry Miles of Pottinger's Creek); children: Harriet (married Irvin Buckman), Martha (Sister Generose of Loretto), Austin (relocated to Mississippi), and George (Daviess County, Kentucky). Second wife: Polly Hagan. By Polly: Mahala (married Washington Mattingly), Edward H. (married Alethair Spalding), Nancy (married A.J. Mudd), Mary Jane (married Joseph Spalding, half-brother of Archbishop Spalding), Henry (married Susan Jane Spalding), and Burrilla M. (married J.W. Montgomery). Dr. Ernest Mattingly of Lebanon, Kentucky was a grandson of Basil and Polly. (Webb, pp. 47, 51–52)
- **John Mattingly** — married Polly Fenwick. Six children noted for exceptional Catholic devotion. (Webb, p. 47 footnote)
- **William** (son of Leonard Sr.) — married Henrietta, daughter of Charles Buckman. Son: Dr. C.P. Mattingly of Bardstown. (Webb, p. 47 footnote)
- **Joseph** — married Mary, daughter of Joseph Hayden (sister of Rev. George Hayden, who died in Texas ~1840s). (Webb, p. 47 footnote)
- **Ignatius** — married a Miss Fowler. (Webb, p. 47 footnote)
- **Margaret** (daughter) — one of the founding band of the Sisterhood of Loretto (1812). (Webb, pp. 47, 51)
- **Nancy** (daughter) — wished to follow Margaret into Loretto but was a hopeless invalid; wore the habit at home. (Webb, p. 47 footnote)
- **Susan** — married John Thomas. (Webb, p. 47 footnote)
- **Elizabeth** — married John Miles. (Webb, p. 47 footnote)
- **Polly** (the unnamed daughter recovered by name) — married Basil Payne. (Webb, p. 47 footnote)
- **Jane** (eldest daughter) — married Charles Russell, progenitor of all Russells in St. Charles congregation; through his son Ignatius, became grandfather of Rev. David Russell of the Diocese of Louisville. (Webb, p. 47 footnote)
- **Benjamin F. Mattingly** (son of John) — married Susan Mary Graves. Children included John G. and Benjamin F. Mattingly Jr., noted as "business men of enterprise and integrity" with "large distillery interests in Louisville" as of 1879. (Webb, p. 47 footnote — note: this is John's son, not Leonard Sr.'s direct son)

## Richard Mattingly of Breckinridge County (Separate Branch)

**Richard Mattingly**, born in Maryland in 1756, removed to Kentucky in 1791 and settled on Long Lick, within what became Breckinridge County. He did not travel with the 1785 cohort. His nine children: John, Margaret, Zachariah, Edward, William, Thomas, Mary, Elizabeth, and Sarah. By 1810, twenty families of his posterity were living in Breckinridge County. His house served as the church station for the neighborhood until Father Charles Nerinckx built a church on Long Lick around 1818. (Webb, pp. 141–142)

In 1795, **Barton Mattingly** came from Maryland with Elias Rhodes and settled on Long Lick. (Webb, p. 142)

**Zachariah Mattingly** (son of Richard) was the first resident Catholic of Hardinsburg. He likely donated the ground for the church and cemetery of what became St. Romuald's. He and Henry Beaven were the only two Catholic families between the Long Lick settlement and Cloverport in 1810. Ignatius and Joseph Mattingly of Long Lick assisted in getting out the heavy timbers for the first log chapel, begun 1810, completed 1816. (Webb, pp. 142–143)

Webb notes in a footnote: "The Mattinglys of Maryland and Kentucky are evidently not of one lineage." He distinguishes between the darker-complexioned descendants of Leonard Mattingly (Hardin's Creek) and the lighter-complexioned descendants identified with Joseph, Philip (Washington County), and Richard Mattingly (Breckinridge County). (Webb, p. 47 footnote)

## Ignatius Mattingly in Bardstown (Nelson County)

**Ignatius Mattingly** is documented as a resident of Bardstown town proper as of approximately 1836, listed among Catholic residents there. His age and specific lineage are not given by Webb but he is placed in Nelson County. He appears to be the same Ignatius Mattingly who was a son of William Mattingly (first of the three brothers) and his second wife Elizabeth Clark — still living in Bardstown in 1879 per Webb's footnote on William's children. (Webb, pp. 47 footnote, 255)

## Dispersal: Mattinglys Who Left Kentucky

The Webb documentation identifies the following Mattinglys who dispersed outside Kentucky:

- **Austin Mattingly** (son of Basil and Monica Miles) — relocated to Mississippi (CONFIRMED, Webb footnote)
- **George Mattingly** (another son of Basil) — Daviess County, Kentucky (stayed in state but moved west from Marion County) (CONFIRMED)
- **Benjamin F. Mattingly Jr. and John G. Mattingly** (grandsons of Leonard Sr. via John) — maintained residences near St. Charles but had large distillery interests in Louisville (CONFIRMED, Webb 1879)
- **Mary (married John J. Mattingly)** — living at Florissant, Missouri in 1879 (from the Hill family marriage connection) (CONFIRMED)
- **Richard H. [Hill, not Mattingly]** — removed to Texas, alive 1879 with descendants to third generation (this is a Hill-family member who married into Mattingly network, not a Mattingly by surname) (CONFIRMED for Texas presence, not a Mattingly)

Webb's footnote states: "There are twenty families of Richard Mattingly's posterity living to-day [1884] in the lower part of Breckinridge county." This confirms the Breckinridge branch remained predominantly in Kentucky.

The broader Hardin's Creek (Leonard Sr.) branch had dispersed by 1884 to "every State of the South and West" (Webb's exact words), but Webb names only Mississippi and Louisville specifically for traceable individuals.

## Mattingly & Moore Distillery (Bardstown, 1877)

The Webb connection to this distillery is established but indirect. Webb (1884) identifies John G. and Benjamin F. Mattingly Jr. — grandsons of Leonard Mattingly Sr. via his son John — as men "widely known and respected... as business men of enterprise and integrity" who "both have large distillery interests in Louisville" (Webb, p. 47 footnote, writing of conditions in 1879).

The Mattingly & Moore Distillery in Bardstown (established 1877) is attributed in bourbon history sources to B.F. (Benjamin Franklin) Mattingly, who founded it with T.J. Moore. The 1877 date and B.F. Mattingly founder name are consistent with Webb's 1879 description of Benjamin F. Mattingly Jr. as a prominent distillery businessman. A 1963-vintage Mattingly & Moore bottle is documented as "distilled in Lawrenceburg, Indiana (now MGP)" in the Seagrams era, indicating the brand had passed out of Mattingly family ownership by mid-20th century.

**Direct documentary confirmation** of the 1877 founding date from a primary source has not been located in the material reviewed. The connection from Leonard Mattingly Sr. → John Mattingly → Benjamin F. Mattingly (married Susan Mary Graves) → John G. and B.F. Mattingly Jr. (distillery interests, 1879) represents a **PROBABLE** genealogical line to the 1877 distillery, pending confirmation of B.F. Mattingly Jr.'s full name and the 1877 date in a primary source (Kentucky corporate records, newspaper, or distillery license).

## Leonard Mattingly Jr. (1764–1843): His Position in the Community

Leonard Mattingly Jr. was the eldest son of Leonard Mattingly Sr. (patriarch) and was "past middle life" when his father arrived in 1786. Webb's great-grandson informant notes that Leonard Jr. married "a sister of Mathew Cissell" — meaning he married into the Cissell family, one of the most prominent Catholic families in the Hardin's Creek settlement. The Cissells (Mathew, Zachariah, Sylvester, and Jeremiah) were among the earliest Maryland Catholics to reach Hardin's Creek and were described as men of rare intelligence and influence. By marrying into this family, Leonard Jr. was embedded in the leadership stratum of the settlement.

Leonard Jr.'s death year from prior research documents is given as 1843, Marion County. Webb confirms his father died in 1827 with approximately 300 living descendants at that time. By 1843 when Leonard Jr. died, that number would have grown substantially. His four confirmed Kentucky-born children (Mary Alvey, Henry Martin Mattingly Sr., William Cissell Mattingly, Leonard Mattingly III) are the Generation 6 anchors established in research document `10-kentucky-to-texas.md`.
