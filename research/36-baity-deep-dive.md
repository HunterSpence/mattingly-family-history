## Executive Summary

The Baity branch is **genuinely colonial-era North Carolina**, with verifiable presence by the **1753 NC Land Grant** era in what is now Yadkin/Surry County (then Rowan County). The deepest primary-source Baity ancestor directly cited in NC court or deed records is **George Baity/Batee/Baty** (adult by 1774 in Rowan County), with a plausible but unconfirmed line extending back to a shared Beatty ancestor who arrived in North Carolina by 1753.

The surname is a **Scots-Irish (Ulster Scots) spelling** of the Border Scottish surname **Beatty / Beattie / Beaty**, derived from the personal name Bartholomew (pet form "Bate" or "Baty"). The name spread from the Scottish/English Border into Ulster (particularly counties Down, Tyrone, Antrim, and Fermanagh) during the Plantation of Ulster (1610–1640), and then emigrated to Pennsylvania in the 1720s–1730s before moving south along the Great Wagon Road into NC by the early 1750s.

## 5. Surname Origin: Beatty / Beaty / Beatie / Baty / Baity

### Etymology (`CONFIRMED by multiple reference sources`)

- **Primary meaning:** The surname **Beatty** derives from **Bate** or **Batey**, diminutive/pet forms of the personal name **Bartholomew**. Not from "Beatrice" as often assumed.
- **Border surname:** Beatty is classified as a "Border surname" — found equally in counties on both sides of the Scotland–England border (Northumberland, Dumfries, Roxburgh, Lanark). Source: SurnameDB, citing Reaney & Wilson *Dictionary of English and Welsh Surnames*.
- **Spelling variants documented on the Scottish/English Border:** Baetie, Baitie, Baittie, Batie, Baty, Batye, Baytie, Bette. Source: HouseOfNames, citing *Dictionary of English and Welsh Surnames* (Heraldry Today, 1901, ISBN 0-900455-44-6).
- **Wikipedia summary (CONFIRMED):** "Beatty is a surname of Scottish and Irish origin. In some cases, it was derived from the given name Bartholomew, which had diminutives including Bate or Baty." Source: `https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beatty_(surname)`
- **Baity specifically:** The "Baity" spelling is an American Southern phonetic rendering of "Beaty/Beatie" as spoken by Scots-Irish settlers. By the 1800s the NC branch consistently uses "Baity" in all records (court, deed, census, cemetery). The transition is documented in the pre-1800 Rowan/Surry records where the name appears as Batee, Baty, Beaty, Batey, and Baity interchangeably.

### Ulster Origin (`PROBABLE`)

- The Beatty/Beattie surname concentrated in **Ulster** (Northern Ireland) during and after the **Plantation of Ulster (1610–1640)**, when Scots families from the Border counties received land grants in counties Down, Antrim, Tyrone, Armagh, Fermanagh, and Cavan.
- **County Down** is the most common origin for Beatty families in Ulster, particularly around Strangford Lough and the Ards Peninsula, which received heavy Scots plantation settlers from Galloway and Ayrshire.
- The Wikipedia article on the Beatty surname states the name originated in "Scotland and Ireland" with the Scottish Borders and Ulster as twin distribution centers.
- The BeattyProject.org (dedicated Beatty DNA surname project) confirms the name's Scots-Irish background and documents DNA research connecting NC/PA Beattys to Ulster lines.
- **Most likely counties of origin for the NC/PA Beatty branch:** County Down, County Tyrone, or County Fermanagh (based on the dominant Ulster Beatty distribution and the typical PA-corridor Scots-Irish settlement pattern).

### Migration Corridor: Ulster → Pennsylvania → NC (`PROBABLE`)

The documented and well-established Scots-Irish migration pathway:

1. **Ulster (1610–1720):** Scots planters (many from Galloway, Ayrshire, Lanark) settle County Down, Tyrone, Fermanagh
2. **Pennsylvania (1718–1740):** Mass Scots-Irish emigration from Ulster ports (Londonderry, Belfast) to Philadelphia and New Castle, Delaware; settle Lancaster, Chester, Cumberland, and Bucks counties PA
3. **Shenandoah Valley (1730s–1750s):** PA Scots-Irish press south down the Great Wagon Road into Virginia's Shenandoah Valley
4. **North Carolina backcountry (1740s–1760s):** Continue south through the Valley of Virginia into the NC Piedmont — specifically to **Rowan County (formed 1753)** along the Yadkin River valley
5. **Surry and Yadkin Counties (1771+):** As population grew, Surry was split from Rowan in 1771; the Baity cluster is in what becomes Yadkin County (split from Surry in 1850)

The 1753 Beatty land grant in Rowan County places this family among the very first settlers in that county — consistent with a family that had been in PA since the 1729 Charles Beatty immigration, then moved south.

**Source (Great Wagon Road migration pattern):** Wikipedia, *Great Wagon Road* `https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Wagon_Road`  
**Source (Surry County formation):** Wikipedia, *Surry County, NC* — "formed in 1771 from Rowan County"  
**Source (Rowan County formation):** Wikipedia, *Rowan County, NC* — "Established March 27, 1753 from the upper part of Anson County"

## 7. Religious Affiliation

The Yadkin County Baity cluster is associated with **Courtney Baptist Church** and **Deep Creek Baptist Church** — both Baptist congregations. This is consistent with the general pattern of Scots-Irish settlers in the NC Piedmont, where many Presbyterians converted to Baptist affiliation by the late 18th and early 19th centuries during the Great Awakening revivals. The earlier Beatty immigrants in PA (including Rev. Charles Beatty) were Presbyterian, but by the time the Yadkin branch is documented, they are Baptist.

## 8. Key Open Research Questions

### Q1: Who is Isham Baity's father?

The 1830 Surry County census shows David Baity as a separate head of household in the same cluster as Isham. If David is Isham's father (David b. c. 1770–1785, Isham b. 1804), David would have been a young child during the 1774 Rowan County court appearance by George Baity/Batee. The most plausible reconstruction is **George → David → Isham**, but this requires a census household or probate/deed record to confirm.

**Best next step:** 1810 and 1820 Surry County census images (not yet checked online — available at FamilySearch or NC State Archives) showing a David Baity household with a son named Isham.

### Q2: Are the 1753 Rowan County grantees (Charles and Thomas Beatty) the family founders?

The 800-acre grant is large (a prime colonial land claim) and was issued in the first year of Rowan County's existence. If Charles Beatty arrived in PA in 1729 and moved to NC by 1753, he would have been in his late 30s — exactly the right age to be establishing a large family farm. Thomas is likely his brother.

**Best next step:** Rowan County deed and court records 1753–1770 for Charles, Thomas, and Francis Beatty. Available at NC State Archives (Raleigh) under Rowan County collection.

### Q3: William Alexander Baity's specific birth date and parents

**Best next step:** Texas death certificate for William Alexander Baity (may name his parents and NC county of birth). Also: 1870/1880 NC census showing William Alexander as a child in a Baity household.

### Q4: Reeves County land purchase April 1901

The oral history says Pearl bought Reeves County land and retained the mineral rights. This requires the original deed or GLO record.

## 11. Pre-1750 Assessment

**What can be stated without fabrication:**

The surname Beatty/Baity traces to the **Scottish/English Border** (Northumberland, Dumfries-shire, Roxburghshire) and spread to **Ulster** during the **Plantation period (1610–1640)**. The name is especially concentrated in **County Down** and **County Tyrone** in Ulster. Scots-Irish Beatty families emigrated heavily to **Pennsylvania** starting in the 1720s (documented immigrant "Charles Beatty" arrived 1729 per Filby's index). From Pennsylvania they moved south along the **Great Wagon Road** through the Shenandoah Valley into **Rowan County, NC by 1753** — confirmed by the NC Land Grant record.

**What cannot be stated:**

- The specific Ulster townland or parish of origin for the NC/TX Baity family cannot be identified from free-access records without ScotlandsPeople or PRONI paid searches
- The direct genealogical link between the 1753 NC grantees (Charles/Thomas Beatty) and the 1774 George Baity/Batee is a research inference, not a proven chain
- Any specific Scottish parish of origin (likely Galloway or Ayrshire based on the Ulster Down distribution, but unconfirmed)
