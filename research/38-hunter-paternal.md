## 5. Why Hunter Has a UK Passport

Hunter holds both a US passport and a UK passport. Given that:
- Rachel (mother) is an American citizen (daughter of Shari, who is from Texas)
- Rachel's father David Trifon is American (adopted surname, US context)
- **Dale William Spence Sr. is an English immigrant** (confirmed)

The most probable explanation for Hunter's UK passport is **citizenship by descent through his father's line**:

Under the British Nationality Act 1981, a child born to a British citizen parent (or a father who was a British citizen at time of birth) acquires British citizenship. If Dr. Dale William Spence Sr. was a British national who immigrated to the US, his son Dale Jr. would be British by descent. If Dale Jr. retained British citizenship and Hunter was born while Dale Jr. was a British citizen, Hunter would be entitled to register as British.

This is further supported by the Spence surname being distinctly English/Scottish — the surname traces to medieval England (Yorkshire, 1300) and Lowland Scotland (Clan Spens of Fife, documented from 1161). An English immigrant named "Dr. Dale William Spence Sr." arriving in Texas and teaching at Rice University is entirely consistent with a British academic coming to the US on an academic or professional visa in the mid-20th century and raising a family there.

**Confidence:** This is PROBABLE reasoning from the confirmed facts (English immigrant father) but is not confirmed by a primary record. It would need: (a) Dale Sr.'s immigration record, (b) Dale Jr.'s citizenship status, or (c) Hunter's birth certificate showing his parents' nationalities.

## 8. What Remains From 26-spence-paternal.md (Historical Context)

The earlier research in 26-spence-paternal.md documented the general Spence surname history, notable Spences, and the Texas Spence families. That research is **historically valid** but needs to be reframed: it is surname history, not necessarily Hunter's direct ancestry. Key points that remain relevant:

- The Spence surname derives from Old French *despense* (pantry-keeper), first documented in Yorkshire in 1300, with major Scottish adoption by Clan Spens of Fife (documented from 1161, Ragman Rolls 1296)
- The name came to colonial Virginia from 1623 onward, with multiple independent immigration streams
- If Dr. Dale William Spence Sr. immigrated from England, his Spence line likely connects to the English Spence families (Yorkshire → Midlands) rather than the Scottish line, though both streams are possible
