## 2. Reeves County, Texas (oil land, purchased April 1901)

**Confidence: CONFIRMED** (3+ sources: Wikipedia, TSHA Handbook, OSM)

**Key facts:**
- County seat: **Pecos, TX**. Created April 14, 1883; organized November 4, 1884. Named for Confederate Colonel George R. Reeves.
- Trans-Pecos West Texas, borders New Mexico. 2,626 square miles. Average 10 inches of rain/year. Elevation 2,538–4,210 ft.
- **1900 census population: 1,847** (including 474 foreign-born). 1890 population was 1,247. Shari's "there must have been two people" is poetic but grounded in fact — Pearl was buying raw West Texas ranch/grazing land, not a settled community.
- The Texas Pacific Railway built through the county in 1881; Pecos had about 150 residents in 1885. By 1901 land was cheap, speculative, and the area was primarily open-range ranching with subsistence farming.
- **Between 1901 and 1905, Texas law permitted sale of school lands in West Texas at generous credit terms** — Pearl's April 1901 purchase placed her at the very start of this land-rush window, within weeks/months of Spindletop (January 10, 1901). Savvy timing.
- 1960 all-time population high: 17,644. **2020 census: 14,748** — Shari's "18,000" figure is close but a slight overcount; the peak was 17,644 in 1960 and population has declined since. Current figure may include recent Permian Basin worker influx (not captured in 2020 census yet).
- Oil development: smaller Toyah and Geraldine-Ford fields discovered 1952-1956. Major Permian Basin Wolfcamp shale activity began post-2010.

**Sources:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reeves_County,_Texas | https://www.tshaonline.org/handbook/entries/reeves-county

## 3. Wolfcamp Shale / Permian Basin (Reeves County oil royalties)

**Confidence: CONFIRMED** (USGS factsheet + Wikipedia + Cline Shale article, 3 sources)

**Key facts:**
- The Wolfcamp Shale (also called Cline Shale, Lower Wolfcamp, Spraberry-Wolfcamp) underlies the Permian Basin in West Texas including **Reeves County**.
- **November 2016:** USGS published its assessment estimating **20 billion barrels of technically recoverable oil** — the largest continuous oil and gas resource assessment in USGS history at that time, nearly 3x the 2013 Bakken assessment. Also 16 trillion cubic feet of natural gas.
- The USGS factsheet is at: https://pubs.usgs.gov/fs/2016/3092/fs20163092.pdf (confirmed live, 915KB PDF, last modified 2017-03-17)
- Shari's characterization as "the largest field ever found, it's all of West Texas" is broadly accurate — the Permian Basin is the highest-producing US oil field at 4.2M barrels/day (2019 figure).
- Hydraulic fracturing (fracking) unlocked the formation post-2008; commercial royalty payments to mineral rights holders like Pearl Baity's heirs would realistically have started arriving **2010-2012**, consistent with Shari saying she started receiving royalties in 2010.
- Pearl's decision to **retain mineral rights when selling surface rights in the 1920s** was the key legal maneuver. At the time, Reeves County had small conventional oil finds in the 1920s that attracted interest; she or her advisors were clearly thinking ahead.

**Sources:** https://pubs.usgs.gov/fs/2016/3092/ | https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cline_Shale | https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permian_Basin_(North_America)

## 4. Spindletop (January 10, 1901)

**Confidence: CONFIRMED** (Wikipedia, multiple cross-references)

**Key facts:**
- Spindletop gusher struck oil **January 10, 1901** at 10:30 AM. Location: southern Beaumont, Jefferson County, TX (coordinates 30.02°N, 94.07°W — Gulf Coast, not West Texas).
- The Lucas No. 1 well blew for **9 days** at an estimated 100,000 barrels/day.
- Launched the Texas oil age; Gulf Oil and Texaco were both formed as a result.
- Prior to Spindletop, oil was primarily a lighting fuel and lubricant. Post-Spindletop, petroleum as mass-consumption fuel became economically viable.
- Pearl Baity bought Reeves County land approximately 3 months after Spindletop. The news of Spindletop would have been national and immediate. Buying cheap West Texas land with oil speculation in mind — months after the first major Texas gusher — shows Pearl was either well-connected or exceptionally alert to the opportunity.

**Wikipedia image:** https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Lucas_gusher.jpg/330px-Lucas_gusher.jpg
**Source:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spindletop

## 7. Galveston, Texas (1880 immigration port)

**Confidence: CONFIRMED** as a major immigration port through the 1880s (TSHA); specific 1880 arrival records UNVERIFIED

**Key facts:**
- Galveston was the **largest Texas city in both 1870 (pop. 13,818) and 1880 (pop. 22,248)**. It was the state's primary port and economic hub until the 1900 hurricane.
- It served as a significant immigrant entry point through the late 19th century. The formal **Galveston Movement** (1907–1914) directed 10,000 Jewish immigrants from Eastern Europe through the port, but Galveston had been receiving European immigrants for decades before that.
- Cotton left, immigrants came in (per TSHA): "cotton moved outward, and farming supplies and immigrants came in."
- Immigrants arriving in Galveston in 1880 and "settling in the central part of Texas" is historically consistent — central Texas (Austin, San Antonio, San Marcos corridor) was the primary inland destination for European immigrants via Galveston, particularly Germans and Central Europeans.
- **Ship manifests for 1880 Galveston arrivals** can be searched at Ancestry.com (passenger lists) and the Galveston and Texas History Center (Rosenberg Library): https://www.gthcenter.org/

**Texas governor connection note:** No specific governor with a provable Galveston-area family connection matching the family's timeframe was identified without knowing the relative's name or the specific governor. Governors in office 1880-1910 include Oran Roberts, John Ireland, Lawrence Sullivan Ross, James Hogg, Charles Culberson, Joseph Sayers — all Central/East Texas figures.

**Source:** https://www.tshaonline.org/handbook/entries/galveston-tx | https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galveston_Movement

## 8. Mark Twain's 1887 "Louisiana Speaking Tour"

**Confidence: POSSIBLE — with a significant caveat** (1887 activities are documented; no Louisiana appearance found)

**What the speech chronology shows for 1887:**
Based on the authoritative *Chronology of Known Mark Twain Speeches, Public Readings, and Lectures* compiled by Barbara Schmidt (twainquotes.com), Mark Twain's **confirmed 1887 appearances were entirely in the Northeast**:

| Date | Venue | Location |
|------|-------|----------|
| Feb 10 | Stationers Board of Trade, Hotel Brunswick | New York City |
| Feb 26 | Monday Evening Club | Hartford, CT |
| Mar 17 | The Kinsmen | New York City |
| Mar 31 | Authors Readings (Longfellow Memorial) | Boston, MA |
| Apr 1 | Trinity College | Hartford, CT |
| Apr 8 | Union Veterans Association of Maryland | Baltimore, MD |
| Apr 27 | Army and Navy Club of CT | Hartford, CT |
| Apr 30 | U.S. Military Academy | West Point, NY |
| Nov-Dec | Various | New York City / Boston / Hartford |

**No Louisiana appearance is listed for 1887.** In 1887 Twain was based in Hartford, CT, working on *A Connecticut Yankee in King Arthur's Court* (published 1889). His major lecture tours were:
- 1869–1873: Eastward from California
- 1884–1885: Reading tour with George Washington Cable (which *did* include New Orleans and the South — Cable was from New Orleans)
- 1895–1896: World tour to pay bankruptcy debts

**The 1884-85 tour is the most likely candidate.** Twain and Cable toured extensively in the South including New Orleans in January 1885. If Monette was born 1871, she would have been 13 in early 1885, not 16. If she was 16, the year would be 1887, which does not match a documented Louisiana appearance. One of three things:
1. The year/age is slightly off in family memory (she was ~13-14, not 16)
2. There was an undocumented local Louisiana appearance in 1887 (possible for smaller private engagements)
3. The family was visiting New Orleans/Louisiana and encountered Twain incidentally rather than at a formal lecture tour

**Source:** http://www.twainquotes.com/SpeechIndex.html (Barbara Schmidt, *Chronology of Known Mark Twain Speeches, Public Readings, and Lectures*)
