## Q1: Herman's Parents (G6 — Hunter's 3x-great-grandparents)

### Status: UNVERIFIED — Names Not Found in Any Accessible Record

No primary record (census, church register, naturalization paper, death certificate) has been located in this research pass that names Herman F. Boehme's parents.

**Why this gap exists:**
- Herman was born in 1863 in Texas — before statewide vital records registration
- He died in 1900 — before Texas's 1903 death certificate program began
- The 1870 and 1880 US Federal Census records for Lavaca County are microfilm only; FamilySearch's indexed search engine was blocked by bot protection during this research pass
- USGenWeb Lavaca County archives contain 1850 and 1860 census transcriptions but no 1870 or 1880 text transcriptions — only GIF images

- PeopleLegacy's Herman page lists only his burial location and 6 children, no parents
- WikiTree, FindAGrave, and Ancestry were all blocked by Cloudflare during this research pass

**Best method to resolve:**
1. **1870 US Census, Lavaca County TX** — Herman would be ~7 years old in his parents' household. Collection: NARA M593, roll 1595; FamilySearch collection ID 1438024. Access via FamilySearch with login.
2. **1880 US Census, Lavaca County TX** — Herman ~17 years old, parents likely still present. NARA T9, Texas schedules.
3. **Walter John Boehme death certificate (1972), Lavaca County TX** — Walter was Herman's son (b. 1894, d. 1972). Texas death certificates issued after 1903 list the deceased's parents. Walter's 1972 certificate would name his father as Herman F. Boehme and his mother as Minna Marie Macker. The parents section of Texas death certificates also sometimes carries the grandparents' birthplace. Request from Texas DSHS Vital Statistics. Certificate number resolvable via DSHS index.
4. **Edgar Charles Boehme death certificate (1967), Shiner, Lavaca County TX** — Edgar was another of Herman's sons (b. 1889). Buried Shiner Catholic Cemetery. Same rationale.
5. **Frieda Augusta Boehme Donnerberg death certificate (1963), Poth, Wilson County TX** — Frieda is buried at Saint Johns Lutheran Cemetery, Poth. Her death cert may list Herman as father and Minna as mother.
6. **Lavaca County marriage records, Lavaca County Courthouse, Hallettsville TX** — Herman and Minna's marriage record (expected ~1886–1888, before Frieda's 1888 birth) would give both parties' ages and fathers' names in Texas marriage registrations.
7. **Sons of Hermann Lodge records, Lodge No. ??, Shiner TX** — Herman was a member of the Sons of Hermann (confirmed by cemetery). Lodge membership applications often recorded birthplace and parents. Contact: Grand Lodge of Texas Sons of Hermann, San Antonio.
8. **St. Paul Lutheran Church, Shiner TX, or predecessor rural church records** — Lutheran baptism registers for the 1860s–1880s would show Herman's baptism and his parents' names. The church was founded in the 1890s; predecessor congregation records may be held by the Lutheran Church–Missouri Synod Texas District Archives.

## Q3: Province of German Origin

### Status: PROBABLE — Prussian Silesia (Schlesien) with secondary possibility of Saxony

This is the central research question. No ship manifest or naturalization record naming the Boehme family's German origin has been located. The evidence is convergent circumstantial rather than documentary.

### Evidence Supporting Prussian Silesia (Schlesien) as Province

**1. Breslau, Texas — Named After the Prussian City of Breslau (Wroclaw, Silesia) — CONFIRMED**

Breslau is a community 7 miles northwest of Hallettsville in north central Lavaca County. The TSHA Handbook of Texas states explicitly: "The community, named in honor of the Prussian city of Breslau (now Wroclaw, Poland), prospered, and in 1880 a post office was established... German immigrants moved into the area and established small farms to grow cotton... donated land for a school and a Lutheran church."

Source: TSHA Handbook, "Breslau, TX" `https://www.tshaonline.org/handbook/entries/breslau-tx` (Jeff Carroll, updated 2023). CONFIRMED.

This is the most direct evidence: German immigrants in Lavaca County specifically named a community after the capital of Prussian Silesia. Immigrant communities almost universally named their settlements after their places of origin. The Breslau settlers almost certainly came from the Breslau/Wroclaw region of Prussian Silesia.

**2. The Böhme/Boehme Surname — Silesian Concentration — CONFIRMED (surname etymology)**

The surname Böhme (Boehme) derives from the Old German word "Boheim" meaning "a Bohemian." HouseOfNames confirms: "The name Boehme, which is a local surname, reveals that the original bearer of the name once lived, held land, or was born in the historic kingdom of Bohemia."

Critically: the Böhme surname was concentrated in areas of Germany immediately adjacent to Bohemia — primarily Saxony and Silesia. Jakob Böhme (1575–1624), the most famous historical bearer of this surname, was born in Görlitz, Saxony-Silesia border. The name was particularly common around Breslau/Wroclaw (Silesia) and the Lausitz/Lusatia region where Saxony, Silesia, and Bohemia meet. A German family named "Böhme" living in Silesia (near Bohemia) is exactly the expected origin pattern.

Source: HouseOfNames `https://www.houseofnames.com/boehme-family-crest`. CONFIRMED (surname etymology).

**3. TSHA Lavaca County Article — "Eastern Provinces" Wave — CONFIRMED (geographic context)**

The TSHA Handbook of Texas ("Lavaca County") states: "the 1890 census listed 4,402 foreign-born residents, with the largest contingents from Germany (1,884) and Austria (1,748)." The TSHA Germans article confirms: "during these years, larger numbers of colonists from the eastern provinces of Germany began arriving in Texas."

Silesia was an "eastern province" of Prussia. The Lavaca County German-born population of 1,884 in 1890 placed it in the German Belt's non-Hill-Country tier — exactly where Silesian settlers, who avoided the Adelsverein Hill Country, would have landed.

Source: TSHA "Lavaca County" and TSHA "Germans" `https://www.tshaonline.org/handbook/entries/lavaca-county`, `https://www.tshaonline.org/handbook/entries/germans`. CONFIRMED.

**4. Lutheran Church — Missouri Synod Connection — PROBABLE**

Herman F. Boehme was buried in the Sons of Hermann Cemetery and his daughter Frieda was buried at Saint Johns Lutheran Cemetery, Poth (CONFIRMED). The Missouri Synod was the dominant Lutheran body in the German Texas Belt. The TSHA notes that the Missouri Synod in Texas grew from the Wend colony of Lusatia (Saxony/Prussia border) and from Saxony-derived Lutheran settlers. Silesian Lutherans (Old Lutherans who resisted the Prussian Union of 1817) were among the most dedicated emigrants to America. Several Old Lutheran congregations from Silesia established themselves in Missouri and then spread to Texas via the Missouri Synod network.

Source: TSHA "Lutheran Church" `https://www.tshaonline.org/handbook/entries/lutheran-church`. PROBABLE.

### Alternative Province: Saxony

Saxony is the second-most-likely province. The TSHA Germans article identifies "West central Germany, particularly Nassau, southern Hanover, Brunswick, Hesse, and western Thuringia" as the Adelsverein wave. The postbellum wave (which most likely included Herman's parents) drew from eastern Germany including Saxony. Jakob Böhme himself was from Görlitz, which was in the Kingdom of Saxony at his time but later became part of Prussian Silesia. The Lausitz/Lusatia region, where the Wend/Sorb colony settled in Texas, spans Saxony and Silesia. A Böhme family from Saxony is plausible.

However, the direct naming of a Lavaca County community "Breslau" after the Silesian capital makes Prussian Silesia the more probable origin for this specific area's German settlers.

### Provinces Assessed as Less Likely

- **Westphalia / Nassau / Holstein / Oldenburg**: These were the Adelsverein wave provinces (1845–1853). The Boehme family settled in Lavaca County, NOT in the Adelsverein Hill Country (Fredericksburg, New Braunfels, Mason). Their settlement zone is wrong for this wave.
- **Bavaria**: No Bavarian community indicators in Lavaca County. The Bavarian wave went primarily to the Hill Country or to specific Catholic communities. Herman's Lutheran affiliation argues against Bavarian origin.
- **Brandenburg / Pomerania**: Possible but no community naming evidence in Lavaca County points to these provinces.
- **Bohemia**: Despite the surname meaning "Bohemian," the family is almost certainly ethnic German (Lutheran, German surname) not Czech/Bohemian. The TSHA distinguishes: "Germany (1,884) and Austria (1,748)" — the Austria figure covers Bohemian Czechs (who settled Moravia TX, Czech communities), while "Germany" covers ethnic Germans. The Boehmes are in the German column.

## Q4: Ship Name and Arrival Port

### Status: UNVERIFIED — No Ship Manifest Located

**Most likely arrival port: Indianola (Calhoun County, TX), 1855–1862**

The TSHA confirms that Indianola was "the chief port through which European and American immigrants flowed into western Texas." All German settlers bound for the Lavaca/DeWitt/Victoria County area (the South Texas German Belt) entered via Indianola on Matagorda Bay, then traveled the Old Indianola Trail to San Antonio. The Yorktown TSHA article confirms: "Many German immigrants traveled this route, the Old Indianola Trail."

**Why Galveston is less likely (though possible):** Galveston was the main port for the Hill Country Adelsverein settlers. Lavaca County settlers generally used Indianola. However, Galveston became more important after Indianola's 1875 hurricane destruction. Since Herman's parents arrived ~1855–1862, Indianola is more probable than Galveston.

**Why the ship manifest is unfound:** The pre-1875 Indianola passenger manifests are largely lost due to the 1875 hurricane that obliterated the city. The Hamburg Emigrant Lists (Auswandererlisten Hamburg, 1850–1934) are the most comprehensive source for this period but require login access to Ancestry or FamilySearch. The Bremer Auswandererbriefe (Bremen passenger lists) were largely destroyed in WWII.

**Next steps:** Search the Hamburg Emigrant Lists via Ancestry.com for surname "Boehme" or "Böhme" with Texas/America destination, 1853–1862. Access: Ancestry.com collection ID 1068 (Hamburg Emigration Lists).

**Missouri Synod vs. Old Lutheran distinction:** The TSHA confirms that the Missouri Synod spread through Texas primarily via the Wend colony (Lusatia, Saxony/Prussia) and Saxony-derived settlers. If the Boehme family came from Silesia (where Old Lutherans resisted the Prussian Union), they may have been Old Lutherans who joined the Missouri Synod network upon arriving in Texas — which is exactly what many Silesian and Saxon Old Lutheran emigrants did. This is consistent.

**Iowa Synod possibility:** The Texas Synod joined the Iowa Synod in 1895. Some Lavaca County Lutheran churches were under Iowa Synod rather than Missouri Synod. This is unresolved.

## Q7: Why Lavaca County

### Status: CONFIRMED (settlement pattern)

Lavaca County fit the postbellum German immigration pattern perfectly:
1. **Entry port:** Indianola (pre-1875) or Galveston (post-1875)
2. **Route:** Old Indianola Trail northwest toward San Antonio
3. **Land availability:** Post-Civil War, large Anglo-American plantations in Lavaca County were being broken up and sold to new immigrant farmers (confirmed by TSHA entries for Breslau, Wied, Witting — all note that "German immigrants replaced the original occupants" in the 1865–1875 period)
4. **German community infrastructure:** By the late 1860s–1870s, Lavaca County had German stores, fraternal lodges, Lutheran and Catholic churches
5. **Sons of Hermann:** Founded in San Antonio 1861; by the 1870s–1880s was active in Lavaca County as a mutual aid/insurance society. Herman's burial there confirms his parents were established in the community before his death in 1900.

The specific township of Shiner (western Lavaca County) was not formally established until the 1887 railroad arrived. Before that, German settlers in the Shiner area lived in dispersed farm communities. Herman's parents likely farmed in the rural German communities west of Hallettsville in the 1860s–1870s before Shiner became a town.

## Boehme Surname Distribution Note

The surname Böhme is most heavily concentrated in east-central Germany, specifically in a belt running from Saxony through Silesia. The most famous historical Böhme — Jakob Böhme, the mystic philosopher — was born 1575 in Görlitz, then part of the Kingdom of Saxony but on the Silesian border. By the time of the great German-Texas emigration waves (1845–1880), this surname was well established in both Saxony and Prussian Silesia. A Texas-German Boehme family from the Breslau area of Silesia would perfectly explain: (1) the surname, (2) the proximity to Breslau TX (named by their neighbors from the same region), (3) the Lutheran affiliation consistent with Silesian German Protestants.
