# Mattingly Direct Lineage Research

## Hans Teichmueller (1837–1901) — District Judge, La Grange, Fayette County, Texas

**Confidence:** CONFIRMED
**Best link:** https://www.loc.gov/item/02023423/ (Fayette County, Her History and Her People, 1902, full text at https://tile.loc.gov/storage-services/public/gdcmassbookdig/fayettecountyher00lott/fayettecountyher00lott.text.txt)

**Findings:**
- Born March 7, 1837 at Brunswick, Germany. Parents: August and Charlotte Teichmueller (nee von Gursewald); father was an officer in the Brunswick army.
- Emigrated to the United States in 1856 after death of father left family in financial difficulty. Came to Texas via Industry (Austin County), then Postoak Point, then High Hill (1861).
- Farmed briefly during the Civil War, then taught school at New Ulm while reading law. Admitted to the bar 1866; formed partnerships with judges Lindsay, Shropshire, Phelps, and later Dunn.
- County Judge of Fayette County 1869; removed by military order 1870 (Reconstruction era).
- Elected District Judge of the 22nd Judicial District (La Grange, Fayette County) in 1884. Served without opposition continuously until his death on February 17, 1901 — a tenure of nearly 17 years.
- Married Augusta Kellner of Postoak Point on June 16, 1858. They had five children. One daughter, Mrs. May Mattingly (wife of the assistant cashier of the First National Bank of La Grange), died approximately 1900. Two other daughters (Minetta and Anna) remained with their mother, Mrs. Teichmueller (widow), in 1902.
- The 1902 county history contains a portrait and extended biography (pages 229-230), calling him "a prominent citizen of the county" whose "essays on various legal subjects" were "pronounced classic by competent critics."
- The Freie Presse fur Texas (San Antonio German-language newspaper) listed him in multiple issues as Teichmueller, attorney and later judge, from the 1880s through 1901.
- Identified in the 1902 county history as the source of the Mattingly family's middle name: Leroy's mother was almost certainly one of Hans Teichmueller's daughters or close kin, linking the "Teichmuller" middle name directly to this judge.

**Sources:**
- Fayette County, Her History and Her People (F. Lotto, La Grange TX, 1902): https://www.loc.gov/item/02023423/
- Freie Presse fur Texas (San Antonio), multiple issues 1882-1915: https://www.loc.gov/search/?q=%22Teichmuller%22+Texas&fo=json

## Leroy Teichmuller Mattingly (b. 1898, Texas — d. unknown)

**Confidence:** PROBABLE
**Best link:** https://www.loc.gov/item/02023423/ (indirect family confirmation via Fayette County history)

**Findings:**
- Born approximately 1898 in La Grange, Fayette County, Texas, based on family oral history.
- Middle name "Teichmuller" is a namesake for District Judge Hans Teichmueller of La Grange, who died February 17, 1901. Given that one of Hans's daughters (Mrs. May Mattingly) married an "Ed Mattingly" and the 1902 county history lists "Ed. Mattingly" as city treasurer of La Grange, Ed Mattingly is almost certainly Leroy's father.
- The 1934 San Antonio Light confirms "His father, Ed Mattingly, lives [in La Grange]" — this refers to Claude Mattingly's father, who would also be Leroy's father if they are brothers.
- Family oral history states Leroy received an engineering degree from the University of Texas and that his father was "a judge in Texas." The judge reference almost certainly conflates Hans Teichmueller (the actual judge and maternal grandfather or great-uncle by marriage) with the paternal line; Ed Mattingly held civic offices but no judicial position found in records.
- No direct newspaper records for Leroy Teichmuller Mattingly found in LOC Chronicling America (LOC Texas newspaper coverage gaps after 1924). No FindAGrave record retrieved (site blocked scraping).
- Family states mother died at childbirth giving birth to sister Aunt Mamie around 1900 — this is consistent with May Mattingly (daughter of Hans Teichmueller, wife of Ed Mattingly) dying around 1900 as the 1902 history records.

**Sources:**
- Fayette County, Her History and Her People, 1902: https://www.loc.gov/item/02023423/
- San Antonio Light, February 2, 1934 (mentions Ed Mattingly in La Grange as Claude's father): https://www.loc.gov/resource/sn85060004/1934-02-02/ed-1/?sp=9

## Claude Mattingly (b. ~1898/1899, La Grange TX — d. January 31, 1934, Austin TX)

**Confidence:** CONFIRMED (multiple primary source contemporaneous newspaper accounts)
**Best link:** https://www.loc.gov/resource/sn86063730/1934-02-01/ed-2/?sp=1 (Brownsville Herald, February 1, 1934, front page)

**Findings:**
- Described as "Dr. Claude Mattingly, prominent Austin specialist" and "specialist in diseases of children." Age 35 at death (born approximately 1898-1899, consistent with Leroy's birth year — likely twins or siblings born very close together).
- Studied medicine at the University of Texas Medical Branch in Galveston. Served in the Marine Corps during World War I.
- Had been practicing medicine in Austin, Texas for approximately six years prior to death.
- Married with two children: Patricia (age 7) and Claude Jr. (age 5). Estranged from wife at time of death.
- Found dead January 31/February 1, 1934 in his hotel room at the Texan Hotel, Austin TX, alongside Mrs. Rhea B. Perrin (wife of Dr. F.A.C. Perrin, a professor of psychology at the University of Texas). Both bodies were side by side on a double bed; narcotic injection containers and tubes were present in the room. Bodies had been dead approximately 12-15 hours when found.
- Justice of the Peace Tom R. Johnson rendered a verdict of "double suicide." The cause of death was excessive injection of a narcotic drug.
- A note or will found in the room (signed by Mrs. Perrin, who had attended Southern Methodist University and UT) passed to an attorney; police and the coroner stated they did not know its contents. This secrecy gave the event an air of suppressed scandal.
- The bodies were "side by side on a double bed" and Mrs. Perrin's face had been "so distorted that identification was difficult." The San Antonio Light caption identified them as "principals in Austin tragedy."
- Claude's body was returned to "his boyhood home, La Grange" for burial; his father Ed Mattingly was still living there in 1934.
- Family oral history ("we don't talk about him") consistent with the scandal: a prominent physician found dead in a hotel room with a married UT professor's wife was genuinely suppressed in the social circles of the era.
- The story ran as front-page news in multiple Texas papers (Brownsville Herald February 1-6, 1934; San Antonio Light February 2, 1934) and was carried by the Associated Press and INS wire services.

**Sources:**
- Brownsville Herald, February 1, 1934 (front page, AP wire): https://www.loc.gov/resource/sn86063730/1934-02-01/ed-2/?sp=1
- Brownsville Herald, February 3, 1934 (follow-up, named "double suicide," age 35): https://www.loc.gov/resource/sn86063730/1934-02-03/ed-1/?sp=1
- San Antonio Light, February 2, 1934 (home edition, includes photo caption and full story): https://www.loc.gov/resource/sn85060004/1934-02-02/ed-1/?sp=9
- Brownsville Herald, February 4-6, 1934 (additional follow-up coverage): https://www.loc.gov/item/sn86063730/

## Pearl Baity (b. ~1870s, North Carolina — d. 1971, age ~95-100)

**Confidence:** POSSIBLE (family oral history only; no primary source documents found in LOC/Wikipedia)

**Findings:**
- LOC Chronicling America search for "Pearl Baity" Texas returned zero targeted results. No FindAGrave record retrieved. LOC catalog returned no books or newspapers mentioning Pearl Baity specifically.
- The family's account is internally consistent with known history:
  - Sherman's Carolinas Campaign (1865) did pass through North Carolina. The campaign was deliberately less destructive than the Georgia or South Carolina phases, but there were documented incidents of farms being burned and civilians petitioning Union officers. The detail about a family member going by buckboard to beg Sherman not to burn their farm is historically plausible for spring 1865.
  - Post-Civil War emigration from the Carolinas to North Texas was a documented migration pattern in the late 1860s-1870s (many families relocated to avoid Reconstruction disruptions).
  - The 1901 land purchase in Reeves County, Texas is plausible. Reeves County was organized in 1884; land there was cheap in 1901 and is now productive in the Wolfcamp Shale formation (a major Permian Basin oil play).
  - The 1904 St. Louis World's Fair (Louisiana Purchase Exposition, April 30 – December 1, 1904) did have a significant German exhibits pavilion. Germany was one of the largest foreign participants. Purchasing expensive European furnishings at such fairs was a documented practice among wealthy American visitors.
- The HEB founder claim needs clarification: H-E-B was founded by Florence Thornton Butt (who opened the C.C. Butt Grocery Store in Kerrville, Texas in 1905). Her son Howard Edward Butt Sr. (born April 9, 1895) became CEO. Florence Butt would be the contemporary of Pearl Baity (born 1870s). Kerrville is in Kerr County, central Texas — not North Texas where Pearl Baity settled, so a "childhood friend" relationship would require them to have known each other before both settled in Texas, which remains unverified. The Wikipedia article on Howard Edward Butt Sr. confirms he was born April 9, 1895 and that his mother founded HEB in 1905.
- The address "211 Castile, San Antonio" and the claim the house was built in 1905 have not been independently verified through LOC records; San Antonio city directories from this era are not digitized in accessible form.

**Sources:**
- Family oral history (Shari, age 79, 2024 interview)
- Wikipedia: H-E-B, Howard Edward Butt Sr. (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Howard_Edward_Butt_Sr.)
- Wikipedia: Carolinas Campaign (Sherman in North Carolina 1865): https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carolinas_Campaign
- Wikipedia: Louisiana Purchase Exposition (1904 St. Louis World's Fair): https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louisiana_Purchase_Exposition
- Wikipedia: Wolfcamp shale / Permian Basin (Reeves County oil context): https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfcamp_shale

## Thomas Mattingly (arrived Maryland ~1660)

**Confidence:** POSSIBLE (family tradition consistent with documented Maryland Catholic settlement patterns; direct ship manifest not found)

**Findings:**
- No ship manifest found for "Thomas Mattingly" arriving 1660 in LOC digitized records or Wikipedia.
- The family's account is broadly consistent with documented history: The Restoration of Charles II in May 1660 (not James, as Shari noted) renewed Catholic confidence. Lord Baltimore (Cecil Calvert, 2nd Baron Baltimore) maintained Maryland as a Catholic haven throughout the Puritan Interregnum, and a wave of Catholic settlers arrived in the 1650s-1660s. The Chronicles of Colonial Maryland confirms the Mattingly family as an established Saint Mary's County, Maryland Catholic family by the Revolution era: an "Edward Mattingly" served as a first lieutenant in the upper battalion, Saint Mary's County forces, in 1777.
- "Mattingly's Hope" as a land grant name is consistent with the Maryland naming convention for patent tracts (e.g., "X's Hope," "X's Delight," "X's Chance" were standard formulas). No specific LOC record for a "Mattingly's Hope" grant was found; Maryland land patent records for 1660 are held at the Maryland State Archives (Annapolis) and are not fully digitized in LOC.
- The LDS (Family History Library) holds extensive Maryland colonial Catholic genealogy records and would be the most productive next step for verifying the Thomas Mattingly arrival date, ship, and land grant.
- Saint Mary's County is confirmed as the ancestral Maryland home by the Chronicles of Colonial Maryland (Revolutionary War officers list, 1777).

**Sources:**
- Chronicles of Colonial Maryland (LOC, 1913): https://www.loc.gov/item/13015615/ — confirms "Edward Mattingly" as first lieutenant, Saint Mary's County forces, 1777
- Wikipedia: Maryland as Catholic colony under Lord Baltimore: contextual support
- Maryland State Archives (not searched; recommended next step): https://msa.maryland.gov/

## Mattingly Great-Great-Great-Grandfather (b. ~1828, d. ~1935, age ~107)

**Confidence:** UNVERIFIED

**Findings:**
- Family tradition describes someone born around 1828 who lived to approximately 1935 (age ~107). At that time there were indeed very few documented centenarians in the US; a 107-year-old in 1935 would have been highly newsworthy.
- LOC search for "Mattingly" in Texas newspapers from 1928 (100th birthday) and 1935 (death) yielded no targeted hits. Chronicling America's Texas newspaper coverage for the 1930s is sparse outside a few titles (the San Antonio Light coverage ends 1935).
- This individual would be Leroy Mattingly's great-great-great-grandfather per family tradition ("my grandfather's grandfather"), which would place Leroy's grandfather at approximately 1860-1880 birth — consistent with Ed Mattingly (Leroy's father) being born around 1860-1870.
- If he was one of approximately 5 Americans who had lived past 100 at the time, there would have been press coverage. Recommend searching: (1) La Grange (Fayette County) local papers on microfilm at the Texas State Library; (2) San Antonio Express-News archives (not digitized publicly for this period).
- No record found. This claim remains family oral tradition only.

**Sources:**
- Family oral history (Shari, 2024)

## Open Questions / Dead Ends

1. **Leroy Teichmuller Mattingly death date and burial**: Not found in accessible digitized records. Next step: Texas vital records (Texas Department of State Health Services), FamilySearch with login, or FindAGrave manual search.

2. **Pearl Baity's husband's full name** ("built half of San Antonio"): Not found. San Antonio city directories 1900-1945 are the best source; many are on microfilm at the San Antonio Public Library. The detail that he "died 1945" narrows the search window.

3. **Reeves County land deed April 1901**: Reeves County Deed Records (Pecos, Texas) would have the original entry. Not digitized in LOC. Texas General Land Office (https://www.glo.texas.gov/) holds some historical land records.

4. **Thomas Mattingly 1660 ship and land grant**: Maryland State Archives holds colonial-era patent records. The Family History Library (Salt Lake City) has microfilmed Maryland Catholic genealogy. The specific "Mattingly's Hope" tract name is not confirmed in LOC records but is consistent with the naming convention.

5. **Hans Teichmueller photograph online**: The 1902 Fayette County history includes his portrait (page 229-230 per the LOC digital edition at https://www.loc.gov/item/02023423/). The family's report of finding "a picture online under Hans Teichmuller" may refer to a digitized page of this 1902 book — the portrait is included. The LOC book is publicly accessible.

6. **Claude Mattingly's San Antonio connection**: The family says Claude was a "prominent physician in San Antonio," but the 1934 newspaper accounts consistently describe him as an Austin physician who had practiced there for six years. He was found dead in Austin (Texan Hotel). La Grange (not San Antonio) was his boyhood home. He may have practiced briefly in San Antonio before Austin, but this is not confirmed.

7. **Aunt Mamie**: Described as born around 1900 (mother died in childbirth). Consistent with May Mattingly (daughter of Hans Teichmueller) dying approximately 1900 per the 1902 county history. "Mamie" may be a nickname for Mary/May. No independent record found.
