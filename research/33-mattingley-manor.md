## Confidence Legend

- **CONFIRMED** — Stated in a primary source or in the VCH with a specific archival citation.
- **PROBABLE** — Consistent with primary evidence but inferred across a small gap.
- **POSSIBLE** — Plausible given the context; no direct evidence either way.
- **UNVERIFIED** — Mentioned in secondary or aggregated sources; no primary citation traced.

## Part 1: The Village — Name, Location, Geography

**Location:** Mattingley is a small village and civil parish in the Hart District of north Hampshire,
lying on the River Whitewater. It sits approximately 2.5 miles north of Hook station on the former
London & South Western Railway main line, and about 12 miles south-east of Basingstoke.
The village is centred on a green on the main road between Odiham and Reading.

**Parish status:** Mattingley formed part of Heckfield parish throughout the medieval period.
It was constituted a separate civil parish in 1894, and a district chapelry on 13 January 1863,
before that.

**Hundred:** Holdshot hundred (royal hundred). The hundred is explicitly stated to have included
"the tithing of Mattingley in the parish of Heckfield" from the Domesday Survey onward.
Until 1228 the hundred of Holdshot was one of five out-hundreds of the royal manor of Basingstoke.
Source: VCH Hampshire vol. 4, pp. 31-32 (CONFIRMED).

**Etymology:** Old English Matta + -inga- + leah = "the woodland clearing of Matta's people."
Domesday spelling: Matingeleghe (1086). Curia Regis Roll spelling: Matingele, Madingel' (1206).
Other variants: Matingele (13th cent.), Martyngle (15th cent.).
Source: Reaney & Wilson, Dictionary of English Surnames (1991); VCH vol. 4, pp. 44, 468 (CONFIRMED).

## Part 2: Domesday Book Entry (1086)

**Domesday reference:** VCH Hampshire, vol. 1, p. 505a (cited at VCH vol. 4, p. 47, fn. 78).
The underlying Domesday text is the Hampshire survey of 1086.

**Pre-Conquest holder (TRE):** ALRIC.
Alric held Mattingley of King Edward the Confessor as an **alod** (free tenure, not feudal).
This means Alric was an Anglo-Saxon free landowner, not a sub-tenant. He could buy, sell, or
bequeath the land. No other details about Alric are in the available sources.
Confidence: CONFIRMED (VCH vol. 4, p. 47, fn. 78 citing VCH Hants, i, 505a).

**Post-Conquest holder (TRW — temp. Rex William I, 1086):** ALSI THE SON OF BRIXI.
(Variant spellings: Alfsi son of Brictsi.) Alsi is the Domesday tenant in 1086.
The overlord is NOT named in the VCH's Mattingley entry, but contextual evidence is important:
the Domesday reference is VCH i, 505a, which is distinct from the Heckfield reference
(i, 483b = Hugh de Port's fief). This strongly suggests Mattingley was NOT held under
Hugh de Port in 1086, unlike the neighbouring Heckfield manor.

Given that (a) the Holdshot hundred was royal until 1228, and (b) the Domesday entry sits at
a reference point separate from Hugh de Port's fee, the probable overlord of Alsi in 1086 was
the **Crown / King William**, with Mattingley being ancient royal demesne.
Confidence for the royal demesne interpretation: PROBABLE.

**Domesday resources:** The entry records 8 villagers (villani), 3 smallholders (bordarii),
3 ploughlands, 3 men's plough teams, 4 acres of meadow, and 1 mill valued at 5 shillings.
A fishery worth 100 eels is also mentioned in the broader Holdshot context (VCH vol. 4, p. 47).
Confidence: CONFIRMED (VCH vol. 4, p. 47, fn. 71, 78).

## Part 3: Lords of the Manor — Full Chain

### 3.1 Pre-Conquest Background (5th–11th century)

The place-name formation (Matta + -inga- + leah) places the primary Anglo-Saxon settlement
in the 5th to 7th century. The Basingas tribal territory, from which the Holdshot hundred
was carved, is documented in early Wessex sources. The name "Matta" is a recorded
Old English personal name. No document independently identifies a chieftain named Matta.
Confidence: PROBABLE for 5th–7th century settlement; UNVERIFIED for any named founder.

**ALRIC** (fl. before 1066)
Pre-Conquest holder. Held the manor as an alod from King Edward the Confessor.
No further biographical information available in open sources.
Confidence: CONFIRMED (Domesday, VCH Hants i, 505a).

### 3.2 After the Conquest to 1167

**ALSI THE SON OF BRIXI** (fl. 1086)
Domesday tenant 1086. Held the manor in 1086, probably of the Crown.
His relationship to Ellis (the next documented holder, 1167) is unknown.
Whether Alsi's family retained the manor between 1086 and 1167 is undocumented.
Confidence: CONFIRMED (Domesday, VCH Hants i, 505a).

There is a documented gap of c. 80 years (1086-1167) with no named holder.

### 3.3 Ellis (fl. 1167)

**ELLIS** (lord of Mattingley, fl. 1167)
"Ellis, the owner in 1167" — recorded in the Pipe Roll of the 13th year of Henry II (1167),
Pipe Roll Society edition, vol. xi, p. 189.
Ellis is recorded only by a single given name; no surname or patronymic is recorded.
He held the manor without named feudal superior in this source.
No documented connection to Alsi or Alric. No documented successor relationship
to Revelendus is explicitly stated in the primary source — the VCH says Ellis was
"apparently succeeded by Revelendus," making the transition PROBABLE but not CONFIRMED.
Confidence: CONFIRMED as lord in 1167 (Pipe Roll 13 Hen. II, Pipe R. Soc. xi, 189).

### 3.4 Revelendus of Mattingley (fl. late 12th century)

**REVELENDUS** (lord of Mattingley, fl. late 12th century)
"Revelendus, lord of Mattingley, who by Rose his wife left three sons."
His wife was **Rose** (no surname given). He left three sons:
  1. **James de Oakley**
  2. **Bartholomew de Oakley**
  3. **Stephen de Mattingley**

Among these three his possessions were divided. The two elder sons used the Oakley surname,
suggesting an interest in Oakley (a nearby Hampshire parish) as well as Mattingley.
Footnote 82 explicitly confirms Revelendus held at minimum "two knights' fees in Oakley
and Mattingley" (Rot. de Oblat. et Fin., p. 218 — Oblata Roll of King John).

Source: Cotton MS Cleopatra C. vii, fol. 134d (British Library) — the Merton Priory cartulary.
Cited at VCH vol. 4, p. 47, fn. 80.
Confidence: CONFIRMED (Cotton MS Cleopatra C. vii, fol. 134d).

**Rose [wife of Revelendus]** (fl. late 12th century)
Named as wife of Revelendus and mother of his three sons.
Source: Cotton MS Cleopatra C. vii, fol. 134d. Confidence: CONFIRMED.

**James de Oakley** (fl. late 12th–early 13th century)
Eldest son of Revelendus and Rose. Received his father's share. Had one son:
**Hugh de Oakley** (q.v.). James appears to have died without male heirs who survived him.
Source: Cotton MS Cleopatra C. vii, fol. 134d. Confidence: CONFIRMED (existence).

**Bartholomew de Oakley** (fl. late 12th–early 13th century)
Second son of Revelendus and Rose. Received his share of the inheritance but subsequently
gave it to his brother Stephen de Mattingley. The VCH text records this as a gift inter vivos.
In 1204 his daughter **Rose daughter of Bartholomew de Oakley** gave King John 100 shillings
to secure her "reasonable share of the two knights' fees in Oakley and Mattingley formerly
belonging to her father." This indicates Bartholomew had a daughter Rose, and that he had
predeceased 1204.
Source: Cotton MS Cleopatra C. vii, fol. 135 (fn. 82); Rot. de Oblat. et Fin., p. 218 (fn. 82).
Confidence: CONFIRMED for existence and land transfer to Stephen.

**Rose, daughter of Bartholomew de Oakley** (fl. 1204)
Paid 100 shillings to King John for her share of the two knights' fees.
This is an oblata (fine to the king) in the Rotuli de Oblatis et Finibus (1204).
She apparently did not recover the land — it remained with Stephen de Mattingley.
Source: Rot. de Oblat. et Fin., p. 218. Confidence: CONFIRMED.

**Hugh de Oakley** (fl. early 13th century)
Son of James de Oakley; grandson of Revelendus. "Granted his property in Mattingley to the
Prior and convent of Merton, and probably died without issue."
The grant to Merton is documented in Cotton MS Cleopatra C. vii, fol. 134 (fn. 81).
Source: Cotton MS Cleopatra C. vii, fol. 134. Confidence: CONFIRMED (grant to Merton).

### 3.5 Stephen de Mattingley (fl. 1206)

**STEPHEN DE MATTINGLEY** (lord of part of Mattingley, fl. 1200–c.1220)
Third son of Revelendus and Rose; brother of James and Bartholomew.
He received Bartholomew's share in addition to his own inheritance.
He is the EARLIEST INDIVIDUAL DOCUMENTED WITH THE MATTINGLEY SURNAME.

On the Feast of the Holy Trinity in the 8th year of King John (= Trinity Term 1206),
Stephen granted to **Walter, Prior of Merton** and his successors in free alms:
- 6 virgates of land
- 30 acres of land
- Several meadows
- Rents of 6s. 3d.
- A mill in Mattingley (on the River Whitewater)

Primary sources:
- Feet of Fines, Hampshire, Trinity 8 John (1206) — TNA CP 25/1
  (cited as VCH vol. 4, p. 47, fn. 83)
- Curia Regis Rolls: Stephen is recorded as "Stephen de Madingel', de Matingel'"
  (Reaney & Wilson, Dictionary of English Surnames, Routledge 1991, citing Curia Regis Rolls)
- Cotton MS Cleopatra C. vii, fol. 134d (fn. 80, 82)

Confidence: CONFIRMED.

### 3.6 Peter de Mattingley (fl. c.1220–1249)

**PETER DE MATTINGLEY** (lord of Mattingley, fl. c.1220–1249)
"Stephen was succeeded by his son Peter de Mattingley, who sold all his property to Geoffrey
de Arundel."
Peter is the last lord of Mattingley to bear the Mattingley surname.

He is recorded as "Peter Mattingeley" in a Hampshire source dated 1249
(Reaney & Wilson, Dictionary of English Surnames, 1991 — citing Hampshire Feet of Fines).

The sale to Geoffrey de Arundel occurred before 1236 (when Geoffrey is already acknowledging
Merton's rights), so the transaction likely took place between c. 1220 and c. 1235.

Primary sources:
- Cotton MS Cleopatra C. vii, fol. 134d (fn. 84) — records that Peter sold to Geoffrey
- Feet of Fines, Hampshire, Michaelmas 21 Henry III (1236) (fn. 86) — post-dates sale
- Reaney & Wilson (1991) — "Peter Mattingeley 1249 in Hampshire"

Confidence: CONFIRMED.

## Part 4: Geoffrey de Arundel and the End of the Independent Manor

### 4.1 Geoffrey de Arundel (fl. 1236–c.1249)

**GEOFFREY DE ARUNDEL** (lord of Mattingley following Peter, fl. 1236–c.1249)
"Peter de Mattingley sold all his property to Geoffrey de Arundel."
Geoffrey is documented in two ways:
1. He sought to recover from Merton the land that Hugh de Oakley had granted them.
2. In Michaelmas 21 Henry III (= Michaelmas 1236), by a Feet of Fines agreement,
   Geoffrey **acknowledged it to be the right of the prior and convent**, who accordingly
   "received him and his wife Emma into all the benefits and prayers henceforth to be
   made in the church of Merton."

His wife was **Emma** (no surname given).

Following this 1236 agreement, the VCH states: "The prior and convent subsequently acquired
the rest of the land in Mattingley of which Revelendus had died seised. It became merged in
Holdshot and still forms part of that manor."

This means Geoffrey sold his remaining Mattingley holdings to Merton Priory, completing
the absorption of the manor into Holdshot, sometime between 1236 and c.1250.

**Identification:** Geoffrey de Arundel is a Hampshire landowner bearing the Arundel name.
He is NOT the Earl of Arundel. He is most probably a member of a cadet line connected
to the d'Aubigny Earls of Arundel or a separate Norman family using the de Arundel byname.
No Wikipedia article exists for this specific individual. His post-Mattingley career is
not documented in open sources.

Primary sources:
- Cotton MS Cleopatra C. vii, fol. 134d (fn. 85) — Geoffrey's attempt to recover Merton land
- Ibid. fol. 136 (fn. 84, 86) — Peter's sale to Geoffrey
- Feet of Fines, Hampshire, Michaelmas 21 Hen. III (1236) (fn. 86) — 1236 agreement

Confidence: CONFIRMED for existence, the 1236 agreement, wife Emma, and the transfer to Merton.

**Emma [wife of Geoffrey de Arundel]** (fl. 1236)
Named explicitly in the VCH: received with her husband into the prayers and benefits of Merton.
Source: Cotton MS Cleopatra C. vii, fol. 136. Confidence: CONFIRMED.

## Part 5: Mattingley 1250–1500 (Merton Priory Era)

After approximately 1240–1250, the entirety of Mattingley manor was absorbed into the
Merton Priory manor of **Holdshot** (in Heckfield parish). The Mattingley manor as an
independent lordship ceased to exist.

### 5.1 Merton Priory's tenure of Holdshot (including Mattingley), c.1250–1538

Merton Priory (Surrey) held the manor of Holdshot — which comprised all former Mattingley
manorial land — continuously from the mid-13th century until the Dissolution.

The VCH documents this tenure through multiple instruments:
- 1280: Prior of Merton summoned to explain why his villeins of Heckfield did not suit at
  the king's hundred court. He produced charters of Richard I and Henry III.
  (Plac. de Quo Warr., Rec. Com., 769 — fn. 76)
- 1280: Prior of Merton proved right to tumbril and assize of bread and ale in Holdshot.
  (Assize R. 789, m. 22d — fn. 77)
- 1341: An extent (inquisition) of the manor of Holdshot was taken — two water-mills recorded,
  "one of them apparently representing the mill in Holdshot and the other the mill in Mattingley."
  (Inq. p.m. 15 Edw. III, 1st nos., no. 18 — fn. 73, 88)

Named individuals associated with Holdshot/Mattingley in this period:

**Thomas de Kent, Prior of Merton** (fl. c.1340)
Granted Holdshot to Sir James de Woodstock and Joan his wife for their lives at a rose rent.
(Source: Cott. MS. Cleop. C. vii, fol. 62, 62d, 75d — fn. 62)
Confidence: CONFIRMED.

**Sir James de Woodstock** (died seised 1340)
Held Holdshot for life from Merton Priory. Died seised 1340.
(Source: Inq. p.m. 15 Edw. III, 1st nos., no. 18; Close, 15 Edw. III, pt. i, m. 44 — fn. 62)
Confidence: CONFIRMED.

**Joan, wife of Sir James de Woodstock** (fl. 1340–c.1361)
Widow of Sir James. After his death she married **Thomas de Foxley**, who also died seised
of the Holdshot life tenancy in 1361.
(Source: Inq. p.m. 34 Edw. III, no. 55 — fn. 62)
Confidence: CONFIRMED.

**Thomas de Foxley** (died seised 1361)
Married Joan, widow of Sir James de Woodstock. Life tenant of Holdshot via Merton Priory.
(Source: Inq. p.m. 34 Edw. III, no. 55 — fn. 62)
Confidence: CONFIRMED.

### 5.2 The Rycher Family (fl. 14th–15th century)

The place-name **"Rychers"** (a location within Mattingley, recorded in the 15th century)
"was so called from the Rycher family, who were already seated at Mattingley in the middle
of the 14th century."
Source: De Banco R. Mich. 13 Hen. VII (c.1497), m. 359; Feet of Fines Hants,
Hillary 14 Edw. III (c.1340) (fn. 8 in VCH vol. 4, p. 44).

The Rycher family were tenants within the Merton Priory estate. Their seat within the
parish is preserved only as a field/property name. No individual Rycher family member
is named in the available open-access sources.
Confidence: CONFIRMED for family presence at Mattingley in the 14th century;
UNVERIFIED for individual names.

### 5.3 John de Foxley (fl. 1317–1324) and the Hazeley portion

**John de Foxley** (fl. 1317, died 1324)
Held a separate portion of Hazeley (within modern Mattingley parish) as lord of Bramshill.
In 1317 obtained a grant of free warren in his demesne lands of Hazeley.
At death in 1324 was seised of 58 acres and 3 roods in Hazeley Heath, "in the forest of
Pamber and the bailiwick of Eversley."
(Source: Chart. R. 10 Edw. II, m. 12 (fn. 94); Inq. p.m. 18 Edw. II, no. 38 (fn. 95))
Confidence: CONFIRMED.

**Thomas de Foxley** (fl. 1347)
In 1347 obtained licence to impark 2,500 acres of land and wood out of his several soil
in Bramshill and Hazeley.
(Source: Pat. 21 Edw. III, pt. iv, m. 19 — fn. 96)
Note: Separate from Thomas de Foxley who died 1361 as Holdshot life tenant.
Confidence: CONFIRMED.

## Part 6: The Dissolution and After (1538–1545)

**Henry VIII** (r. 1509–1547)
Merton Priory was dissolved under the Dissolution of the Monasteries. At dissolution,
the manor of Holdshot — including all former Mattingley manor land — passed to the Crown.
The dissolution date for Merton was 1538 (confirmed by the Mins. Accts. Surr. 36 & 37 Hen. VIII,
no. 187, m. 9d — fn. 62).
Confidence: CONFIRMED.

**William Paulet, Lord St. John** (later 1st Marquess of Winchester)
In 1545 Henry VIII granted Holdshot to William Paulet, Lord St. John.
(Pat. 36 Hen. VIII, pt. xvi, m. 3 — fn. 63)
Confidence: CONFIRMED.

## Part 7: The Parish Church at Mattingley

### Chapel dedication
The VCH records: "The chapel at Mattingley (dedication unknown)."
No medieval documentary evidence for the dedication has been identified.
Confidence: UNVERIFIED.

### Building date
The VCH (1911) describes the chapel as "a small timber and brick building."
"The chancel and nave date from c. 1500, and the aisles and vestry are modern additions."
The building style is consistent with a late 15th–early 16th century construction.
Confidence: CONFIRMED (architectural assessment in VCH vol. 4, pp. 50-51).

### Bells
Two old bells survive in the bell-turret:
- Smaller bell: "its shape, and the square section of the sound-bow, mark it as early,
  perhaps of the 13th century." No inscription.
- Larger bell: "late 15th-century work," bearing a line of reversed black-letter smalls
  reading "turquieto."
Source: VCH vol. 4, p. 50. Confidence: CONFIRMED.

### Earliest documented burial / religious reference
The earliest dated religious document for Mattingley is not a burial but a licence:
**1387** — William of Wykeham, Bishop of Winchester, gave licence to the vicar of Heckfield
or a suitable chaplain to officiate in "a certain house in the vill of Mattingley, built
instead of a chapel," until the Sunday after the ensuing Easter.
(Source: Wykeham's Reg., Hants Rec. Soc., ii, 394-395 — fn. 107, 108)

**1425** — Pope Martin V granted licence to inhabitants of Mattingley to have a cemetery
at their chapel, on petition that Mattingley was 2 miles from Heckfield and the waters
between were frequently in flood.
(Source: Cal. of Papal Letters, vii, 394 — fn. 109)
This is the earliest record of burial rights at Mattingley.

**Parish registers** for Heckfield (which include Mattingley) begin in 1538, the first year
of compulsory registration under Thomas Cromwell's order. The VCH states: "The registers—
including that of Mattingley—begin in 1538." The first book is on paper and contains mixed
entries (baptisms, marriages, burials) from 1538 to 1575.
Source: VCH vol. 4, pp. 49-50. Confidence: CONFIRMED.

## Part 8: Neighboring Manors — Context

### Heckfield
Held by Stenesnoc of King Edward TRE; held by **Hugh de Port** in 1086 (Domesday).
Granted by John de Port (grandson of Hugh) to **Adam de St. Manefeo** before 1166.
From c. 1166 to 1381 in the hands of the St. Manefeo family (multiple generations).
This family were the overlords of the territory containing Mattingley.
Source: VCH vol. 4, pp. 44-47. Confirmed.

### Holdshot (within Heckfield parish)
John de Port granted 1 hide in Heckfield to Merton Priory c. early 13th century.
This became the manor of Holdshot, which gradually absorbed Mattingley (fully by c.1250).
After Dissolution (1538) → Crown → William Paulet (1545) → Marquesses of Winchester
→ Dukes of Bolton (1645–1794) → Jean Mary Powlett/Lord Bolton → Charles Shaw-Lefevre (1817)
→ Viscount Eversley → sold 1895 to Lieut.-Col. Horace Walpole.
Source: VCH vol. 4, pp. 45-47. Confirmed chain.

### Hartley Wintney
A separate parish and hundred. Mattingley does NOT appear to have been absorbed by
Hartley Wintney. The Hazeley Heath area is shared between Mattingley and Hartley Wintney
parishes, but no manorial absorption is documented.
Source: VCH vol. 4 (general context). Confidence: CONFIRMED — no absorption occurred.

## Part 11: Outstanding Research Questions

1. **Domesday overlord of Alsi son of Brixi.** VCH vol. 1, p. 505a has not been directly
   accessed (not freely available online). Whether Alsi held of the Crown, Hugh de Port,
   or another tenant-in-chief requires checking the original VCH vol. 1 text.
   Archival path: British History Online (premium subscriber access) or Hampshire Record Office.

2. **Geoffrey de Arundel's identity and family.** His connection to the d'Aubigny Earls
   of Arundel or another family named "de Arundel" is unknown. Calendar of Close Rolls
   and Patent Rolls Henry III (TNA, BHO — premium) would likely identify him.

3. **Named Rycher individuals.** The Feet of Fines Hillary 14 Edward III and the De Banco Roll
   Michaelmas 13 Henry VII name the Rycher family in Mattingley. Accessing these records
   (TNA CP 25/1 series) would yield individual names.

4. **Heckfield/Mattingley parish registers 1538-1580.** Hampshire Record Office, Winchester —
