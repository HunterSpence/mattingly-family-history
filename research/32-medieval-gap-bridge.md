## Sources Searched (This Session)

### 1. Hampshire Heralds' Visitation 1530 / 1575 / 1622 / 1634
**Source:** Harleian Society vol 64 (1913), *Pedigrees from the Visitation of Hampshire*
**Archive.org identifier:** `pedigreesfromvis64beno`
**Access method:** Direct DJVU text download — 473,137 characters, fully read
**Reference:** Harleian MS 1544, fos. 152–153 (underlying manuscript)

**Finding — CONFIRMED:** The surname MATTINGLEY does not appear in the Index Nominum (personal names index) of the Hampshire Heralds' Visitation. The name appears only in the Index Locorum (places) at pages 174, 175, 213, 214 — exclusively as a place name (the village/tithing).

**Implication:** No Mattingley family was of armigerous (gentry) rank in Hampshire during the Visitation periods. The Heralds recorded only families bearing coats of arms. Henry MATYNGLE (married 1548) and his kin were commoners — tenant farmers or yeomen — below the Heralds' threshold. This is consistent with, and does not contradict, a genuine Mattingley-surname family in the area.

**Note on variant:** The Index records "Madingley, see Mattingley" — confirming the spelling Madingley was used interchangeably for the place.

### 2. Families Actually Seated at Mattingley (Visitation)
Two gentry families are documented at Mattingley in the Visitation records:

**DEANE family — CONFIRMED (from Visitation text, pages 174–175):**
> "A vellom escochion was subscribed the Armes & Crest of Mr John Deane of mattiuyley in hampshire sonn of John Deane of wallingford in Berkshire gentlman exemplified Ratified & confirmed to him & his posterity wth ther due difference for ever — William Camden, Clarenceux king of Armes."

The Deane family from Wallingford, Berkshire was seated at Mattingley in the late 16th/early 17th century. Arms confirmed by William Camden as Clarenceux King of Arms (Camden held the office 1597–1623). These were the gentry residents; the Mattingley-surname commoners were likely their tenants on the Holdshot/Merton Priory lands.

**TUTT family — CONFIRMED (from Visitation text, pages 213–214):**
> "John Tutt [late] of Madingley in Com. Southton" died March 9th 1680, aged 65.
> "Thomas Tutt of Mattingly aetat. 36 an°" living 1686, signed his pedigree.

The Tutt family (also spelled Tott) was at Mattingley c.1680–1686. John Tutt (b. ~1615, d. 1680) was described as "late of Madingley." This gives Hearth Tax era (1662) context: the Tutt family, not any Mattingley-surname family, held gentry status at the location in the Restoration period.

### 3. VCH Hampshire — Mattingley Manor History
**Source:** BHO VCH Hampshire vol 4, pp44–51
**URL:** https://www.british-history.ac.uk/vch/hants/vol4/pp44-51
**Access:** Successfully fetched

**Finding:** The manorial narrative confirms:
- Peter de Mattingley conveyed his holding to Geoffrey de Arundel, Michaelmas 21 Henry III (~1236). Footnote 84 cites the TNA CP 25/1 Feet of Fine.
- After this conveyance, the land passed into Holdshot manor under Merton Priory's administration.
- The Rycher family appears at Mattingley by ~1340 (Feet of Fines, Hillary 14 Edward III — Robert le Rycher and Joan his wife vs. Thomas le Rycher).
- No Mattingley-surname individual appears in the VCH narrative between 1236 and the 16th century.

**Implication:** The surname-bearing family (i.e., people called Mattingley) were almost certainly tenants of the successive manorial lords, not lords themselves. They would appear in manorial court rolls (not surviving online), lay subsidy rolls, or church records — not in the gentry sources VCH draws from.

### Q3: Hearth Tax 1662 — Hampshire
**Answer: PARTIAL — contextual finding only**

The 1662 Hearth Tax returns for Hampshire are held at TNA (E 179/176/565 and related pieces) and at Hampshire Record Office. They are not freely available online in full transcription. The Hampshire Record Office (calm.hants.gov.uk) was inaccessible due to CloudFlare challenge.

**Contextual finding:** The Tutt family (John Tutt, d. 1680; Thomas Tutt, b. 1650) held gentry status at Mattingley in the 1680s Visitation. For 1662, a Mattingley-surname entry would appear in the non-exempt householder lists if any family member was resident and taxable. This requires in-person access to E 179/176/565 or the HRO copy.

### Q4: PCC wills before 1548
**Answer: UNRESOLVED — requires subscription or in-person access**

TNA PROB 11 (Prerogative Court of Canterbury wills) is searchable via Ancestry.co.uk and Findmypast subscriptions, and in person at TNA Kew. TheGenealogist.co.uk (which indexes PCC wills) was blocked by CloudFlare in automated access. No free online index of PCC wills before 1600 is available for this surname.

**Search strategy when access is available:** Search PROB 11 for testators with surname variants: MATTINGLEY, MATTINGLY, MATYNGLEY, MATYNGLE, MOTTINGLEY, MOTYNGLE, MADINGLEY — date range 1380–1560 — counties: Hampshire, Surrey, Berkshire, London/Middlesex.

### Q5: Heralds' Visitations
**Answer: CONFIRMED — definitively answered (see Section 1 above)**

No Mattingley family appears as a gentry/armigerous family in the Hampshire Heralds' Visitations of 1530, 1575, 1622, or 1634 (Harleian Society vol 64, fully read). The surname appears only as a place name. This confirms the family was of commoner (yeoman or tenant farmer) status throughout the Visitation period. The gentry families actually resident at Mattingley location were the Deane family (from Wallingford, Berkshire, fl. 1575–1622) and later the Tutt family (fl. 1680–1686).

## Assessment

The 307-year gap (1249–1548) remains **UNBRIDGED** by any confirmed or probable named individual.

The gap is not evidence against the family's continuous presence at Mattingley. Commoner/yeoman families in medieval England appear in records only when they: (a) paid taxes (subsidy rolls), (b) made wills through church courts, (c) appeared in manorial court rolls, or (d) conducted land transactions recorded as Feet of Fines. All four of these record types for Hampshire/Holdshott are either unindexed, subscription-locked, or require in-person archive access.

The Heralds' Visitation finding (Q5) actually reinforces the family narrative: the Mattingley-surname bearers were commoners, not gentry, which is exactly why they do not appear in the armigerous record series that ARE accessible online.

**Next action with highest expected value:** Request TNA document delivery or in-person visit for E 179/173/xx (Hampshire Holdshott hundred lay subsidies 1327, 1334, 1524–25). The 1524–25 assessment is particularly valuable as it predates Henry MATYNGLE's marriage by only 24 years and may name his father or grandfather.
