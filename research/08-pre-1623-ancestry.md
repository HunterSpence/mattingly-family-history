## Summary

The Mattingly line before Thomas I (b. ~1623) can be traced through three distinct tiers of evidence:

1. **WikiTree genealogical data (POSSIBLE–PROBABLE):** Reaches John Mattingly, buried at St. Mary Colechurch, London, December 17, 1641 — grandfather of Thomas I. One primary source document (the burial record) anchors this person.

2. **Medieval primary sources (CONFIRMED):** Stephen de Mattingley (fl. 1206) and his son Peter de Mattingley are documented in the Curia Regis Rolls and the Victoria County History of Hampshire, published by British History Online. They held land at Mattingley and granted property to the Prior of Merton.

3. **Deep-time context (historical/cultural, not personal genealogy):** The Domesday Book (1086) names Alric as the pre-Conquest free-holder of Mattingley. The village itself was founded by the Basingas tribe in the 5th–6th century. The Mattingly Y-chromosome (R1b-Y14084 > R1b-DF27) traces to the Bell Beaker expansion approximately 2500 BCE.

The "1525 ancestor" Shari references from LDS research is structurally consistent with the generation of John Mattingly (b. ~1560)'s father — born approximately 1490–1530. No named individual at this exact depth has been independently confirmed in open sources. The Hampshire Record Office holds the parish registers (starting 1538) that could confirm this generation.

## The Documented Generations

### Generation 0: Thomas Mattingly I (b. ~1623 — The Immigrant)
Born approximately 1623 in Omny Parish, Sussex or Berkshire, England. Died July 24, 1664, St. Mary's County, Maryland. Wife: Elizabeth (maiden name unknown). Catholic.

Arrived Maryland late 1663 or early 1664. His sons Thomas II and Cezar received "Mattingly's Hope" — a 300-acre land patent in Charles County, Maryland, dated September 4, 1666.

Y-DNA haplogroup: **R1b-DF27 > R1b-Y14084** (terminal SNP unique to all documented Mattingly descendants; established by FTDNA Big-Y testing via the Mattingly-Matney Surname DNA Project).

**Confidence: CONFIRMED.** Multiple consistent primary and genealogical sources.

## Medieval Mattingley Family: The Primary-Source Line

These are not proven direct ancestors but are the earliest documented individuals with the Mattingley surname, living at the village:

**Ellis** — lord of Mattingley in 1167. Succeeded by Revelendus.

**Revelendus of Mattingley** (fl. late 12th century) — lord of Mattingley after Ellis. Married Rose; their three sons were James de Oakley, Bartholomew de Oakley, and Stephen de Mattingley. Documented in VCH Hampshire Vol. 4.

**Stephen de Mattingley** (fl. 1206) — son of Revelendus. In 1206 granted to the Prior of Merton in free alms: 6 virgates, 30 acres of land, several meadows, 6s. 3d. rent, and a mill in Mattingley. Also recorded in the Curia Regis Rolls as "Stephen de Madingel', de Matingel'" — the earliest dated individual with the Mattingley surname per the Dictionary of English Surnames (Reaney & Wilson, Routledge, 1991). Source: British History Online: https://www.british-history.ac.uk/vch/hants/vol4/pp44-51

**Peter de Mattingley** (fl. 1220s–1240s) — son of Stephen. Sold all his property to Geoffrey de Arundel. After this, the surname disappears from Mattingley's manorial records as major landholders — the family likely continued as yeoman farmers or tenants. **Confidence: CONFIRMED** for all three via BHO VCH.

The gap between Peter de Mattingley (1240s) and John Mattingly (d. 1641) is approximately 400 years with no named individual yet bridging it in primary sources. The surname continuity through the village is plausible; the personal genealogical chain is unproven.

## The 1086 Domesday Entry

Mattingley appears in the Domesday Book as **Matingeleghe**. The entry records:

- Pre-Conquest holder: **Alric**, who held it of King Edward (the Confessor) as an alod (free of feudal tenure)
- 1086 holder: **Alsi the son of Brixi** (Alfsi son of Brictsi), holding under **Hugh de Port**
- The village was a chapelry of Heckfield, in the hundred of Holdshott

Alric was an Anglo-Saxon thane or free farmer, almost certainly part of the Basingas community whose ancestors had named the place. His fate after the Norman Conquest is unknown. He is a village-level ancestor — not a proven direct lineal ancestor of later Mattingley families — but represents the community from which the surname derives.

Source: British History Online (VCH Hampshire, 1911): https://www.british-history.ac.uk/vch/hants/vol4/pp44-51

## The Basingas and Matta: Anglo-Saxon Deep Roots

The village of Mattingley derives its name from Old English Matta + -inga- + leah: "the woodland clearing of the followers/people of Matta." The personal name Matta (or Mætta) is inferred from the place name.

The naming clan was part of the **Basingas**, an Anglo-Saxon tribe whose territory in the Loddon Valley formed a regio of the early Kingdom of Wessex. Their leader Basa gave the tribe its name, which survives in Old Basing and Basingstoke (both approximately 22 km west-southwest of Mattingley). The Basingas settled eastern Basingstoke in the early 6th century. Their territory (Basengum) is recorded in the Anglo-Saxon Chronicle as the site of the Battle of Basing in 871 AD.

Matta is a probable real person — place-name conventions require an eponymous leader — but no document independently names him. His existence is inferred. The founding dates to the 5th–6th century.

Source: Wikipedia, Basingas: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basingas (citing Yorke, Wessex in the Early Middle Ages, 1995)

## What the Parish Registers Could Confirm

The Heckfield parish registers — including the Mattingley chapelry — begin in 1538. Per British History Online (VCH Hampshire, p. 38): "The registers — including that of Mattingley — begin in 1538; the first book is of paper, containing mixed entries of baptisms, marriages and burials thence to 1575."

Books two through nine cover 1575–1812 with minor gaps. There is a gap from approximately 1554–1559 (incumbent absent during the Marian restoration).

To confirm the 1525 ancestor: search the Hampshire Record Office, Winchester for baptisms of Mattingly children in the 1550s–1570s, which would reflect a parent born in the 1520s–1530s.
