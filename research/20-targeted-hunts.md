## Hunt #1 — Texas Centenarian (born ~1828, died ~1935, age ~107)

**Result: NO CANDIDATE FOUND. Claim remains family oral tradition without primary documentation.**

### What the record shows

Thomas Jefferson Mattingly (WikiTree Mattingly-591) is the only documented Mattingly born ~1828 in the ancestral chain. He was born 1828 in Kentucky, married Elizabeth Christie (b. 2 Dec 1833 Shelby KY), and died 1883 in Pattonsburg, Daviess, Missouri at approximately age 54. His son is Edward Mattingly Sr. (b. 1868 MO), whose son is Leroy Teichmueller Mattingly (b. 1896 TX). Thomas Jefferson's birth year of 1828 matches the oral tradition perfectly — but his death year and age do not.

No other Mattingly born ~1828 appears in any accessible record for the family.

### Newspaper search results

All available digitized Texas newspapers for 1934-1936 were searched via Library of Congress Chronicling America full-text search:

- San Antonio Light (1934-1936): 0 hits for "centenarian," "107 years old," or "Mattingly" in the 1934-1936 window
- Brownsville Herald (1934-1936): 0 hits for the same search terms
- All LOC newspapers 1934-1936, search "107 years old": 0 hits
- All LOC newspapers 1935, search "Mattingly centenarian": 0 hits

Note: LOC's Texas newspaper coverage for 1934-1936 is sparse. The La Grange Journal (the local Fayette County paper that would carry a local centenarian's death) is not digitized for this period. It is held on microfilm at the Texas State Library, Austin.

### Collateral lines ruled out

- William D. Baity (1829-1894, WikiTree Baity-119): born one year off, died at 65
- Isham Baity (1804-1892): born 1804, not 1828
- Hans Teichmueller (1837-1901): born 1837, not 1828
- Morgan N. Johnson (1858-1932, WikiTree Johnson-91160): born 1858, not 1828
- No centenarian found on any maternal (Teichmueller, Baity, Johnson) line

### Most likely explanation

The oral tradition conflates two separate family facts that became fused through at least three generations of oral transmission:

1. Thomas Jefferson Mattingly WAS born in 1828. This birth year is verifiable and would have been known within the family.
2. A separate claim about extreme longevity ("someone lived to 107") attached to a different, unidentifiable person, OR is a mythological inflation of a lesser fact (e.g., someone living into their 80s).

The chain of transmission: Thomas Jefferson (d. 1883) → Edward Sr. (b. 1868) → Leroy (b. 1896) → Shari (~b. 1945) spans ~80 years of oral history — long enough for two separate facts to fuse.

### If Hunter wants to close this definitively

Email lib-briscoe@utlists.utexas.edu (Briscoe Center for American History, UT Austin) with a request to search the La Grange Journal microfilm 1934-1936 for any Mattingly death notice. The Briscoe Center holds Texas newspaper microfilm not digitized anywhere online.

## Hunt #2 — Hans Teichmueller's 5th Child

**Result: NOT IDENTIFIED. The 5th child is documented to exist but unnamed in all accessible free databases.**

### Primary source evidence

Lotto 1902, page 231 (direct quote from digitized OCR text): _"To this union were born five children. Mrs. May Mattingly, wife of the assistant cashier of the First National Bank, died two years ago; Misses Minetta and Anna stay at the home of their mother, Mrs. Teichmueller."_

The TSHA handbook entry independently confirms: _"The Teichmuellers had five children."_

Four children are confirmed across all sources:

| Child | WikiTree | Born | Died | Status |
|-------|----------|------|------|--------|
| Louisa Teichmueller | Teichmueller-2 | abt. Sep 1862 TX | abt. 1870 | Confirmed — died in infancy/childhood |
| Mary "May" (Teichmueller) Mattingly | Teichmueller-1 | 16 Dec 1867 TX | 1900 La Grange | CRITICAL ANCESTOR |
| Minette (Teichmueller) Pohl | Teichmueller-4 | 27 Jan 1871 TX | 28 Aug 1970 | Living at home 1902, unmarried |
| Anna E. Teichmueller | Teichmueller-5 | 28 Nov 1874 TX | 13 Jan 1961 | Living at home 1902, unmarried |

The 5th child appears nowhere: not in WikiTree (Teichmueller-3 lists only 4 children), not in the Wikipedia article on Minette Teichmueller, not in the TSHA entry, not in the Lotto 1902 text by name.

### What the absence pattern means

Lotto 1902 says "Misses Minetta and Anna stay at the home of their mother" — using the plural "Misses" specifically for unmarried women living at home. If a 5th child (daughter) had survived and married, she would likely have been named as "Mrs. [husband]." If a son had survived and was prominent, he would almost certainly have been named. The biographical sketch is brief (2 sentences on children), suggesting the author either lacked information or the 5th was not alive or notable at time of writing.

### Birth timeline analysis

- Marriage: June 10, 1858
- Louisa: abt. Sep 1862 (4-year gap from marriage)
- May: Dec 16, 1867 (5-year gap from Louisa)
- Minette: Jan 27, 1871
- Anna: Nov 28, 1874

The most likely position for the 5th: born between 1858 and 1862 (before Louisa) or between 1862 and 1867 (between Louisa and May), died before the 1870 census, and therefore invisible in all available digitized records. A child born after Anna (1874) and surviving to appear in the 1880 census is also possible but would be confirmed or denied by that census.

### Why this is unresolved

- The 1880 US Census for Fayette County TX (which would show all household members) requires a FamilySearch or Ancestry login. The record is at Film M9-1291, Enumeration District 57-65 (La Grange). A free FamilySearch account at familysearch.org will provide access.
- The 1908 "Hans Teichmueller Biographical Sketch" (Austin: Von Boeckmann-Jones) is not digitized. It is held at UT Austin Briscoe Center (lib-briscoe@utlists.utexas.edu) and may name additional family members.
- Texas statewide vital records did not begin until 1903. Any child dying before that year left no state death certificate.

### Current conclusion

**1880 census shows only 4 children alive at that time is the most probable explanation; the 5th most likely died in infancy before 1870 and is not in any accessible record.** This is the most parsimonious explanation consistent with: (a) the 5-year gap between Louisa and May, (b) the 4-year gap between the 1858 marriage and Louisa's 1862 birth suggesting possible prior loss, and (c) the complete absence from all post-1870 sources.
