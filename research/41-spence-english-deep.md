## 10. Surname Etymology and Distribution — Final Summary

### Origins (CONFIRMED — multiple authoritative sources)

**Etymology chain:**
- Latin *dispendere* = "to weigh out / distribute"
- Medieval Latin *dispensa* = the household larder/pantry
- Old French *despense* / Anglo-French *espense* = the storeroom/buttery
- Middle English *spence*, *spens* = the larder
- Occupational role: *spenser* or *dispensator* = the person controlling food distribution in a great house
- Surname: Initially *de la Despense* or *atte Spence* → reduced to Spens/Spence
- Variant forms: Spencer (extended occupational), Spens, Spense, Spenceley, Spencley

**The Le Despenser connection:** The most famous medieval Despenser family (Hugh le Despenser, 1st Earl of Winchester, 1261–1326; his son Hugh le Despenser the Younger, 1286–1326, favourite of Edward II) represents the noble English version of the same occupational designation. The common Spence/Spens families are the non-noble bearers of the same occupational surname.

**First English record:** Simon del Spens, Charters of Guisburn Priory, Yorkshire, 1300 (Source: Bardsley, *A Dictionary of English and Welsh Surnames*, 1896, via HouseOfNames)

**First Scottish record:** John 'Dispensator', 1161–1171, vassal of Walter fitz Alan, Steward of Scotland (Source: Wikipedia, Clan Spens, citing Anderson, *The Scottish Nation*, 1877)

**Distribution today (CONFIRMED — name census data):**
- United States: ~37,305 bearers (rank 808th) — highest total
- England: ~14,198 bearers
- Scotland: ~4,663 bearers (but higher per capita; rank 149th in Scotland vs. 646th in England)
- Jamaica: highest density globally (1 in 350) — reflects Scottish plantation-era settlement

## 13. Summary Assessment

### What Is CONFIRMED
- Dale William Spence Sr. was **American-born** (Beaumont, TX, ~1934–1936), educated in Texas, and a professor at Rice University 1963–2003 — he is NOT himself an immigrant
- The Spence/Spens surname first appears in England at Yorkshire (Guisborough, 1300) and is documented throughout NE England and Scotland for 700+ years
- The surname's strongest documented concentration is in Scotland (rank 149th) with NE England (Co. Durham, Yorkshire, Northumberland) as the principal English region
- Clan Spens of Fife is the principal Scottish family; their line traces from Norman French *dispensator* (household steward) roles from 1161 to the present
- Sir William Spens (1882–1962) was born in Glasgow, son of John Spens — the Clan Spens academic tradition includes Cambridge's Vice-Chancellor
- Jonathan Spence (1936–2021) was born in Surrey, England, and became a Sterling Professor at Yale — the closest career parallel to Dale Sr.'s trajectory
- "Dovie" is classified as an American (South) female given name — Dale Sr.'s mother was almost certainly American-born

### What Is PROBABLE
- Dale Sr.'s **father** (name unknown) was the English immigrant — born in England c. 1900–1915, emigrated to Beaumont TX approximately 1920–1935
- The immigrant ancestor came from **NE England** (Yorkshire or County Durham being the most likely counties), the region of highest English Spence surname concentration
- He arrived via the **Port of Galveston** (the primary Texas immigration port 1880–1918) and was drawn to Beaumont by the oil/industrial boom following Spindletop (1901)
- He married Dovie, an American-born Southern woman, and raised Dale Sr. as a Texas American
- Hunter's UK passport derives from this English great-grandfather's British citizenship passing through Dale Sr. and Dale Jr.

### What Is UNVERIFIED
- The English immigrant's name, specific birthplace, and year of immigration
- Dovie's maiden name and the exact date/place of her marriage to the English immigrant
- Any direct DNA or documentary connection between Hunter's Spence line and Clan Spens of Fife
- Whether the immigrant arrived with family members or was a solo immigrant
