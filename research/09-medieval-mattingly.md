## Deepest Verifiable Ancestor: John Mattingly (~1560, d. 1641)

**Full name:** John Mattingly
**Born:** approximately 1560 (birthplace uncertain — Berkshire or Hampshire per family tradition, unverified)
**Buried:** December 17, 1641, St. Mary Colechurch, London
**Spouse:** Mary Winch (claimed in MyHeritage-derived data; no primary document found)
**Son:** Charles Mattingly (~1600)

**What the primary source actually says:** The London Church of England burial register for St. Mary Colechurch, 1641, page 38, contains the entry: "John Mattingly was buried the 17 of December 1641 in the church." That is the entire primary record. The original is at London Metropolitan Archives. WikiTree profiles Mattingly-368 and Mattingly-60 connect this person to the Mattingly immigrant line via aggregated genealogical data — no second primary document independently confirms John as father of Charles.

**Confidence: PROBABLE.** The burial record is genuine. The family connection relies on secondary aggregation.

## The 300-Year Gap: 1249 to 1641

The most significant finding of this research is the confirmation of a 300-year documentary void. The BHO Victoria County History of Hampshire (vol. 4, pp. 44-51) — a scholarly publication based directly on primary archives — states explicitly:

> "Stephen was succeeded by his son Peter de Mattingley, who sold all his property to Geoffrey de Arundel."

After that sale (confirmed in the Feet of Fines for Hampshire, Michaelmas 21 Henry III, approximately 1236-1249), **no individual bearing the Mattingley surname appears in any published primary source for the village of Mattingley until John Mattingly (d. 1641).** The surname disappears from the manorial record for approximately 390 years.

The village continued to be inhabited. Other families are documented: the **Rycher family** (seated at Mattingley by the mid-14th century, per Feet of Fines, Hillary 14 Edward III, ~1340). But no Mattingley-surname person appears.

This is not a failure of research — it is the actual historical record. After the de Mattingley lords sold their property and the manorial connection severed, the surname-bearing line likely continued as tenant farmers or moved to another area, generating no land transaction or court appearance for centuries. Their reappearance is only confirmed when John Mattingly (~1560) surfaces via his 1641 London burial record.

## The Medieval Confirmed Line (Not Direct Ancestry — Village Surname Origin)

These individuals are CONFIRMED in primary sources as real historical persons with the Mattingley surname. They lived at the village. They are not proven direct ancestors of John Mattingly (~1560). They establish that the surname originated at Mattingley village and was being used by at least 1206.

### Ellis (fl. 1167)
Lord of Mattingley. No surname given — identified only as "Ellis, the owner in 1167."
**Source:** Pipe Roll 13 Henry II (Pipe Roll Society, xi, 189) — cited at https://www.british-history.ac.uk/vch/hants/vol4/pp44-51

### Revelendus of Mattingley (fl. late 12th century)
Lord of Mattingley, succeeded Ellis. Wife: Rose. Three sons: James de Oakley, Bartholomew de Oakley, and Stephen de Mattingley.
**Source:** Cotton MS Cleopatra C. vii, fol. 134d (British Library) — charter of Merton Priory. Cited at https://www.british-history.ac.uk/vch/hants/vol4/pp44-51

### Stephen de Mattingley (fl. 1206) — EARLIEST DATED SURNAME BEARER
Son of Revelendus. In 1206 granted to Walter Prior of Merton and his successors in free alms: 6 virgates, 30 acres of land, several meadows, 6s. 3d. rent, and a mill in Mattingley. Also received Bartholomew de Oakley's share of the inheritance.

Recorded in the Curia Regis Rolls as "Stephen de Madingel', de Matingel'" — the earliest dated individual with the Mattingley surname per the Dictionary of English Surnames (Reaney & Wilson, Routledge 1991).

**Primary sources:**
- Feet of Fines, Hampshire, Trinity 8 John (1206) — TNA CP 25/1
- Curia Regis Rolls (cited in Reaney & Wilson 1991 via https://www.houseofnames.com/matingley-family-crest)
- BHO VCH Hampshire vol. 4: https://www.british-history.ac.uk/vch/hants/vol4/pp44-51

**Confidence: CONFIRMED**

### Peter de Mattingley (fl. 1220s-1249)
Son of Stephen. Sold all his property to Geoffrey de Arundel. Listed as "Peter Mattingeley" in Hampshire, 1249. After this sale the surname disappears from the Mattingley manorial record.

**Primary sources:**
- Cotton MS Cleopatra C. vii, fol. 134d
- Feet of Fines, Hampshire, Michaelmas 21 Henry III (1236)
- Reaney & Wilson Dictionary of English Surnames, 1991 (via https://www.houseofnames.com/matingley-family-crest — "Peter Mattingeley 1249 in Hampshire")

**Confidence: CONFIRMED**

## Domesday Entry: Mattingley 1086

The village appears in the Domesday Book as **Matingeleghe**:

- Pre-Conquest holder: **Alric** — held Mattingley of King Edward the Confessor as an alod (free, not feudal)
- 1086 holder: **Alsi the son of Brixi** (Alfsi son of Brictsi), holding under **Hugh de Port**
- 8 villagers, 3 smallholders, 3 ploughlands, 3 men's plough teams, 4 acres of meadow, 1 mill worth 5 shillings

**Alric** was an Anglo-Saxon thane or free farmer. He is a village-level figure — not a proven genealogical ancestor of any later Mattingly family. His presence establishes the pre-Conquest Anglo-Saxon community whose descendants named the clearing.

**Source:** V.C.H. Hants, i, 505a (referenced as footnote 78 in https://www.british-history.ac.uk/vch/hants/vol4/pp44-51)

## What the 1969 Internet Archive Book Contains

The book "The Descendants of Henry Mattingly" (1969, Internet Archive: https://archive.org/details/thedescendantsofhenrymattingly) covers Henry Mattingly (c.1750-1823) and his Ohio/Indiana/Illinois/Maryland descendants only. Its Appendix I states: "In England there is a town called Mattingly, the origin of which goes back into the centuries of long ago; therefore we conclude that the name is Anglo-Saxon." That is the full extent of the English-origins content. No named pre-immigration ancestor is provided. The book explicitly notes it did not research the colonial or pre-Revolutionary period.

## Unworked Archival Leads

These avenues have NOT been searched in open-access sources. They are the realistic paths to pushing the line one or two generations beyond John (~1560):

1. **Hampshire Record Office, Winchester** — Heckfield/Mattingley parish registers beginning 1538. Search for Mattingly baptisms 1538-1580. A child baptised 1558-1575 would imply a parent born ~1520-1540. Reference: https://www.hants.gov.uk/librariesandarchives/archives

2. **TNA E 179 series (lay subsidy rolls), Hampshire, Holdshott hundred, 1524-25** — Tudor lay subsidy would list household heads by name. A Mattingly householder in Holdshott hundred at this date would push the confirmed line back to the generation of John's (~1560) father.

3. **TNA E 376/E 377 (recusant rolls), Hampshire, 1559+** — The Mattinglys were Catholic. Catholic recusants were fined and listed from 1559. A Hampshire Mattingly recusant entry in the 1560s-1590s would be a significant find. Reference: https://discovery.nationalarchives.gov.uk/

4. **TNA PCC wills (PROB 11 series)** — Search Prerogative Court of Canterbury for any Mattingly will from Hampshire, 1530-1620.

5. **Herman R. Mattingly, "The Mattingly Family in Early America" (1975)** — FamilySearch catalog entry: https://www.familysearch.org/en/search/catalog/570053 — This is the probable source of the "LDS 1525 ancestor" claim. Requires interlibrary loan or FamilySearch microfilm access.

## Honest Assessment

**How far back can we go with confidence?**

With primary-source backing: **John Mattingly, buried December 17, 1641, St. Mary Colechurch, London.** He was born approximately 1560. One burial register entry anchors this person. Everything before him — his parents, birthplace, and even his connection to the village of Mattingley in Hampshire — rests on aggregated genealogy data without primary corroboration.

With confirmed primary sources but unproven genealogical connection: **Stephen de Mattingley (fl. 1206)** and his father Revelendus. These individuals are documented in the Feet of Fines, Curia Regis Rolls, and Cotton MS Cleopatra C. vii — genuine medieval primary sources. But the 300-year gap between Peter de Mattingley (~1249) and John Mattingly (~1560/1641) has no named individual bridging it in any published source. The connection between the medieval manorial family and the 17th-century immigrant family is plausible (same village name, same county, surname continuity) but genealogically unproven.

Many genealogists have attempted to push Mattingly lines into the medieval gentry. All attempts examined in this research were uncited fabrications asserting gentry descent across the documented 300-year gap. Better to stop at John Mattingly (~1560) with honest confidence than fabricate ancestors to pad the tree.

The realistic next step is a direct search of the Hampshire Record Office Heckfield/Mattingley registers (1538-1580) and the TNA E 179 lay subsidy for Holdshott hundred (1524-25). These are in-person or institutional-access archives. No professional genealogist has published findings from these specific records in any open-access source found in this research.
